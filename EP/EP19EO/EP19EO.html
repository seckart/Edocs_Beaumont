EP19EO

 

Culture of Safety EP19EO

 

Nurses are involved in the facility- or system-wide approach focused on proactive risk assessment and error management.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of an improvement in patient safety that resulted from nurses' involvement in facility- or system-wide proactive risk assessment or error management. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.

 

 

Example One: Ensuring Patients Receive Influenza Vaccination Prior to Discharge

 

Identifying patients who require an influenza vaccination has always been a challenge at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP), where patients were frequently discharged without receiving the immunization. The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) and The Joint Commission (TJC) have identified influenza vaccination as a core measure of performance that must be completed before discharge for all patients who qualify. BGP’s target for compliance was 99% for the 2014-2015 flu season, increasing to 100% for the 2015-2016 flu season. 

 

Risk Assessment
During the 2014-2015 flu season, clinical nurses were notified that patients who possibly qualified for influenza vaccination had to have screening completed through the nursing dashboard located on all computers in the nursing areas. An icon would appear if the patient required screening.  

 

One issue that clinical nurses identified was that they did not always remember to review the dashboard multiple times during the day. The nurses would review the dashboard during morning and evening report, but the icon could appear at any time during the patient’s hospital stay. For example, a patient admitted for fever has to be afebrile for 24 hours before the immunization can be administered. A patient could reach that 24-hour mark at 10 a.m., and the physician could come into the hospital by noon with the patient being discharged by 2 p.m. If the clinical nurses had not reviewed the dashboard multiple times during the day, the immunization in this case would be missed. 

 

Missing the immunization puts the patient at risk for contracting the flu. The failure to administer the immunization also puts BGP at risk of not achieving a hospital core measure.

 

Once the screening was completed and the patient qualified for the immunization, a best practice alert (BPA) would fire indicating that the patient required the vaccination. The clinical nurse was then responsible for giving the vaccination prior to discharge. This process led to many errors in the administration of the immunization: The patient might leave before the nurse could give the immunization, the vaccine might not be readily available or the nurse might simply err by forgetting to give the immunization. As the process for administering the immunization was not structured, a correction needed to be developed before the next flu season. At the end of the flu season in March 2015, BGP’s compliance rate was 96.9%, below the target rate of 99%.

 

Goal Statement
To increase the percentage of patients receiving influenza vaccination before discharge from Beaumont Grosse Pointe.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
April–June 2015
In preparation for the upcoming flu season, the oneChart Nursing Review Committee (oCNRC) met to identify challenges with the flu vaccination administration process and identify strategies to make improvements. The oCNRC consists of clinical nurses, nurse leaders, clinical nurse specialists and IT staff who work to optimize the electronic medical record. The committee determined that procedures and processes should be changed to ensure that all patients would have the opportunity to receive an influenza vaccination at BGP. Their discussion focused on whether the computer system assists or hinders the screening process. The committee decided that since the benchmark was increasing to 100% screening, the hospital would have to be proactive to ensure that the clinical nurses had the tools to perform the screening.

 

July–September 2015
During third quarter 2015, Beaumont Health Information Technology (IT), with the assistance of the oCNRC, discussed multiple ways of developing optimizations to simplify the influenza screening process for the 2015-2016 flu season and improve the rate of immunizations.

 

A new process was developed and then approved by the oCNRC, and an IT Communication regarding changes to influenza screening and BPAs was published on September 10, 2015. Effective September 22, 2015, Epic oneChart, BGP’s electronic health record, began looking at all admitted patients who are older than 6 months and have not had a fever in the previous 24 hours, and then checking for any previous documented influenza vaccination for the current flu season. If the system determines that the patient is eligible for a vaccination, a BPA will fire. This process was different from the previous year, as the BPA now fires automatically if the qualifications are met, rather than the clinical nurse having to complete the screening before the BPA fires. Once the BPA fires, the clinical nurse has the option to pick one of the following orders:

  • Give the Influenza Vaccination
  • Influenza vaccination: Refused
  • Influenza Vaccination Contraindication: Bone Marrow Transplant within last six months
  • Influenza Vaccination Contraindication: History of Guillian-Barre Syndrome
  • Influenza Vaccination Contraindication: Allergy to Eggs
  • Influenza Vaccination Contraindication: Allergy to Latex
  • Influenza Vaccination Contraindication: Allergic Reaction to Influenza Vaccination

 

The new immunization process was addressed at the September 21, 2015 Model of Care (MOC) meeting, which all nurse leaders attend to discuss hospital issues. Pam Lively, BSN, RN, Assistant Manager of 2 south, expressed concerns about the new process, particular whether the clinical nurses would remember to give the immunization before the patient is discharged. The group decided to invite Tom Sullivan, RPh, Director of Pharmacy, to the next MOC meeting to address this concern.

 

Sullivan attended the September 28, 2015 MOC meeting to discuss this with the nurse leaders. The group decided that to prevent the patient from being discharged before the immunization is administered, the pharmacy would automatically schedule influenza vaccinations for 0900 on the next day after receiving an order.

 

October–November 2015
In October 2015, work continued at both the corporate and hospital levels. The IT department continued to build and test the new process, and education on the rollout of the new process was being planned.

 

On November 2, 2015, Kathy Grady, BSN, RN, Computer Specialist from the IT department, reviewed the changes with the Professional Nurse Council.

 

On November 11, 2015, an It Communication was published to inform the clinical nurses of the process to follow when a patient or family refuses the vaccination. On November 23, 2015, a weekly flyer was published outlining the process for documenting refusal of immunization. Weekly flyers are communication tools used by nursing leadership to inform the staff of current issues. These are discussed in the daily huddles for the week. With the new process, it was not sufficient to say the immunization was not given; a new order would have to be put into the computer, a new BPA would fire and the clinical nurse would select “patient refusal.”

 

All interventions for the 2015- 2016 influenza season were fully implemented by the end of November 2015.

Outcomes

As a result of the nurses’ involvement in BGP’s error management and risk assessment processes,with multiple interventions being implemented, the percentage of patients receiving the influenza vaccination increased from 96.9% in March 2015 to 100% who qualify in March 2016.
