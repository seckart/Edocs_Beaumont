EP18EO

 

Culture of Safety EP18EO

 

Workplace safety for nurses is evaluated and improved.

  • Provide two examples, with supporting evidence, of improved workplace safety for nurses resulting from the safety strategy of the organization. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.

 

 

Example One: Ekahau Safety System, a Personal Protective Device for Clinical Nurse Safety

 

Background/Problem
Workplace safety is always a concern for clinical nurses. Acts of violence in the workplace, including verbal abuse, shouting, spitting, biting, hitting and even homicide, have become increasingly common throughout the healthcare industry.

 

Hospitals and other healthcare facilities must serve a diverse population in a timely and thorough manner. Stressors on patients such as poor health, loss of income, loss of independence and a new environment can negatively affect their relationship with healthcare providers. Episodes of violence in the hospital setting encompass not only threats to employees, but can involve escalating tensions within a family when making critical decisions for loved ones. Clinical nurses are usually at the greatest risk because of their close contact and frequent interactions with patients and their family members.

 

Workplace safety and security is a priority at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP), with the Security Department responsible for implementing strategies to ensure employees’ safety. The safety strategies currently in place at BGP include security at all entrances of the hospital, the hospital doors being locked after 9 p.m. every night and security rounds on the units throughout the day and night. Employees are required to wear their identification badges at all time, and two hospital units, the Family Birth Center (FBC) and Women’s Health Care Unit (WHC), are locked down.   

 

Prior to 2014, the standard protocol for the clinical nurse when encountering a threatening situation was to call a Code Strong. This was initiated by dialing 55 on any phone in BGP and relaying to the operator a Code Strong along with the location. The operator, who was located on the BGP Royal Oak campus, would announce Code Strong and the location on the overhead paging system at the BGP campus. Security officers would then report to the location and assess the situation. There was also an expectation that any available men working in the area would respond as a precautionary measure. However, there were many issues with this procedure:

  • There was a delay in sending the message to the off-site operator at Royal Oak and having it relayed to BGP.
  • The exact location of the incident could be vague or not communicated clearly because of stress and/or the need to get the message relayed quickly. This sometimes resulted in Security and staff members showing up at the wrong location and then having to find the correct one.
  • Having too many people respond could actually aggravate a situation rather than resolve it.

 

The afternoon of March 30, 2014, provided an example of a failure to quickly and efficiently ensure workplace safety. Erin Savinov, BSN, RN, 3 West clinical nurse, was assigned as the primary care nurse for a patient in room 358A. 3 West is a 49-bed medical/surgical unit that sees medical patients as well as all GI and general surgeries.  The unit is fairly large and is divided into two separate areas by a hallway. Savinov was providing care for a patient who became angry and frustrated and removed a pair of scissors from her uniform pocket. Savinov made several verbal attempts to reassure the patient and requested the return of her scissors. 

 

Jodi Howell, BSN, RN, 3 West clinical nurse, overheard the discussion as she was walking by the room and stopped to assist Savinov. The patient, still in control of the scissors, cut her IV line and NG tube. Attempts by Savinov and Howell to verbally calm the patient were unsuccessful, and Howell called the charge nurse, Laureen LoPiccolo, RN, 3 West clinical nurse for assistance. The patient attempted to close the door to Room 358, with Howell stepping in the doorway to prevent this. LoPiccolo arrived at the scene and attempted to push the door open while the patient was exiting, and the patient stabbed LoPiccolo’s right forearm with the scissors. At this point LoPiccolo called a Code Strong, and Security officers David Kelly, Dennis Burnett and Al Johnson arrived at the unit.

 

The patient began to run, knocking over a mobile computer and running to the stairwell hallway across from Room 362. Paul Tocco, BSN, RN, clinical nurse, Critical Care Unit, responded to the overhead page. After attempts to verbally de-escalate the situation were unsuccessful, the patient was overpowered by Security in the hallway and handcuffed. The patient was escorted to an empty private room on 3 West, and the patient’s husband was called to alert him to the situation and room change. Michael Pieh, MD, House Officer, was notified and arrived to assess the patient with subsequent orders.

 

LoPiccolo was treated in the BGP Emergency Room. Patricia Matway, BSN, RN, Administrative Supervisor, was notified, along with 3 West Assistant Nurse Managers Patricia Ackley, BSN, RN and Molly Brieden, RN, and Administrator on Call Christine Stesney-Ridenour, MBA.

 

The patient continued to receive care on 3 West until she was transferred to 3SE on March 31, 2014, where she stayed until her discharge. 3SE is a 44-bed medical/surgical unit that cares for two different patient populations, with medical patients in one area and surgical joint and spine patients in another. This unit typically has a short length of stay because of its surgical patient population. Security searched this patient’s belongings, and all valuables and sharp items were sent home with her husband.  Security was required to round frequently that evening, and a Potential for Violence alert was placed in her chart.

 

There was one clinical nurse injury caused by a patient in first quarter 2014, which equates to 0.38 clinical nurse injuries per 100 clinical nurses. BGP’s commitment to providing a safe environment for clinical nurses led to action being taken to prevent further injuries to clinical nurses. 

 

Goal Statement
To decrease the rate of clinical nurse injuries resulting from patients’ acts of violence at BGP, defined as the number of clinical nurse injuries per 100 clinical nurses.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities:
The safety of clinical nurses and all staff members is a high priority of Hospital Administration. Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, was immediately informed of the injury to LoPiccolo. Stewart and members of Hospital Administration requested that a team be formed to investigate the situation and determine ways to ensure the safety of all employees.

 

April 1, 2014 and April 17, 2014
Stewart arranged for debriefing and action planning sessions with clinical nurses, nursing leadership and key hospital leaders. The goal of the meeting was to identify solutions to prevent clinical nurses or other staff members from being harmed by patients. Stewart knew it was important to hear about the incident from the staff members involved. The sessions were attended by Christine Stesney-Ridenour, MBA, Vice President and Chief Operating Officer, and Robert Kilpatrick, BSRS, RTR, Administrative Director, from Hospital Administration; nursing leaders Ackley and Biden; clinical nurses LoPiccolo, Howell, Savinov and Tocco; and members of the Security Department.

 

The purpose of the meetings was to hear what happened from the clinical nurses’ perspective, solicit input into what went right and what went wrong, and generate ideas to prevent future incidents.

 

During the meetings on April 1 and 17, attendees discussed a safety system to ensure the safety of the nursing

staff. Chris Hengstebeck, Director of Security, reviewed the Ekahau Safety System, which Beaumont Royal Oak and Beaumont Troy already had in place. 

 

Nursing leadership developed a three-part Safety Plan based on the discussion from the meetings:

  • Purchase the Ekahau Safety System
  • Provide S.A.F.E. classes, which are non-violent crisis interventiontraining, for clinical nurses and nursing care assistants (NCA)
  • Institute a new phone number for Security

 

May 2014
A new Security phone number was activated to ensure that assistance from Security would arrive to clinical nurses in a timely manner. It was changed to an in-house number, 3-911, that would be easy for employees to remember in an emergency.

 

June 2014
Although the S.A.F.E. classes had been available through Beaumont University prior to this incident, they were not well attended. Nursing leadership determined that all clinical nurses and NCAs should attend the S.A.F.E. training, so it was made available and nursing leaders strongly encouraged all clinical nurses to attend.The S.A.F.E program trains workers on verbal intervention techniques to de-escalate potentially violent situations, along with basic physical self-defense techniques. 

 

Security continues to play a vital role in workplace safety, as it is responsible for teaching the initial eight-hour S.A.F.E. class and the two-hour refresher class. Participants are provided education and engage in role play about handling dangerous situations such as a patient or family member yelling, pacing, acting agitated or pulling a worker’s hair, and they learn how to get out of a choke hold and various arm holds.

 

July 2014
Because the seriousness of the event, Stewart and hospital leadership supported the purchase and implementation of the Ekahau Safety System for BGP. Although this was a non-budgeted item, the safety of the staff was the most important concern of Hospital Administration. All bedside staff members, including clinical nurses, NCAs, nursing leaders and Respiratory Therapy staff, are required to wear Ekahau badges, which are small GPS devices that track the wearer wherever they travel in BGP. The badge is activated by simply pulling it against the lanyard, which will alert Security and other unit staff. Security and co-workers can then respond quickly to the exact location, and the clinical assistant can identify the exact location through a desktop computer program. The clinical assistants function in a dual role, with most of their day spent at the desk functioning as a unit secretary, although they are also trained to perform certain nursing care assistant tasks. Clinical nurses are encouraged to pull the Ekahau badge whenever they feel threatened.

 

August-September 2014
The Ekahau Safety System was installed at BGP.

 

October 2014
Education on the Ekahau Safety System began. Classes were offered at various days and times during the month, with the following topics discussed:

  • What is Ekahau?
  • Who wears the badges?
  • Where will Ekahau be used?
  • When the badges are in use?
  • When the badges are not in use?
  • Why are we doing this?

 

All safety strategies to ensure a safer work environment for nurses and all employees were fully implemented by the end of fourth quarter 2014.

Example Two: Providing Clinical Nurses Education to Decrease Needlestick Injuries in Inpatient Nursing

 

Background/Problem
Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) supports and promotes a safe environment for clinical nurses working on the patient care units and throughout the hospital. One issue that the hospital monitors frequently is the number of injuries incurred by clinical nurses. The Occupational Health Department reviews the reports and sends them to the hospital for follow up. These injuries include needlesticks, strains and sprains, blood and body fluid splashes, and falls. As clinical nurses can sustain needlestick injuries in many different ways, BGP promotes a safe environment and provides a number of measures to prevent them.

 

In January 2016, Rick Swaine, MBA, MSF, CPA, Hospital President, distributed a report during an administrative team meeting outlining the number and cost of employee injuries at BGP. The cost of injuries was on the rise, and the project to address this was assigned to Robert Kilpatrick, BSRS, RTR, Administrative Director. 

 

Kilpatrick convened a meeting with representatives from inpatient nursing, Surgical Services and the Emergency Center. The group’s purpose was to discuss staff injuries and develop a plan to decrease their number. Its first meeting, on January 27, 2016, was attended by Kilpatrick; Sandy Rizzo, MSN, RN, CEN Clinical Nurse Specialist, Emergency Center; Ruth Pinto, BSN, RN, Education Specialist, Surgical Services; and Randy Whitney, MSA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Director of Professional Practice. The group reviewed the employee injury data and quickly identified the three injuries that were most prevalent at BGP: puncture wounds, strains and sprains, and exposure to body fluid.  

 

A review of the data demonstrated that most of the puncture wounds were caused by needlesticks on the inpatient units. Rizzo suggested that this data was older and that these injuries might be isolated to one area. She therefore recommended that the group review additional data to determine whether there was a trend that needed to be addressed.

 

The group continued to monitor the injuries to nurses, with data showing 0.76 needlestick injuries per 100 clinical nurses in January 2016 and an increase to 1.15 per 100 clinical nurses in February 2016. It was clear that this was a safety issue for nurses and that workplace safety improvements were needed to decrease the rate of needlestick injuries to clinical nurses.

 

Goal Statement:
To decrease the rate of needlestick injuries to clinical nurses, as measured by the number of needlestick injuries per 100 clinical nurses.  

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
March 2016
Whitney met with nursing education staff to discuss the number of needlestick injuries at BGP, and Deb Briske, MSN, RN, CEN, Clinical Nurse Specialist, asked whether BGP had up-to-date products that prevent puncture injuries. Briske said there are products on the market to prevent puncture injuries and that an inventory should be taken to determine whether BGP had all the available products.

 

Whitney spoke with Cindy Harrison, Manager of Inventory Control, to inquire about safety needle products. Harrison, who is responsible for the inventory control at BGP, assured him that BGP had all the available products for preventing puncture injuries.

 

Angela Falzarano, MSN, RN-BC, Clinical Nurse Specialist, had said during the discussion that Beaumont Troy had an issue with puncture injuries a couple of years previously. The Education Department had therefore developed an education program for clinical nurses, which decreased the number of needlestick injuries at Beaumont Troy. Falzarano suggested that Whitney contact Andrea Carr, DNP, MSN, MSA, RN, CCRN, Director of Education at Beaumont Troy, to ask whether BGP could use the program that Carr had developed for Beaumont Troy.

 

April 2016
Whitney reviewed the comprehensive Sharps Safety education module that Beaumont Troy had developedin January 2015 and asked Carr for permission to use it.  Permission was granted, and it was decided that BGP would provide education sessions for clinical nurses in June. 

 

May 2016
Once Carr’s approval was obtained, Briske and Falzarano modified the program and developed the education for the June sessions. Briske and Falzarano also taught the education sessions. 

 

June 2016
Eight education sessions were provided:

  • Thursday June 9, 2016, 1230 to 1530
  • Friday June 17, 2016, 1030 to 1330
  • Wednesday June 22, 2016 , 0930 to 1230
  • Thursday June 23, 2016, 1230 to 1530
  • Thursday June 23, 2016, 1545 to 1845
  • Friday June 24, 2016, 0900 to 1200
  • Tuesday June 28, 2016, 1330 to 1630
  • Wednesday June 29, 2016, 1100 to 1400

 

The topics addressed were:

  • Sharps Safety
    • Be prepared
    • Be aware
    • Dispose with care
  • Be Prepared
    • Organize work area
    • Work in well-lit areas
    • Never recap a needle
    • Assess for any hazards
    • Get help if needed
  • Be Aware
    • Keep sharps in view
    • Be aware of others around you
    • Stop if you feel rushed or distracted
    • Watch for sharps in linen, beds, and the floor and waste containers
    • Focus…Focus…Focus
  • Dispose with Care
    • Be responsible for the needle
    • Activate safety feature after use
    • Dispose of sharp
    • Do not overfill sharp containers

 

The education was well received, as evidenced by the evaluations that were completed afterward by the nurses in attendance. A total of 263 clinical nurses attended the nursing education sessions on sharps safety.

Outcomes
As a result of BGP’s safety strategies, the Needlestick Injury Reduction Committee implemented interventions from March 2016 to June 2016 to improve workplace safety for nurses. This led to the clinical nurse needlestick injury rate decreasing from 1.15 injuries per 100 clinical nurse in February 2016 to 0.00 in July 2016, 0.74 in August 2016 and 0.00 in September 2016.

 

 

Evidence EP18EO-2, Needlestick Injuries per 100 Clinical Nurses

 

The Needlestick Injury Reduction Committee was disbanded as a result of its great success, although the injury reports are still shared with its members so they can monitor for any increase in the number of needlesticks in the hospital. Kilpatrick also shares these reports with the hospital administrative team.
