EP15

 

Accountability, Competence, and Autonomy, EP15

 

Nurses at all levels engage in periodic formal performance reviews that include a self-appraisal and peer feedback process for assurance of competence and continuous professional development.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of clinical nurses using periodic formal performance review that includes a self-appraisal and peer feedback process to enhance competence or professional development.
    AND
  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of nurse leaders using periodic formal performance review that includes a self-appraisal and peer feedback process to enhance competence or professional development.

    •  

Note: The CNO and nurse educators are included in nurse leaders.

 

 

 

Beaumont Grosse Pointe Performance Review Process

 

Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) nurses at all levels are introduced to the evaluation process by their manager during the initial orientation period. This includes expectations, timing of evaluations, performance scoring and annual goal setting. Performance appraisals are completed annually, with the performance evaluation period coinciding with the calendar year and reflecting a summation of the previous 12 months of performance. The information used in the evaluation includes feedback from the employee (self), peer(s) and manager. An electronic program, Halogen®, is used to document the evaluation for registered nurses at all levels.

 

Performance evaluation (self, peer and supervisory evaluation) for nurses other than nursing leaders, including clinical nurses, Advanced Practice Nurses, Nurse Educators, Care Managers, specialty nurses and all registered nurses who are not in formal leadership positions, uses the same electronic evaluation tool, the Employee Appraisal. Nursing leaders, including the Chief Nursing Officer, Directors, Administrative Supervisors, Administrative Managers and Associate Nurse Managers, use the Leadership Appraisal. (Evidence EP15-1, Policy 281 Performance Appraisal Program)

 

Employee Non-nurse Leader and Nurse Leadership Appraisal

 

Self-evaluation for All Nurses
A self-evaluation for nurses at all levels is completed on the Halogen program using the following rating scale for each competency:

  • 1 – Fails to Meet (Never or not able to demonstrate competency)
  • 2 – Developing (Sometimes or usually demonstrates the competency)
  • 3 – Fully Effective (Consistently demonstrates the competency)
  • 4 – Commendable (Always demonstrates the competency and sometimes exceeds the expectations)
  • 5 – Role Model (Consistently exceeds the expectations)

 

During the self-evaluation process, the expectation is that nurses will reflect on the achievement of goals from the previous year’s performance evaluation and will develop personal and professional goals for the coming year. Goals are documented in the “My Performance” section of the self-evaluation tool.

 

Peer Evaluation for all Nurses
Nurses at all levels are expected to participate in providing feedback about peer performance.

 

Peer evaluation at all levels is completed on the Halogen program. The peer evaluator, using the same rating scale as the one used for the self-evaluation, rates each competency for which they have knowledge regarding their peer’s performance. Peer review is completed by one to three peers who are selected by the nurse and the nursing leader. The nurse submits names of peers to their leader, who then usually selects one from the nurse’s list and then another one or two of the leader’s choice to complete the peer review. Nurses who are selected to complete a peer review are sent an automatic email by Halogen informing them that they have a peer review to complete. The peer feedback is usually anonymous. The peer rates the nurse on a scale of one to five on the following categories:

  • Quality and Safety
    • Job Responsibilities/Productivity
    • Job Skills/Competencies
    • Reliability
  • Service
    • Beaumont Standards
    • Employee Engagement
  • People and Systems
    • Personal/Professional Development
    • Partnerships
  • Financial Performance
    • Efficient Use of Resources/Enhanced Productivity

 

Enhanced Evaluation Tools
The nurse leader can also send the evaluation tool through Halogen to other members of the healthcare team, including internal customers and direct reports of the person being evaluated. This is not required, but is available as a tool to give the nurse leader a 360 degree report of the employee. One example of internal customers being used was in the evaluation of Debra Briske, MSN RN, CEN. Briske is a Clinical Nurse Specialist (CNS) who reports directly to Randy Whitney, MSA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, but is the CNS for the medical/surgical units. Whitney sent the evaluation tool to a sample of the Administrative Managers of the medical/surgical units to solicit their input as Briske’s internal customers. (Evidence EP15-2, Briske 2016 Evaluation)

 

Evaluation Process
During the evaluation time frame, nurses at all levels meet with their immediate supervisor to discuss their performance and mutually decide on ratings for each competency. The supervisor gathers feedback from all sources, including the self-evaluation, peers, internal customers and/or direct reports if applicable, and the supervisor, to discuss with each nurse.

 

Professional Development Goals
During the evaluation process, the professional goals from the previous year are reviewed with the nurse and new goals are agreed upon and developed for the coming evaluation period. These goals are tied to the strategic goals of the organization and also reflect the personal professional development goals identified as being important to the clinical nurse or nurse leader. The Halogen program includes a section for documenting and tracking goals. The nurse and supervisor both sign off electronically on the Halogen program appraisal once a consensus is reached. The nurse is allowed to make final comments on the evaluation process, which is separate from the actual annual evaluation.

 

Example One: Clinical Nurse Evaluation – Self and Peer  

 

For the 2015 performance appraisal process, S.D., Clinical Nurse II Orthopaedic/Surgery unit, began by completing a self-evaluation in the “Employee” section of the Halogen tool. S.D. gave herself a rating of 3 (fully effective) in the job skills/competencies section of the evaluation. Peer feedback was incorporated into the 2015 employee appraisal and was shared with S.D. during the evaluation with her manager. Comments from her peer included that she had gotten more comfortable in her role as an RN and that she works very hard to give her patients great care. Based on the peer feedback and S.D.’s self-assessment, a professional development goal was set to attend a medical/surgical or orthopedic conference in 2016 to further her professional growth. (Evidence EP15-3, 2015 Employee Appraisal Including Self and Peer)

 

Professional Development
S.D. was again evaluated in 2016. A self-evaluation was completed (highlighted in yellow in Evidence EP15-4), and a peer review was completed (highlighted in pink). S.D. met her goal to further her professional growth by attending a national conference. In April 2016, she attended the National Association of Orthopaedic Nurses (NANO) Chapter Annual Conference. Peer feedback and her manager comments demonstrate that S.D. met her goal to continue to develop in her role. Peer comments included, “is a role model in nursing competencies and nursing skills; she knows how to do almost anything in caring for our patients.” (Evidence EP15-4, 2016 Employee Appraisal)

 

Example Two: Nurse Leader Evaluation

 

R.W, MSA, BSN, RN NE-BC, Director of Professional Practice and Magnet Program Director, is an experienced nurse leader who oversees multiple areas that support nursing, including education, the nursing business office and the Magnet Program.  R.W.’s 2015 performance evaluation began with a comment from a peer that he is not afraid to say what needs to be said, but he also knows when to put on the kid gloves. During the evaluation process, R.W.’s supervisor, Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, discussed how some of the Administrative Managers feel that when nothing is said, it must mean that the idea is not looked upon favorably. Based on this feedback, R.W. followed up with Marie Reinman, BSN, RN, Director of Heart and Vascular and Critical Care Nursing, to seek her feedback. Reinman shared with R.W. that the Administrative Mangers believe that when he does not give this opinion on a topic or solution being discussed by the team, it is viewed as not supporting the idea. (Evidence EP15-5, 2015 Leadership Appraisal)  

 

Professional Development
Based on feedback from the 2015 evaluation and follow up discussions with Reinman, R.W. identified a professional development goal to improve his communication style to minimize misunderstandings. In 2016, he read articles on communication styles and made a conscious effort to not remain quiet and to give his opinion on most, if not all, issues.

 

In the self-evaluation section of the 2016 Leadership Appraisal, R.W. stated that:

“Based on feedback from last year’s evaluation, I have adjusted my communication style. I try to give my opinion on everything that is being discussed, so that people do not get the impression that I do not like the idea.”

According to his 2016 evaluation, R.W. adjusted the way he communicates with the Administrative Managers. The CNO commented that he “is open, honest, direct and thoughtful in his interactions.” Peer feedback supported the effectiveness of R.W. achieving his goal: “is an active listener; asks questions and looks for solutions outside the box.” (Evidence EP15-6, 2016 Leadership Appraisal)
