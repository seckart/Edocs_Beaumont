EP12

 

Interprofessional Care EP12

 

Nurses assume leadership roles in collaborative interprofessional activities to improve the quality of care.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of a nurse-led (or nurse co-led) collaborative interprofessional quality improvement activity.

 

 

Improving the Quality of Care through Nurse-initiated Patient and Family Centered Interdisciplinary Rounds in the Critical Care Unit

 

Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) supports the inclusion of families as an integral component in the ongoing effort to provide compassionate, extraordinary care every day. The inclusion of families, as an extension of the patient, positively impacts the care of the patient and enhances the relationship between patient and caregiver. This relationship is enhanced through open communication, which keeps patients and families better informed of the plan of care and improves teamwork among an interdisciplinary group of caregivers. The overt, intentional transition to a more formalized Patient and Family Centered approach to care is consistent with the efforts of inpatient nursing at BGP to be more inclusive of families and loved ones as an essential part of that care. Patient and family centered rounds in the Critical Care Unit (CCU) are one example of this effort to provide extraordinary care at BGP.

 

Collaborative Interprofessional Activity
Prior to February 2016, patient rounds in the CCU were conducted at a stationary, central location at the nurse’s station. Rounds were conducted there to include a variety of caregivers, including the intensivist; the clinical nurse caring for the patient; Kelly Adams, MSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager (ANM); a representative from Physical Therapy, Pharmacy and Spiritual Care; Gael Rodgers, BSN, RN, Epidemiology; and Ed Neumier, RN, Palliative Care. Physicians document patient rounds in the Progress Notes of the electronic medical record (EMR). (Evidence EP12-1, Electronic Medical Record Documentation of Interdisciplinary Rounds) 

 

Rounds began at varying times between 0730 and1000, depending on the rounding physician’s preference. The early timing of rounds often frustrated nurses, who had the challenge of presenting a comprehensive view of the patient without first having an opportunity to fully assess the patient at the beginning of their shift. The rounds also required the RN leave the patient’s bedside to wait at the nurse’s station for their turn to discuss their patient’s care. The logistics of this process were frustrating to the nurse when care for the critically patient necessitated their close proximity to the bedside. In addition, rounds were conducted in an area that was not easily accessible to families and inaccessible to most patients.

 

Nurse-led Initiative
In January 2016, CCU clinical nurse Tony Fazi, RN, approached Adams with the journal article, Communication in Critical Care:  Family Rounds in the Intensive Care Unit.” After discussing this with clinical nurses Karen Johns, RN and Elizabeth Handran, RN, Adams decided to support the concept of patient and family centered rounds. Adams, with the support of the nurses, assumed responsibility for leading this interprofessional, collaborative initiative.

 

Adams presented the idea of patient and family centered rounds to the Critical Care Committee on January 18, 2016. (Evidence EP12-2, Critical Care Meeting Minutes) The multidisciplinary Critical Care Committee meets every other month to discuss issues related to the CCU. The agenda includes report from the Medical Executive Committee, service, Infection Control, sepsis data, Respiratory Therapy and any other issues that relate to the CCU.

EP12, Critical Care Committee Membership

 

With feedback given by CCU RNs, the committee discussed the need to change the format of rounds for the benefit of patient and families and the engagement of intensivists in the CCU. The committee agreed that rounds should be conducted in the patient’s room and involve the multidisciplinary team, and that a formal invitation should be extended to families. The committee also agreed that rounds should be performed at a uniform time daily and should involve moving from room to room in the CCU. The committee further discussed the most effective way to inform families of this opportunity to communicate with their loved one’s caregivers. Members’ suggestions included giving a verbal invitation to rounds, leaving a tent card on the bedside table and/or posting a sign inside the room. The members agreed on using signage and decided to defer the verbiage of the notice and format to the CCU Unit Practice Council (UPC).

 

On February 9, 2016, Adams drafted a proposed invitation to families both informing them of the purpose of rounds and inviting them to attend. Adams presented the draft invitation to the UPC and asked the members for suggestions. A final draft was agreed upon by the UPC and presented to Robert Marchese, MD, chairperson of the Critical Care Committee, for his review and consideration. Adams received Marchese’s suggestions during the meeting and presented them to the UPC members on March 8, 2016 for their final approval.

 

In late March 2016, Adams met with Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, to discuss the UPC’s suggested format for informing families of rounding. Adams decided to communicate the invitation to families using an 8 ½ X 11 inch wall cling hung prominently in each patient room. Staff members were also encouraged to educate families about rounds during admission to the CCU and to extend a daily verbal invite to families.

 

Adams led daily efforts to inform clinical nurses of the proposed change in the process of critical care rounds, using huddles in the CCU near the end of the midnight shift and the beginning of the day shift. For two weeks before the initiation of these rounds, the white board for communication in the report room was used to advertise the planned initiation of patient and family centered rounds. To prepare staff members and educate them on this change to rounds, Adams wrote messages on the white board announcing, for example, the go-live date, the expectation for the midnight shift to participate in rounds by educating families on the process and the expectation for the day shift to extend daily invites.

 

Education extended to physicians as well, as Adams engaged in many conversations with the intensivists to discuss the timing of rounds, the role of families in the daily rounding and the advantage of bringing rounds to the bedside. The clinical nurses held many discussions about the change to the format of rounds, which allowed them to anticipate issues that might be raised by physicians (e.g., excessive questions/
repeated interruptions from families) and prepare their responses.

 

Patient and family centered rounds began in the CCU in mid-April 2016. A set time of 1000 was implemented for rounds, which allows RNs to first fully assess their patients.  The RNs immediately appreciated this, but some physician participants took issue with it as they were concerned that their obvious availability would extend their time in the CCU by increasing the time needed to address family questions. Their concern was addressed, however, after weeks of rounding showed increased efficiency in addressing patient/family concerns in a timely and appropriate manner. Physicians and nurses appreciated the feedback of families, who could provide information that was not readily available in the chart, such as whether their loved one took any medicine other than those prescribed by the doctor. Families appreciated the transparency and were comforted by the obvious involvement of many people in the care of their loved one.

 

Feedback from staff and families was overwhelmingly positive. Families would make comments such as, “I wouldn’t miss it [rounds]!” or “I don’t understand everything, but it makes me feel better to listen to you [the interdisciplinary team].” The same positivity was expressed by physicians, nurses and Respiratory Therapy staff at the Critical Care Committee meeting in May 2016. (Evidence EP12-3, Critical Care Committee Meeting Minutes) All those in attendance agreed that the rounds were productive. RNs were satisfied with the open communication that families were able to witness and be involved in, and with the team approach that was apparent in the rounding discussions about patient care. Physicians were satisfied with the families’ comfort and apparent decrease in anxiety. All the team members were able to present a collaborative, unified approach to the family that represented their efforts and was warmly received.

 

A formal, written invitation was not available at the time that patient and family centered rounds were initiated, so CCU nurses verbally encouraged families daily to attend, making them feel a welcome part of the team. (The wall cling was not yet available because of the Marketing Department’s overwhelming workload related to the affiliation of eight hospitals to create Beaumont Health.)Adams also visited patients daily before the interdisciplinary rounds to inform families of the purpose of rounds, invite them to attend and reiterate their importance as part of the patient care team. Nearly 100% of families attend the rounds when they are at the bedside during the rounding time. Rarely, a loved one who is not related to the patient will decline the invitation to attend out of respect for the patient’s privacy.

 

In June 2016, the invitation to CCU rounds was made available in each patient room and in the CCU waiting room. The invitation was posted in a prominent position in each room of the CCU. (Evidence EP12-4, Wall Cling)

 

Outcome
Communication between patients/families and caregivers has improved as a result of the implementation of patient and family centered rounds in the CCU, as evidenced by the increase in Press Ganey scores from 2015 to 2016. Prior to the implementation of patient and family centered rounds in the CCU, the 2015 Press Ganey scores for communication with doctors and nurses varied each month. The July 2015 year to date score was 4% for both communication with nursing and communication with physicians.  The July 2016 year to date score for communication with nursing is 99%, and for communication with doctors it is 76%. The improved communication scores are a result of the implementation of patient and family centered rounds. (Evidence EP12-5, Press Ganey Inpatient Scorecard) These rounds will continue in the CCU in an ongoing effort to provide compassionate, extraordinary care every day.
