EP7EO

 

Care Delivery System(s) EP7EO

 

Nurses systematically evaluate professional organizations' standards of practice, incorporating them into the organization's professional practice model and care delivery system.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of an improvement resulting from a change in clinical practice due to the application of a professional organizations’ standards of nursing practice. The example provided may be at the unit, division, or organizational level. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.

 

 

Decreasing Blood Draws from Central Lines in the Critical Care Unit to Decrease Bloodstream Infection Rates

 

Patients with central lines are at an increased risk for central line-associated bloodstream infections (CLABSI), which are costly to the patient physically and to the organization financially. In August 2015, Critical Care Unit (CCU) clinical nurses Meredith Janas, RN and Mary Kuznia, RN, CCRN, and Administrative Manager Kelly Adams, MSN, RN reviewed the NDNQI data on CLABSIs and noted that the CCU was above the benchmark. The rate had been higher than expected for a CCU for the previous three quarters, ranging from 5.59 per 1,000 central line days in fourth quarter 2014 to 2.82 in second quarter 2015, compared to the NDNQI average mean of 0.94.  CCU nurses determined that the CLABSI rate was unacceptably high, so several practice changes were implemented to lower this rate in the CCU. 

 

In May 2015, the Assistant Nurse Managers and Janet Hamlin, MS Ed, RN, Administrative Manager, discussed the possibility of using extended dwell catheters as an alternative to peripherally inserted catheters and/or central venous catheters. The literature supported the use of extended dwell catheters to decrease infection rates, and the Assistant Nurse Managers supported this change. Prior to this time, extended dwell catheters had not been used at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP).

 

In May 2015, a representative from Bardbegan educating the Intravenous Team on the use of extended dwell catheters. In June 2015, education was provided to clinical nurses on the care and management of extended dwell catheters, and a policy regarding extended dwell catheters was provided.

 

The use of extended dwell catheters slightly decreased the CLABSI rate in the CCU, but it was still above the national benchmark. For third quarter 2015, the CCU’s CLABSI rate was 2.06. The CCU nurses were determined to continue their efforts to decrease this.   

 

Goal Statement
To decrease the CLABSI rate in the Critical Care Unit.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
In fourth quarter 2015, Gael Rodgers, BSN, RN, Infection Control Practitioner,    reviewed the Infusion Nurses Society (INS) Standards of Practice. Standard 9 states, “The nurse implements infection prevention measures with the goal of preventing all infusion related and vascular access device related infections.” In addition, the INS Journal of Infusion Nursing Standard 57.1 states, “Blood sampling for laboratory testing from a central venous access device (CVAD) should be considered based on an evaluation of benefits versus risks. Risks include occlusion and catheter related bloodstream infection due to increased hub manipulation.”  

 

Rodgers determined that these standards were consistent with what BGP needed to do to positively impact the incidence of CLABSIs in the CCU. The review of INS standards and the CCU’s CLABSI data, along with a continued focus on the Professional Practice Model that keeps the patient at the center of everything that is done at BGP, made it evident that a change in practice was necessary.

 

January 2016:
Angela Falzarano, MSN, RN-BC, a Clinical Nurse Specialist new to BGP, questioned why routine blood work was being drawn from central lines in the CCU. Falzarano spoke with Rodgers about the INS standard of limiting blood draws from central lines and decided to discuss the issue with Kelly Adams, MSN, RN, Administrative Manager of the CCU. Falzarano discussed with Adams her suggestion of eliminating blood draws from the central lines based on INS Standard 57.1.

 

This recommended change in nursing practice was presented to Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, who was fully supportive. Because this was a major change in practice based on the INS standards, Stewart recommended that the medical staff be included in the discussions. Stewart suggested that drawing from a central line is easier for nurses and more comfortable for patients, so she believed that the medical staff should be made aware in case patients discussed the change with their physician.

 

February 2016:
Stewart and Janet Hamlin, MA, BSN, RN, Administrative Manager for the Internal Resource Team and Nursing Business Office, presented the proposal for the change in practice at the Physician Chief Meeting. Nicholas Gilpin, DO, Medical Director Infection Control and Epidemiology, was aware of the INS standards and strongly supported the change, as did Donna Hoban, MD Physician in Chief. The plan was approved to move forward.

 

William Hanna, MD and Robert Marchese, MD, physicians with the CCU, were made aware of the changes and supported the idea.

 

March 2016:
On March 4, 2016, Stewart, Rodgers, Adams, Falzarano and Deb Schroeder, BSN, CGRN, Education Specialist, met to finalize the details of creating an education plan for clinical nurses to roll out the change in practice (no blood draw from central line initiative). They decided to provide unit-based in-services at change of shift for the first two weeks of March, with the plan of implementing this practice change on March 15, 2016.

 

In the first two weeks of March 2016, the CCU clinical nurses were provided unit-based education on a newly drafted policy that identified the practice change of nurses not using central lines for routine blood draws. The new practice for the clinical nurses was to obtain blood for laboratory testing by calling Phlebotomy to do a peripheral stick. This practice change was at first difficult for the clinical nurses to understand because it was more efficient to draw from the central line, but once they realized that this is a best practice they recognized the need. If deemed necessary, a one-time order from the physician would allow for a central line blood draw. 

On March 14, 2016, a weekly flyer outlining the new practice was distributed to the CCU staff.

On March 15, 2016, the newly introduced policy regarding blood draws from central lines went into effect. On March 15 the education was also reinforced through an educational flyer posted in the CCU.
