EP5

 

Care Delivery System(s) EP5

 

Nurses are involved in interprofessional collaborative practice within the care delivery system to ensure care coordination and continuity of care.

  • Provide two examples, with supporting evidence, of nurses' involvement in interprofessional collaborative practice that ensures care coordination and continuity of patient care.

 

 

Example 1: Nurse Navigator for Congestive Heart Failure (CHF) and Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) Population

 

Johanna Castronero, BSN, RN, Nurse Navigator (NN) holds the position that is used within Beaumont Health and Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) to provide education to patients and their families about disease process and management of CHF and COPD.

 

(Evidence EP5-1 Job Description of Nurse Navigator)

 

Interprofessional Collaborative Practice
The Nurse Navigator follows patients from admission to post discharge in an effort to continue and reinforce teaching efforts and to ensure care coordination and continuity of patient care once the patient is discharged from BGP. The Navigator also collaborates with other disciplines, including In-patient nursing, care management, physicians and home care to ensure the patient and families have their healthcare needs met at the time of discharge.  The Navigator also assists the patients and families with care choices for continued care in the out-patient setting.

 

CHF and COPD are chronic diseases that become progressively worse over time.
Heart failure is a progressive condition, beginning with predisposing factors and leading to the development and worsening of clinical illness. The need for hospitalization is an important marker for poor prognosis.

Chronic bronchitis is 1 of the 2 main types of a serious lung disease called "COPD." The other main form of COPD is called "emphysema." COPD can make it hard to breathe. Both types of COPD are often caused by smoking. Both conditions have been identified as the underlying cause for hospital admissions and readmissions.

 

Beaumont Grosse Pointe offers a disease specific Nurse Navigator to assist patients and their families to gain understanding of the disease process and management. The Navigator works to provide consistent education to the CHF and COPD patients. Education may include the following:

  • Disease management
  • Nutrition information
  • Post discharge follow-up

(Evidence EP5-2 Note from Electronic Medical Record)

 

Care Coordination and Continuity of Care
Castronero, Nurse Navigator, coordinates patient care with the clinical nurse, care managers, medical staff and home care.   She works to educate patients and their families while they are in the hospital as well as post hospitalization.

 

The CHF and COPD patients are provided with a direct phone line to the Nurse Navigator. Patients and families are able to contact the Navigator with any questions they have while in the hospital or post discharge. Castronero provides patient and family centered education based on her assessment of the patients’ needs. 

 

The education tools for the CHF patients include:

  • A CHF calendar
  • What is heart failure
  • CHF action plan/CHF checklist
  • Heart failure maintenance guidelines
  • Why should I limit sodium
  • Sodium tracker and understanding nutrition labels.

 

The education tools for the COPD patients include:

  • Lung conditions packet which includes disease process and treatments, diet, medications and follow up care.
  • COPD action plan and COPD checklist.

 

(Evidence EP5-3 Education tools for CHF and COPD Patients)

 

If the patient does not have a primary care physician, Castronero assists the patient with obtaining a physician to provide care for them once they are discharged from the hospital.  She will also assist with post discharge appointment arrangement as needed.

 

Nurse Involvement in Care
Castronero begins her day with running reports to find the CHF and COPD patients in the hospital. Once she has established which patients need to be seen, she carefully reviews each patients’ individual charts in EPIC, BGP electronic medical record.

 

Castronero will gather patient teaching information for either the CHF or COPD patient. The Navigator also completes smoking cessation teaching if needed at the time of the patient assessment.

 

Prior to seeing the patient, Castronero will meet with the clinical nurse and physician to gain information regarding the care of the patient.  She will question the nurse to find out as much information regarding the patients’ current status, and prior medical history.  She wants to know whatever the clinical nurse knows about the patient.  Castronero will then try to meet with the physician if available to discuss teaching needs of patients, any information that needs to be reinforced and discharge plans.

 

The Navigator meets with patients and their families to educate them on disease process and management. If family is not available the Navigator completes phone interviews. During the interview/assessment process the navigator is gathering information about the patient’s perception and knowledge of COPD or CHF. She identifies if there are any knowledge deficits and provides education as needed. She discusses with the patient what support systems the patient has and if additional support is needed.

 

If patients are well knowledgeable about their disease process, diet and follow up with care the patient will be informed regarding post discharge follow up calls.  If the patient needs reinforcement in their education, Castronero will meet with the patient and family for further education.  Castronero will then make contact with care management to assist in the arrangement of home health care.

 

The Navigator documents her findings of her assessment in a narrative note in EPIC. The documentation in Epic allows for all members of the care team to know what was taught and if there are any issues to be addressed.
(Evidence EP5-4 Note from Electronic Medical Record)

 

Post Discharge Plans
The Navigator coordinates with other members of the care team to formulate optimal discharge plans for patients.  

 

A post discharge call is placed to COPD and CHF patients that are agreeable. The post discharge call allows for the reassessment of education provided. It also provides an opportunity for the Navigator to reinforce teaching, to answer questions and to provide follow up support/problem solving.

 

When a COPD or CHF patient readmits to the hospital, the Navigator revisits the patient to find out why they returned to the hospital:

 

  • Was it disease related
  • Was there a knowledge deficit
  • Was there a lack of support/infrastructure
  • When knowledge deficits are found repeat teaching is completed.

 

Some examples of discharge plans that care coordination and continuity of patient care are supported by the nurse navigator are:

 

Ms. H. is a 46 year old COPD patient.  She lives alone and has a nebulizer. She had regular follow up with her primary care provider but her pulmonologist retired. Patient was assisted in finding a new pulmonologist that would provide continuity of care.

 

Ms. T is a 56 year old patient with CHF. She lives alone with no durable medical equipment. Patient had received CHF education. On a routine post discharge follow up call, the patient was complaining about chest pain. The Navigator advised patient to return to the ER due to her complaints of having chest pain. (Evidence EP5-5 COPD Physician Referral 2/7, 2/8 & 2/10/17 and CHF post discharge note 1/4/16)

 

Example 2: Progressive Rounds on 3 West to Facilitate Discharges
Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) adopted discharge planning as a readmission reduction initiative in early 2015.Discharge planning begins upon admission when patients are screened for discharge needs and both the patient and family are provided education to prepare them for their journey after discharge.

 

3 West, a 45 bed Medical-Surgical Unit with a specialty of Gastrointestinal Surgical patients, realized the need for improvements in the patient discharge process. To assist in this process a morning huddle is held at 10:00 am to facilitate smooth communication between Clinical Nurse and Care Manager (CM). Each Clinical Nurse would review their patient assignment and update the CM on the nursing care plan and the concerns of the patient and family. The CM would also relay any issues/concerns in the discharge process from the insurance or transition prospective.

 

Patricia Ackley, BSN, RN Assistant Nurse Manager usually attends for informational purposes and to support the clinical nurses and care managers to ensure a safe discharge of the patient. Each case was discussed in depth as to current status and progression to discharge. Patients would be updated by the clinical nurse on the plan of care and status of patient needs.

 

Physical Therapy (PT) plays a vital role in the assessment of the ambulatory status and recommendation of extended physical rehabilitation.  PT became a part of the morning huddle as well. It was found to be very helpful to have therapy present to be notified in real time of therapy needs so that they could be addressed quickly and not inhibit discharge.

 

Interprofessional Group
During the September 3rd, 2015 meeting of the Discharge Domain Task Force the concept of Progressive Rounds was introduced. Maria Miller, Controller, BGP and Kristin Cavender, BSIE, MBA, CSSBB Administrative Director BGP, presented the idea from Diane DiFiore, MHSA, RN, System Director, Performance Excellence.

The three goals of Progressive Rounds are:

  • Plan for discharge
  • Determine what is needed from the Physician for discharge
  • Review outstanding orders from the previous twenty-four hours.

 

(Evidence EP5-6 Agenda and Minutes from September 3, 2015 Discharge Domain Group)

 

In addition to Cavender and Miller, members of this Task Force for Progressive Rounds included:

Deborah Kline, LMSW, ACSW, Director of Care Management
Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Chief Nursing Officer
Molly Brieden, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager 3 West
Patricia Ackley, BSN, RN Assistant Nurse Manager 3 West
Mary Catherine Blaine, Director of Service Excellence
Holly Cory, RN, CCM, Care Manager
Rowena Angeles, RN Care Manager

During the September 3rd meeting Brieden mentioned that she saw a video of Progressive Rounds on Youtube and suggested that all members of the committee review the video and BGP could use that as a guide for the rounding. (Evidence EP5-7 Email regarding progressive rounding video)

 

Collaborative Practice
On September 17, 2015, it was discussed during the Discharge Domain Task Force meeting that Progressive Rounds were something BGP would like to proceed with.  A video from Beaumont Dearborn was shown regarding progressive rounds and it was shared that Beaumont Dearborn has high discharge Press Ganey scores. Holly Cory, RN, CCM, Care Manager, agreed to pilot the program on her assigned patients.  (Evidence EP5-8 September 17, 2015 Agenda and Minutes)

 

The rollout of the pilot began September 21, 2015 with Cory on her assigned 3 West patients. Cory and the clinical nurse would enter the patient’s room with a computer for reference and Cory would introduce the care team and write her name on the white board. The expectation was the clinical nurse would share the nursing Plan of Care, incorporating any pending tests or pertinent medical information necessary for progression to discharge. (Evidence EP5-9 Memo, Template and Documentation of Progressive Rounds)

 

Cory would lead the discussion with the patient and family and then conduct a short needs-assessment interview to screen for discharge readiness. Part of the screen would include the patient’s ambulatory status as it related to the patient’s physical environment. At this time, the patient was invited to communicate any concerns about their continuing care at home. Taking into account the patient’s needs and concerns, Cory and the clinical nurse would formulate an informal discharge plan and communicate this to the patient. This formulary process would be conducted daily, Monday through Friday at 10:00 am. The patient was encouraged to invite family or their support person to be present for these daily progressive discharge rounds. Having support for the patient would ensure all concerns were addressed for the continuity of care after discharge.

 

Coordination and Continuity of Care
Upon leaving the patient’s room, the care team would informally meet and delegate responsibility for the completion of discharge tasks. At this point, the pharmacy would be consulted for any possible discharge medication needs. PT would be notified of a need for an evaluation/re-evaluation and recommendation of possible patient care needs.

 

In order to ensure a smooth transition from the hospital to home and to provide seamless care, Cory would be the person responsible to ensure that the patient and family are ready for discharge.  She would be responsible for ordering equipment, beds, supplies (IV meds, tube feedings, wound vacs, walkers,) to ensure that family has all the appropriate care to the patient when they return to their home setting.   It was also the care managers’ responsibility to verify benefits for medications, rehab services and home care. She would also set up discharge transportation, referrals to Social Work for ECF placement and community resources such as Meals on Wheels. It was Corys’ responsibility to make sure that the patient and family had all the necessary equipment they would need to provide the required care in the home.

 

Nursing would be responsible for the communication with the physician regarding any verification of orders or patient/family concerns. Nursing would also be responsible for any education regarding the care of the patient for the family.  Education may include medication, equipment or signs and symptoms to monitor.  The Respiratory Department would be consulted if there were any respiratory needs post-discharge. Spiritual care would be advised of a patient request to be visited.

 

Inpatient Nursing was the impetus for the change in our discharge planning and communication process. As the planning progressed, it was apparent that this process would be most successful if every discipline was involved as a collaborative effort. In our pursuit of excellent patient care, we continue to monitor our patient’s perception of discharge preparedness through our Press Ganey Discharge Domain Scores.

 

The process was so successful that the process began with the other care manager, Angeles.  (Evidence EP5-10 October 10, 2015 Discharge Domain Meeting Minutes)

 

Outcomes

There is evidence that the new process has made a difference in 3 West discharge information Press Ganey Scores.  In 2014, the percentile ranking for the discharge domain at the end of year was 8%, in 2015 the percentile ranking at the end of the year was 9% and in 2016 the percentile ranking at the end of the year was 26%.
