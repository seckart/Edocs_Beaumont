Example One: Decreasing the Clinical Nurse Turnover Rate on the Women’s and Children’s Unit

 

In 2014, Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) had a seven-bed Pediatric Unit, 3 West, that was staffed by pediatric nurses and had a dedicated nurse leader. The Pediatric Unit was experiencing a lower than expected census and, in the latter part of 2014, its bed capacity was decreased from seven beds to four.  

 

The medical/surgical census continued to increase in the hospital, so Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, opened new beds to support the increased medical/surgical census by increasing its capacity. The census continued to be under budget on the Pediatric Unit, with most days of the week in first quarter 2015 having zero pediatric patients on the unit. As the Pediatric Unit was losing money, it was decided to close the unit on 3 West, move pediatric patients to the Women’s Health Unit on the 4th floor and convert the beds on 3 West into medical/surgical beds. 

 

The Women’s Health Unit (WHU) on the 4th floor was staffed by nurses from the Family Birth Center (FBC) and would only admit uncomplicated women’s surgeries such as hysterectomies. The WHU was underutilized, so at the end of first quarter 2015 it was decided to move the pediatric population to the 4th floor and combine it with the FBC. This new 11-bed unit, called the Women’s and Children’s (W&C) Unit, would take care of both pediatric patients and women with an uncomplicated medical/surgical diagnosis.   

 

In May 2015, Family Birth Center nurse leaders Patricia Heitz, RNC-OB, C-EFM and Janice Davis, BSN, RNC-OB, C-EFM assumed responsibility for the W&C Unit as Assistant Nurse Managers of both the Family Birth Center and the W&C Unit

 

Heitz and Davis decided that the W&C Unit would be integrated into the FBC, with staff members attending the FBC staff meetings and Unit Practice Council (UPC) meetings. (The UPC is the unit’s shared governance body, facilitated by its clinical nurses.) However, this left the W&C Unit with no individual identity.

 

The pediatric census never increased, so it was decided in September/October 2015 that the W&C Unit would no longer take pediatric patients under the age of 12. This further isolated the pediatric nurses, and nurses began to leave the unit because of this decision.

 

The W&C Unit turnover rate was 18.2% in November 2015 and 9.1% in December 2015.

 

Goal Statement
To decrease the clinical nurse turnover rate on thWomen’s and Children’s Unit.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
January 2016:
Heitz, Davis and Marisa Engel, MSN RNC-OB, C-EFM, Clinical Nurse Specialist, discussed the turnover on the W&C Unit and recognized that something needed to be done. They began discussing with W&C clinical nurses ways to retain staff members, and April Wynsma, RN clinical nurse, expressed her opinion that the W&C Unit had no individual identity as a unit. Wynsma suggested that it become its “own” unit and function independently of the Family Birth Center.

 

February 2016:
On February 8, 2016, Engel discussed the issue with Randy Whitney, MSA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Professional Practice Director, who was supportive of any efforts Engel could make to address the situation. Based on Wynsma’s earlier comments, Engel spoke with her about starting a W&C-specific UPC. Wynsma was excited about the possibility of having a UPC, believing that a separate one would help distinguish the unit as its “own” and that this would assist with clinical nurse retention.

 

March 2016:
The W&C Unit held its first UPC meeting on March 8, 2016, attended by clinical nurses Wynsma; Shirley Stier, RN, CPN; Adebukola Moshood, RN; Heitz; and Engel. Wynsma was elected to serve as chair of the UPC and represent the unit on the hospital Professional Nurse Council. The following items were addressed at the first meeting of the W&C UPC:

  • Participation in the hospital Professional Nurse Council
  • Meeting days and times
  • Membership
  • Development of a W&C Unit poster for Nurses Week
  • Having medication available on the W&C Unit instead of having to go to FBC to get floor-stocked medications
  • Communication with FBC

 

Wynsma believed that giving the W&C Unit its own identity would assist in retaining the current staff.

 

April 2016:

  • On April 6, 2016, Wynsma attended the Hospital Professional Nurse Council (PNC) and presented a brief overview of the W&C Unit. She stressed the importance of separating the FBC from the W&C Unit, whichwould now have its own clinical nurse on the PNC. 
  • On April 21, 2016, the W&C Unit held a second UPC meeting with Wynsma, Stier, Moshood and Engel present. They discussed W&C nursing practice issues such as pain documentation, care plan documentation and patient equipment.

 

May 2016:

  • The third UPC meeting was held on May 17, 2016, attended by Wynsma, Moshood, Stier, Whitney and Davis. The meeting topics included:
    • Magnet®
    • The importance of an individual unit identity
    • Turnover rates
    • The importance of retraining the staff

 

June 2016:
By June 2016, the UPC was fully operational and functioning as an independent council. Wynsma and the other clinical nurses were comfortable in their new roles as UPC members and would seek assistance from the nurse leaders as needed. The committee participants and structure were in place, and the UPC began addressing issues that affected the workplace and empowering the nurses to promote and support their unit. The issues they addressed included:

  • The staffing plan when the census goes above budget
  • Reviewing different staffing models
  • Working with the nurse leaders to identify who would stock the unit
  • Assisting with the placement of patients to improve the flow of the unit

 

Once the issues were addressed, the nurses began to take ownership of the unit. This has assisted in retaining the nurses on W&C.

Outcomes
Clinical nurses’ involvement in retention activities, specifically the development of a UPC to provide the nurses with a greater sense of identity and autonomy, improved the turnover rate on the Women’s and Children’s Unit. The clinical nurse turnover rate decreased from 18.2% in November 2015 to 0% in July 2016.
