Care Delivery System(s) EP6

 

Nurses incorporate regulatory and specialty standards/guidelines into the development and implementation of the care delivery system.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of nurses incorporating specialty standards/guidelines into the delivery of care.

 

 

Nurses Incorporating Specialty Standards/Guidelines into the Delivery of Care:  Postpartum Hemorrhage

 

AWHONN Specialty Standards and Guidelines
In 2014, the Association of Women’s Health, Obstetric, and Neonatal Nurses (AWHONN) launched the Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) Project with the goal of reducing the number of women who bleed to death during or after childbirth. The project outlined recommendations for the management of PPH, including improving clinicians’ recognition of, readiness for and response to PPH. In accordance with the AWHONN recommendations, the recognition, prevention and treatment of PPH is a priority for the Family Birth Center (FBC) at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP). 

 

Nurses Review of Specialty Standards and Guidelines
On April 14, 2015, the BGP FBC leadership team, including Patricia Heitz, RNC, Assistant Nurse Manager; Janice Davis, BSN, RNC, Assistant Nurse Manager; and Marisa Engel, MSN, RNC, Clinical Nurse Specialist, attended the Michigan Hospital Association (MHA) Keystone Obstetric Workshop with Keystone OB Champion Cheryl Gibson-Fountain, MD. The conference focused on another Michigan hospital’s successful implementation of a Postpartum Hemorrhage Program, and the improved outcomes that were achieved as a result. Conference attendees participated in an Evaluation of Blood Loss and Educational Workstation activity that involved a simulation of a postpartum hemorrhage and hands-on practice estimating and weighing samples to demonstrate that estimation is not an accurate way to measure blood loss. After attending this conference, the BGP participants collaborated with Michael Mahoney, MD, Chief of the OB Department, to implement the postpartum risk assessment into practice, (Evidence EP6-1, OB Hemorrhage Task Force, May 20, 2016)

 

Incorporating Specialty Standards into the Delivery of Care
In May 2014, the BGP FBC began participating in the Michigan Hospital Association Keystone OB Obstetric Hemorrhage collaborative. The FBC began collecting data on several areas specific to the recognition and treatment of postpartum hemorrhage, and reporting it to the MHA monthly. This process helped the FBC clinical nurses and leaders identify possible areas for improvement, including the assessment of PPH risk and active management of the third stage of labor.  

 

Historically, postpartum bleeding has been “estimated” by the physician. This practice is known to be inaccurate, leading to postpartum hemorrhage not being consistently recognized in a timely manner. AWHONN recommends that cumulative blood loss be formally measured or quantified after every birth. Formal methods such as weighing and using calibrated drapes reduce the likelihood that blood loss will be underestimated and allow for early recognition and treatment. (Evidence EP6-2, Quantification of Blood Loss: AWHONN Practice Brief Number 1 and AWHONN Postpartum Hemorrhage Project) The goal is to give clinical nurse the tools and knowledge to accurately measure the amount of blood loss so that a “quantified blood loss” (QBL) can be documented on the Delivery Summary instead of an “estimated blood loss” (EBL). It was determined that Engel would educate the FBC staff on practice changes in the management of postpartum hemorrhage based on the AWHONN guidelines.

 

In February 2015, all clinical nurses on the FBC attended the Postpartum Hemorrhage Risk Assessment and Quantification of Blood Loss presentation, given by Engel. (Evidence EP6-3, Postpartum Hemorrhage Risk Assessment and Quantification of Blood Loss Quiz and Sign-in Sheet) The clinical nurses reviewed the risk factors for PPH and the appropriate interventions to implement for high-risk patients, including physician notification and sending a type and screen in the event that a blood transfusion is warranted. The indications for the use of uterotonics (cytotec, methergine and hemabate) to prevent or treat PPH were also reviewed. Engel outlined the recognition of PPH, including the acceptable volume of blood loss for both vaginal and cesarean delivery. The presentation also included a PPH simulation with clinical scenarios and validation of clinical nurses’ skill in quantifying blood loss, allowing them to practice their assessment skills. Blood loss was simulated using red juice and jello in different volumes on blue pads or peri-pads. The clinical nurses practiced first estimating how much “blood” was on the pads, and then practiced weighing the saturated pads on a gram scale and calculating the quantified volume of “blood” on the pads.    

 

In 2015, three Beaumont Health Care Hospitals, Grosse Pointe, Royal Oak and Troy, joined forces to improve the recognition, prevention and treatment of PPH. The STORK subject matter experts from each site developed a physician order to improve the assessment and documentation of PPH risk. (The STORK subject matter experts are nurse super users for the OB part of the EPIC electronic medical record.) The Obstetrical Hemorrhage Risk Assessment order was implemented on March 27, 2015, and was included on the admission order sets to allow for PPH risk assessment on all patients admitted to FBC. (Evidence EP6-4, EPIC Order Set for Post-Partum Hemorrhage Risk Assessment) The OB Antenatal Admission, OB Admission and Surgical OB C-section Pre-Op order sets were modified to include an assessment of PPH risk factors to determine whether the patient is low or high risk for PPH, and the order for the type and screen. The implementation of the Obstetrical Hemorrhage Risk Assessment in the order set has enabled the clinical nurse to perform a formal assessment of PPH risk on admission and document it in the electronic medical record. 

 

In May 2015, a PPH cart was assembled based on the California Maternal Quality Care Collaborative (CMQCC) Checklist for Recommended Instruments for OB Hemorrhage Carts. According to the CMQCC guidelines, the physician should have rapid access to surgical instruments and tools designed to treat PPH. The supplies in the cart are used in the treatment of vaginal or cervical lacerations, uterine tamponade, and uterine or ovarian artery ligation. The cart provides all the instruments needed to treat PPH before a hysterectomy is considered. CMQCC PPH management algorithms were also posted on the cart for reference. Prior to implementing the cart, all clinical nurses attended an in-service that outlined the contents of the cart and how the items would be used. A quiz was then administered to assess the clinical nurses’ knowledge of the cart contents and their ability to follow the treatment algorithm and locate items in the cart. Education on the PPH cart is now part of the FBC nursing orientation to ensure that new clinical nurses receive the same education.

 

Improvement in Care Delivery
In June 2015, all the components of PPH management were successfully implemented. Data has continued to be collected and reported to MHA Keystone OB Obstetric Hemorrhage. (Evidence EP6-5, Keystone PPH Process and Outcome Measure) The data demonstrates great improvements in the recognition and treatment of PPH. As of February 2016, a PPH risk assessment is completed for all patients admitted to the FBC, and the appropriate preventive measures are implemented. There have also been improvements in the documentation of the active management of the third stage of labor. In September 2015, only 50% of patients had post-delivery oxytocin documented, whereas the June 2016 data shows that 86% of patients had this documentation completed.
