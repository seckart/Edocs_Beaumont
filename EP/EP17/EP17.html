EP17

 

Ethics, Privacy, Security, and Confidentiality EP17

 

Nurses use available resources to address ethical issues related to clinical practice and organizational ethical situations.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of nurses using available resources to address ethical issues related to clinical practice.
    OR
  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of nurses using available resources to address an organizational ethical issue.

 

 

Example One: 3 West Nurses Using Resources to Address Ethical Issues

 

Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) is committed to facilitating the resolution of conflicts and concerns that can arise due to the competing values and beliefs of patients, families and staff members. The clinical ethics consultation service is a mechanism in place to respectfully address a clinical ethical issue involving the care of a patient.  As part of new hire orientation, employees receive information and education on the availability of Ethics Case Consultations and how to request one. Patients and families are made aware of the availability of this service in the patient and visitor guide that is provided to each patient at the time of their admission to the hospital.

 

Requests for Consultation
A request for a clinical Ethics Case Consultation can be made by patients, family members, surrogates, nursing staff members and anyone else involved in the patient’s care. Requests can be placed by leaving a voicemail message on the BGP Ethics Hotline at 313-473-6222, or any staff member with Epic access may place an order requesting an Ethics Case Consultation into the computer. Requests left on the Ethics Hotline may remain anonymous. Any member of the care team can request an Ethics Case Consultation, and no one (including the attending or consulting physician) has the authority to prevent or rescind this request. Patients and/or their surrogates should be informed of any pending Ethics Case Consultation and asked whether they would like to participate.

 

At the conclusion of the Ethics Case Consultation meeting, the team leader or designee enters a note in the patient’s medical record indicating the Ethics Case Consultation team’s assessment of the ethical issues and recommended course of action, with ethical justification. The Ethics Case Consultation team leader contacts the attending physician with these findings. The Ethics Case Consultation team is responsible to provide clinical ethics consultation to advise the care team about ethical issues related to patient care.

 

Example of an Ethical Issue Related to Clinical Practice
In September 2015, Pat Ackley, BSN, RN, Associate Nurse Manager of 3 West, contacted Edmund Neumaier, BSN, RN, CHPN, Palliative Care Coordinator, with concerns raised by one of her clinical nurses, Erin Savinov, RN. The Palliative Care Coordinator has the role of first responder to the Ethics Hotline and ethics referrals made by individuals. The first responder’s role is to gather information and attempt to identify the underlying ethical concern. Savinov was distressed by the level of suffering she had observed the patient going through due to her extensive wounds, dialysis, recurrent bouts of sepsis and the length of her illness without signs of improvement.  Savinov further reported concern that the patient’s daughter, who was named in her advance directive as the primary decision maker, was having difficulty coping not only with her mother’s illness, but with the overall burden of decision making. Savinov believed this was evidenced by her refusal to allow for a palliative care consult and her agitation with physicians and staff members when attempts were made to discuss her mother’s poor prognosis.

 

Neumaier entered a Request for Clinical Ethics Case Consultation into Epic on September 10, 2015, with a consult reason of concerns regarding complex care and goals of care. (Evidence EP17-1, Epic Order for Case Consultation)  Neumaier, acting in the role of ethics first responder, conducted a chart review and informal discussion with those caring for the patient. These included Critical Care Unit (CCU) clinical nurses Savinov; Linda Amato, RN; Necola Table, RN; and Gail Pagels BSN, RN, CCRN; Renae Mende, BSN, RN, CWCN, Wound Care Nurse; Gwen Colorito, LMSW; Sailaja Datla, MD, Attending Physician; and Robert Marchese, MD, Pulmonologist. 

 

The patient was an 80-year-old female who had been ill for almost a year with prolonged hospitalizations followed by repeated admissions to long-term acute care hospital facilities and extended care facilities. She had been discharged home two weeks prior to this admission and started developing a small pressure ulcer, which developed into a stage II ulcer that was being cared for at home by the visiting nurse.  The patient was found to be unresponsive during her outpatient hemodialysis treatment, and EMS brought her to the Emergency Department. There she was diagnosed with sepsis, and subsequent blood cultures were positive for methicillin-resistant Staph aureus.

 

At the time of the consult request, the patient had been hospitalized 45 days with recurrent bouts of sepsis, congestive heart failure, severe protein malnutrition, uncontrolled diabetes and increasingly intolerance to hemodialysis treatments. The patient’s mental status fluctuated based on her illness, with her baseline being alert and oriented to person and place, and she routinely deferred decision making to her daughter, as indicated in her durable power of attorney for healthcare. 

 

The daughter had repeatedly reported to Datla, the patient’s attending physician, that she believed her mother would make a full recovery and that she wanted full resuscitation in the event of cardiopulmonary arrest. She was resistant to discussing any plan of care that deviated from a full recovery with her mother being discharged home from the hospital. Initial attempts to discuss alternative plans of care were met with agitation and avoidance by the daughter.

 

Palliative Care contacted the daughter to explain the purpose and value of an Ethics Case Consultation, work with her to coordinate and schedule a meeting and encourage her to invite her siblings.  The daughter, as the patient advocate, reacted positively to the presentation of the meeting as an opportunity to discuss her mother’s condition with the physicians and nurses caring for her. She was in agreement with including her siblings, and reported feeling overwhelmed with the responsibility of being the sole decision maker. 

 

The information that had been gathered was discussed with Beaumont Health Ethicist Paul J. Reitemeier, PhD, and the ethical concern was determined to be the non-beneficial treatment expectations of surrogate decision makers. Arrangements were made for a formal case consultation, at which all concerned parties were invited to be present. The meeting was set for September 17, 2015 at 9 a.m. It was attended by members of the Ethics Case Consultation team, invited members of the patient care team and the patient’s four adult children. (Evidence EP17-2, Sign-in Sheet for Ethics Case Consultation)

EP17, Ethics Case Consultation Participants

 

The meeting was led by Jeanne Lewandowski, MD, a member of the BGP Ethics Committee. All those present participated in the discussion respective to their area of specialty, and they answered the family’s questions about their mother’s condition and clinical course. The care options they discussed included the limitations of continuing life sustaining treatments and the initiation of comfort measures. Savinov and Neumaier validated the siblings’ love for their mother and their desire for her to remain with them.  Active listening and emotional support were provided. The siblings made a decision and reported together that, despite wanting their mother to remain with them, they did not want her to continue to endure medical treatments that would not result in her recovery.  The family members were able to express their concurrence with clinical recommendations for care without conflict.

 

Ethical Issue Addressed
As a result of this meeting, changes were made to the patient’s plan of care. These included no escalation in current medical intensive therapies, and resuscitation not to be attempted since it had been deemed to be no longer beneficial. These changes were implemented with the family in agreement. (Evidence EP17-3, Ethics Case Consultation Summation and Recommendations) All participants in the case consultation, including clinical nursing staff members, believed the intervention had provided a supportive and informative setting for the patient advocate and her siblings to have their concerns addressed while bringing resolution to the concerns that led to the case consultation. The Ethics Case Consultation team’s recommendations included that the family be supported and assisted in decision making regarding the compassionate extubation of the patient, followed by initiation of comfort measures with the anticipation of the patient actively dying.

 

CCU clinical nurses Amato and Angelica Cooper, RN, and Palliative Care nurse Neumaier worked closely with the patient and family over the six days following the ethics consultation to facilitate the transition to comfort care, and they supported the patient and family with life completion tasks. (Evidence EP17-4, Clinical Nursing Notes) The patient expired comfortably with her family at the bedside. The nurses involved in supporting the family through end of life believed they were able to support the family through the process and that the family exhibited improved coping and appropriate grieving following their mother’s death.
