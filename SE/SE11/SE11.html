Recognition of Nursing SE11

 

Nurses are recognized for their contributions in addressing the strategic priorities of the organization.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of recognition of a clinical nurse for his or her contribution(s) in addressing the strategic priorities of the organization.

    AND

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of recognition of a group of nurses for their contribution(s) in addressing the strategic priorities of the organization.

 

 

Example One: Clinical Nurse Barbara Sheppard Recognized for Supporting the Strategic Priority of Providing Safe, Quality Patient Care

 

Strategic Priority
“Provide safe, quality patient care” is identified in the Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) 2014-2016 Strategic Planning Process, hospital-based initiatives. The goals identified for safe and quality care are:

  • Identify safe themes, reinforce department action plans, meet with directors and support the plans.
  • Share executive safety rounds overview and write up with department staff.
  • Develop an action plan, by domain, and initiate BGP specific first steps to improve service: Clean, Quiet, Meds and Doctors.
  • Actively work the HCAHPS work plans.
    (Evidence SE11-1 2014-2016 Strategic Planning Process, page 3)

 

Issues Related to Providing Safe, Quality Patient Care
In August 2015, Barbara Sheppard, RN, C-EFM approached Patricia Heitz, RNC-OB, C-EFM, Assistant Nurse Manager of the Family Birth Center (FBC), with concerns about things she was witnessing on the unit. Sheppard is an experienced nurse with 10 years of critical care experience and seven years of experience in the FBC. She also serves as a preceptor for new nurses for the FBC.

 

Sheppard, who has seen the ebbs and flows of staffing, began her career in FBC on the midnight shift and moved to days. Her vast experience positions her to assess new clinical nurses as they proceed through orientation, including their ability to adapt to the particularities of their shift. She is able to judge and define strengths, weaknesses and how past experiences can assist the staff as a whole.

 

Sheppard raised the idea of having the more experienced nurses on the day shift work for a time on the midnight shift to mentor its nurses and strengthen the shift as a whole.
The unit, particularly on the midnight shift, had an increase both in new nurses and nurses who had no experience in the obstetric specialty. At the time that Sheppard raised this idea, the midnight shift had a very inexperienced clinical nursing staff. It did, however, have an experienced Obstetric Assistant Nurse Manager assigned to the shift, Janice Davis, BSN, RNC-OB, C-EFM. The following is a sampling of the midnight shift staff members:

SE11, Sampling of Midnight Shift Nurses

 

To add to the complexity of the staffing struggles, four nurses had active intermittent family leaves that were being used frequently, and one nurse was out on pregnancy-related complications.

 

The composition of the staff included some strong nurses, but the majority were newer nurses working in this highly stressful environment. The midnight shift nurses shared their feeling of being stressed with their counterparts on the day shift. Sheppard took their thoughts and feelings to heart and came up with an idea to bolster not only the confidence of the midnight shift, but also the specialty that is practiced a labor and delivery unit.

 

Sheppard discussed the issue with other day shift nurses and enlisted Zoe Bates, RN, C-EFM and Norine Fuerth, BSN, RN, C-EFM, who were willing to help with mentoring the midnight shift. Sheppard convinced Bates and Fuerth to move to the midnight shift to assist the new clinical nurses in providing safe, quality care.

 

Sheppard, Bates and Fuerth then discussed the idea with Heitz, who decided that she was able to allow these nurses to safely float to the midnight shift by enlisting the help of part-time nurses to fill in the day shifts. They collaboratively decided that the three nurses would work the midnight shift for three months. (Evidence SE11-2, Work Schedules for Clinical Nurses on FBC)

 

During their time on the midnight shift, their duties were to partner with the newer nurses to serve as clinical resources and to help them develop a variety of skills. The specific tasks they accomplished included:

  • Advancing two nurses up to level 2 nursery to enable them to care for ill newborns
  • Training new nurses to become scrub nurses 
  • Completing orientation for nurses on the midnight shift, serving in the preceptor role
  • Serving as resource nurses for all new night shift staff members
  • Reviewing opening the OR for STAT cesarean section procedures
  • Reviewing OB emergencies:
    • Neonatal resuscitation with infant transfer to nursery for observation
    • Shoulder dystocia
    • Postpartum hemorrhage drills
    • STAT cesarean section drills
    • D&C set-up
    • Use and review of new equipment, e.g., yellowfins

 

These three nurses were not counted in the daily staffing, as the intention was for them to serve as resources, mentors and a source of strength for the less experienced clinical nurses.

 

Heitz shared the idea with Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer. Stewart was receptive to its uniqueness and appreciated the empathy and caring that these engaged nurses showed toward the midnight shift and the less experienced clinical nurses.

 

Recognition
Sheppard was recognized in a number of ways for her ability to ensure that safe, quality care was provided on the Family Birth Center. 

  • Sheppard was recognized by being nominated for the Nightingale Award in the category of Staff Nurse Practice. The Nightingale Awards are given by Oakland University School of Nursing in Rochester, Michigan to the outstanding nurses in the Detroit Metropolitan area. (Evidence SE11-3, Nomination form for Nightingale Award for Barbara Sheppard)
  • Stewart was able to secure additional funding for Sheppard, which was paid hourly to the three nurses when they worked the midnight shift to compensate them for their willingness to go above and beyond to foster and strengthen the unit’s ability to render safe, quality care. (Evidence SE11-4, January 20, 2016 Memo Outlining Compensation) While these nurses had never asked for additional compensation for their efforts, they were needless to say happy and they appreciated that management was open to unique suggestions from the staff about how to effectively make a difference on their unit.
  • Sheppard was recognized at the Nursing Excellence Day, during Nurses Week, on May 11, 2016 for being nominated for various awards.
  • In January 2017, Sheppard was recognized at the hospital level with a Beaumont Star Award for the outstanding care she provides to her patients. These awards are given by the hospital president to employees who excel in their positions. (Evidence SE11-5, Memo from the Nurses Week Task Force and Star Award)

 

Sheppard’s co-workers often praise her for her efforts in this endeavor, which were also recognized in her performance appraisals. Her idea demonstrated her willingness to be innovative and forward thinking in the quest to strengthen the unit in both spirit and skill.
This inventive idea, along with Sheppard’s stellar bedside care and mentoring of both new and seasoned staff members, demonstrates the commitment to the BGP Strategic Priority of providing safe, quality care to our patients.

 

Example Two: Recognizing Nurse Leaders for Obtaining BSN Degrees

 

Strategic Priority
Achieving Magnet® designation was identified as a Strategic Priority in the 2014-2016 Strategic Planning Process. The goal was to begin the process in 2014, with a Magnet submission date of 2017-2018. Three goals were identified in the Strategic Planning Documents:

  • Obtain a consultant to assist in the process
  • Complete a gap analysis
  • Begin the search for, recruit and hire a Magnet Program Director.
    Evidence SE11-1, 2014-2016 Strategic Planning Process)

 

Gap Analysis
Eligibility criteria for Magnet designation include 100% of nurse managers and nurse leaders having either a baccalaureate or graduate degree in nursing at the time of application. In early 2015, a gap analysis completed at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) showed that some of its nurse leaders did not have a bachelor’s degree in nursing. The leaders identified as not having at least a BSN were:

  • Molly Brieden, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, 3 West
  • Derek Forman, RN, Administrative Manager, IP Surgery
  • Patricia Heitz, RN, C-EFM, Assistant Nurse Manager, Family Birth Center
  • Charlotta Marion, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, Emergency Center
  • Susan Muscat, RN, BS, Director of Quality Management
  • Marie Reinman, RN, Director of Heart & Vascular and Critical Care Nursing
  • Alexis Rogers, RN, ONC, Assistant Nurse Manager, Orthopedic Unit

 

Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, discussed the issue of nurse leaders’ education with Maureen Bowman, MA, BSN, RN, NEA-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer at Beaumont Royal Oak, which is a Magnet-designated hospital. Bowman said that Beaumont Royal Oak had had the same issue and was able to supplement the education benefits for nurse leaders who needed to pursue an advanced nursing degree.

 

Nurse Leaders’ Commitment to Obtain a BSN Degree
Stewart approached Rick Swaine, MBA, MSF, CPA, Hospital President, about the idea of enhanced education benefits for nurses who needed to obtain a BSN degree so BGP could achieve its strategic priority of achieving Magnet designation. Swaine agreed with Stewart, and Stewart began working with Human Resources to develop a plan similar to the one used at Beaumont Royal Oak for Enhanced Educational Assistance Benefit for nurse leaders to obtain a BSN degree.

 

In June 2015, Stewart met with nurse leaders to review and sign the Enhanced Educational Assistance Benefit Agreement. The agreement consisted of the following:

  • The nurse leader must enroll in a BSN program and graduate with a BSN degree by December 31, 2016.
  • The nurse leader needs to provide proof to Human Resources of graduation completion by December 31, 2016.
  • If the nurse leader is unable to complete the education requirement by December 31, 2016 they will be displaced from their current position.
  • The nurse leader agrees to work for BGP for 24 months after graduation.
  • If the nurse leader leaves before 24 months or does not graduate, all enhanced benefits must be paid back to BGP.

Enhanced Educational Benefit:

  • Beaumont Health System will provide additional educational reimbursement in the amount of $6,000 annually (total of $12,000) over and above the current $2,000 annual benefit to further support education.
  • In exchange for these enhanced education benefits, a 24-month work commitment is required at Beaumont Grosse Pointe.
    (Evidence SE11-6, Enhanced Education Assistance Benefit Agreement Letter)

 

The seven nurse leaders signed the agreement and enrolled in various schools to complete the education by December 31, 2016. Forman and Reinman experienced some difficulties early in the process. They had received their basic nursing education in Canada, and one university that they applied to informed them that it would not accept some of the credits and that they would have to repeat some courses. After receiving this information, Forman and Reinman both enrolled in another university.

Recognition Events
These nurse leaders were recognized individually and as a group. They were recognized in the following ways for obtaining BSN degrees in support of the BGP strategic initiative of achieving Magnet designation.

  • Brieden, Heitz, Marion and Rogers received promotions and a substantial increase in their compensation and benefit package. Their titles were changed from Assistant Nurse Manager to Administrative Manager, and they were moved from regular payroll to executive payroll. Executive payroll provides an enhanced benefit package and less out of pocket expenses. (Evidence SE11-8, Justification for Employee Salary Increase)
  • The Nurse Leaders Group was recognized at the December 2016 Monthly Leadership Meeting, which is attended by all hospital leaders to discuss topics that affect the entire hospital.
  • The Nurse Leaders Group was also recognized by Stewart at the Medical Executive Board on January 17, 2017. The Executive Board was very complimentary of the group of nurse leaders who obtained a BSN degree.

    • (Evidence SE11-9, Minutes of Medical Executive Board, January 17, 2017)

  • The Nurse Leaders Group was also recognized by the hospital at a dinner on January 17, 2017 at Andiamo Trattoria. This event was held in honor of the six nurses who earned a BSN degree. In addition to the dinner celebration, all of the nurse leaders received a small token of appreciation from the hospital. The following people joined in recognizing the six honorees:
