Example One: Transition of New Graduate Nurses into the Nurse Practice Environment – Graduate Nurse Residency Program

 

Onboarding to Beaumont Grosse Pointe
All new graduate nurses onboarding to Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) are required to attend corporate, hospital and nursing orientation and the Graduate Nurse Residency Program. The corporate orientation is a four-hour session in which all corporate information is reviewed, hospital-specific information is covered in the hospital orientation and nursing orientation is a five-day program.

 

The five-day BGP nursing orientation, completed with Beaumont Hospital Royal Oak, includes the mandatory education expectations, electronic medical record education and nursing skills that are deemed difficult, high-frequency or high-risk. (Evidence SE7-1, RN Nursing Orientation Agenda) 

 

Graduate nurses are also required to attend a one-day BGP nursing orientation, consisting of hospital-specific content that is adjusted based on the recommendation of the nursing leadership team.

 

The complexity of patient care today requires highly competent clinical nurses who use research and other forms of evidence to guide practice to ensure patient safety and quality care. The stress of the transition from student to practicing professional clinical nurse is exacerbated by the unrealistic expectation that one will quickly come to function as a competent nurse. Clinical experience in academic programs is limited, and new graduate nurses (GN) are quickly faced with the reality of making critical decisions for the first time in their careers. The Robert Wood Johnson Foundation has reported that new GNs are leaving the profession in large numbers, as high as 20% during their first year of practice and 34% during their first two years. Acute care nursing orientation programs of six to 10 weeks with a nurse preceptor end with little follow-up and support for the orientee. The impact of new graduate competency on patient safety is significant and cannot be ignored. Beaumont’s Graduate Nurse Residency Program provides a comprehensive approach by providing clinical education, competency evaluation and mentorship for GNs through their first year of practice. 

 

Based on the many benefits of graduate nurse residency programs cited in the nursing literature, Beaumont Health decided to offer a GN Residency Program beginning in 2012.This is a shared program between three hospitals in Beaumont Health: Grosse Pointe, Troy and Royal Oak.

 

Graduate Nurse Residency Program
The GN Residency Program was designed by the Clinical Nurse Specialists at Beaumont Grosse Pointe, Royal Oak and Troy. The program is currently overseen by Clinical Nurse Specialists Angela Falzarano, MSN, RN-BC, from Beaumont Grosse Pointe; Caroline Medcoff, MSN, RN, CNN, from Beaumont Royal Oak; and Phyllis Staniec, MSN, RN, CPN, from Beaumont Troy. The hospitals rotate the responsibility for each month’s session.

 

The purpose of the program is to give new GNs the support and education required to successfully transition from school to professional practice. There is a learning gap between what new GNs know when they graduate from a nursing program and the skills needed to perform proficiently in successful practice. The new GN can easily feel isolated, incompetent and frustrated. The GN Residency Program helps to counteract feelings of isolation by giving nurse residents the opportunity to bond with other new nurses and to build a support network with their peers, Clinical Nurse Specialists, preceptors and administrative managers.

 

The objectives of the GN Residency Program are to:

  • Facilitate a smooth transition to practice by bridging education and nursing practice
  • Provide ongoing support and mentoring for new GNs throughout their first year of entry into practice
  • Introduce the GNs to numerous Beaumont nursing experts and other Health System resources.

 

The GN Residency Program provides the following benefits:

  • Clinical Nurse Specialists guide educational activities in a variety of settings to allow the learners to take an active role in the learning process. 
  • The learning activities that are used promote critical thinking and problem solving. These activities include simulation, case studies and clinical narratives.
  • It facilitates a smooth transition to practice by bridging education and nursing practice.
  • New GNs are provided ongoing support and mentoring throughout their first year of entry into practice.
  • New GNs are introduced to numerous Beaumont nursing experts and other Health System resources.
  • New GNs are given the opportunity to develop their organization and prioritization skills.

(Evidence SE7-2, Information about GN Residency Program from Website)

 

GN Residency Program Commitment
Upon hire, GNs sign the Graduate Nurse Residency Agreement. (Evidence SE7-3, Commitment Letter for GN Residency) By signing this agreement, GNs agree to attend a total of 48 hours of paid classroom time over 12 months as part of the GN Residency Program. Each month, the four-hour class is offered at five different times. The topics rotate by month, as shown in the table below. The topic from May is repeated in October, and there is no class in December. GNs must attend a total of 10 classes to successfully complete the series

 

A total of 500 GNs participated in the 2016 Beaumont Health GN Residency Program: 53 from Beaumont Grosse Pointe, 189 from Beaumont Troy and 258 from Beaumont Royal Oak. The following topics are reviewed in the GN Residency Program, which is a rolling 12-month program that GNs usually start in their second month of employment.

Each month, experts from the hospital present on various topics. The Palliative Care Nurses present the Palliative Care/End of Life class, Legal Nurse Consultants present the Legal Issues class and Wound Care Nurses help with the Pressure Ulcer Prevention lectures.

 

The many learning modalities that are incorporated into the GN residency include lecture, videos, interactive games, hands-on practice, case study and team learning concepts to enhance learning opportunities.

 

Five Clinical Nurse Specialists/Education Specialists from the three hospitals help facilitate the classes each month and serve as the liaison at their respective hospital.

Evidence-based practice is one topic that is discussed at length in the GN Residency Program. This program is 3.5 hours long, with 3.5 contact hours awarded. The objectives of the program are to:

    • Describe the five steps in Melynk & Fineout-Overholt’s Model of Evidence-based practice
    • Conduct a review of the literature using an evidence-based nursing electronic database
    • Identify key points in appraising evidence
    • Develop an implementation plan using information from the workshop

 

At the end of each class, the participants complete a written evaluation to provide feedback that is used to improve the classes. The Clinical Nurse Specialist and Education Specialist group review the evaluations annually and make changes to the agenda as needed based on the feedback provided in the course evaluations.

 

Evaluating the Graduate Nurse Residency Program for Effectiveness
Nurse Managers are provided an attendance grid each month to show GNs’ progress through the program. The Administrative Nurse Managers ensure that GNs have scheduled time off the unit to attend the classes. Once the GNs complete the residency program, they receive a certificate signed by the Chief Nursing Officer.

 

The GN Residency Program is evaluated monthly at each session by the Clinical Nurse Specialists from each hospital who run the program and the GNs in attendance.  The GNs complete the Casey-Fink Graduate Nurse Experience Survey, which is administered when they have two months, six months and 12 months of experience.  The survey measures how well the GN is transitioning from the role of graduate nurse to clinical nurse. Once a year, the Directors of Education and Research and the Clinical Nurse Specialist from Beaumont Grosse Pointe, Beaumont Royal Oak and Beaumont Troy meet to review the program evaluation and the Casey-Fink data to determine the program for the following year and adjust the content based on the program evaluations. (Evidence SE7-5, 2015 Casey-Fink Graduate Nurse Experience Survey)

 

Example Two: Transition of a Newly Hired Experienced Infusion Nurse into the Work Environment of Beaumont Grosse Pointe Infusion Services

 

Onboarding to Beaumont Grosse Pointe
For nurses hired from outside Beaumont Health, orientation begins with the one-day corporate orientation. The experienced outpatient clinical nurse attends only days one and two of the combined nursing orientation program at Royal Oak, since the remaining days of that program have an inpatient emphasis that is not applicable to the outpatient practice setting. (Evidence SE7-1, RN Nursing Orientation Agenda)   

 

All mandatory Halogen education (web-based education activities) must be completed prior to working in the clinical area. All computer-based training related to the electronic medical record, Epic oneChart, must be completed before the nurse is granted access to that software. 

At the time of hire, experienced nurses are assigned a “Friend at Work” who serves as the primary preceptor when they begin practicing in the clinical area.(Evidence SE7-6, Friend at Work Memo and Program)

 

Nursing Orientation
On the first day of orientation the newly hired experienced clinical nurse is provided a folder with an orientation competency checklist. This serves as a preliminary needs assessment to identify whether the experienced nurse needs assistance or is inexperienced in any specific performance criteria. (Evidence SE7-7, Overall, Orientation Competency Checklist)

 

Each performance criterion is validated by the preceptor throughout the orientation process. New Outpatient Infusion clinical nurses meet with Josephine Garnoc, MSN, RN, CRNI, OCN, Director of Infusion Center, on the first day to determine the required length of orientation and the frequency of days available to orient. Orientation days are scheduled as consistently as possible to facilitate learning the new work environment and the transition to being integrated into the department. (Evidence SE7-8, Individual Nursing Competency Checklist)

 

The new nurse is welcomed to meet and speak with the Director at any time during the orientation process. A meeting is held with the nurse at 90 days to review progress toward completing department-specific competencies and general progress, with input from the preceptors. The length of orientation is also impacted by the nurse’s previous infusion practice, given the wide variety of medications that are administered.

 

If the new nurse does not have an Oncology Nursing Society Biotherapy and Chemotherapy Provider Care, it is expected that this will be completed within the first six months of employment. Following the completion of this online course, the newly hired experienced nurse is observed administering chemotherapy three times in accord with the established guidelines from the Oncology Nurses Society for the Clinical Practicum Evaluation.

 

Sandra Moore, RN, clinical nurse, Infusion Center was hired as a contingent clinical nurse, which is a non-benefit employee. They have no set hours and are scheduled to work at the discretion of the unit. Moore’s 33 years of experience as a nurse included inpatient oncology. Her most recent practice was working in a physician’s office for nine years doing biological infusions for the treatment of rheumatoid arthritis and bisphosphonates for osteoporosis. 

 

Moore attended corporate onboarding on her first day, October 12, 2015. The orientation plan was for Moore to be at Infusion Services from October 14-16, 2015 to set up her password, complete all the online education that is required before working in the clinical area and complete all the required computer-based training for access to the electronic medical record, which was part of nursing orientation. 

 

Garnoc started with introducing Moore to the staff and giving her a tour of the unit and building to see the Infusion Services routine. Garnoc also met with Moore to discuss the length of orientation based on her experience.

 

Moore was very skilled in IV starts and accessing implanted venous access devices, and she knew the established protocols from the office she had previously worked in, which had only minor differences that were discovered when assessing her office practice. Moore indicated that it had been many years since she had administered chemotherapy and that those medications had changed a great deal, along with all the other medications that are administered in the Ambulatory Infusion Center (AIC). Garnoc and Moore agreed that once the required mandatory education was completed, the orientation would focus on the medications she had the most experience with to learn the differences in the protocols and antibiotics.

 

Individualized Orientation Plan
Moore was paired with Ruth Knipstein, RN, clinical nurse preceptor, who was also assigned to be her “Friend at Work.” Knipstein sent Moore an email prior to her start date to welcome her. During breaks from the computer, Moore and Garnoc talked about the work schedule, requesting days off, holiday/weekend requirements and other department policies. It was determined that the department-specific competency demonstrations would be started once the online mandatory education activities were completed. 

 

It had been several years since Moore had practiced in a hospital setting, so a specific number of days for orientation was not set at the beginning. Needs evaluations were performed as she progressed in her transition process.

 

Contingent nurses are not required to engage in the orientation full-time, but Garnoc explained that the process could in this case take longer. As she was transitioning from office practice to the hospital setting, Moore agreed that consistency would reinforce the learning experience and facilitate the orientation process and integration. Garnoc and Moore together determined that she would orient two to three days a week, with the length of orientation to be determined as it progressed, with input from preceptors. 

 

Moore was very confident with the medications she had administered in the past and with delivering patient care, but she found that documentation was a challenge and was taking longer to master. All of the staff members reassured her that it had been the same for them when they began to use the electronic medical record and that it just takes time and practice. 

 

Four weeks into orientation, Moore told Garnoc that she felt she had made a mistake taking this position and that she was more of a hindrance than a help and was slowing down her preceptors. Moore said she was fine with the patient care aspect, but that she was uncomfortable and frustrated with the computer documentation and other aspects of the work flow. 

 

As consistency and practice reinforces learning, they determined together, with input from her preceptor, that Moore would work three days a week and have them as close to each other as possible. They reviewed the progress of her orientation and determined that Moore had completed the competency demonstrations, protocols, policies, corporate compliance, process of referring patients to the Infusion Center and routine work flows. Moore was willing to assist other nurses for the opportunity to learn new medications and to be part of the team.

 

Patient care was not an issue, as Moore made connections with her patients easily and practiced in accord with

the Infusion Nurses Society (INS) and Oncology Nursing Society (ONS) standards of care, and hospital policy and protocols related to patient care. 

 

They identified the greatest challenge and source of frustration as documentation on the computer, placing lab orders in Soft (BGP’s electronic specimen collection system) and navigating the EMR. Garnoc, Moore and her preceptordetermined that since she had confidence and skill in patient care, the current objective was to support Moore’s learning and acquiring basic mastery of the EMR. In December 2015, a revised orientation plan was developed for Moore to gain experience by working with the preceptor and do all the navigation and documentation on the medical record, enter lab orders and print the labels for any labs, complete the treatment summaries and send them to the physicians, and then close the encounters.

 

Three weeks into this new orientation direction, Moore reported that this approach made a big difference and that working the consecutive days allowed her to repeat the learned skills of computer documentation, ordering labs, printing labels and finding what she needed on the EMR, while also providing patient care. 

 

She indicated she still took longer than the other nurses but was no longer feeling that she had made a mistake in taking the position. She enjoys her new role, being part of the team and being able to fulfill all aspects of her position other than administering chemotherapy. According to ONS, new nurses should have at least six months of exposure to chemotherapy before completing the ONS Biotherapy and Chemotherapy Provider online course.

 

This time gives learners some familiarity with the agents and allows them to observe the process of chemotherapy calculation double checks, laboratory values, patient education and safe handling of hazardous drugs.

 

This was the next phase of orientation, since Moore completed her official orientation on January 22, 2016 and would be counted in staffing numbers on the schedule beginning January 24, 2016.  Moore completed 32 days of orientation, which amounted to 256 hours, in addition to self-directed reading at home to enhance her learning and confidence in the clinical area.

 

Evaluation of the Effectiveness of the Transition Process for Newly Hired Experienced Nurses
The transition of a newly hired experienced nurse is evaluated through her or his completion of the nursing competencies and through the employee appraisal system. The Director of the Infusion Center meets with the employee to discuss her or his progress along with the preceptor; if all the competencies are met, the clinical nurse has completed the transition.

 

The employee appraisal system is another means of evaluating the transition. Moore was given a score or 3.7 (out of 5) on her 2016 employee appraisal, which mean she is functioning in the commendable range. Both of these evaluation measures demonstrate that Moore has successfully transitioned to being a clinical nurse in the Infusion Center. (Evidence SE9-9, Moore 2016 Employee Appraisal)
