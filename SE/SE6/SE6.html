SE6

 

Teaching and Role Development SE6

 

The organization provides opportunities to improve nurses' expertise in effectively teaching a patient or family.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of an educational activity provided by the organization focused on improving nurses’ expertise in teaching a patient or family.

 

 

Education Need Identified 
Clinical nurses on 2 South are charged with the difficult task of caring for and guiding Hospice patients and their family members through the end of life process. In November 2015, Edward Neumaier, BSN, RN, CHPN, Palliative Care Coordinator, brought to the attention of Assistant Nurse Managers Kimberly Little, BSN, RN and Pam Lively, BSN, RN that new clinical nurses (Sarah Boone, BSN, RN; Jessica Safron, RN; and Kristin Suchoski RN) were having difficulty with teaching patients and their families about managing symptoms during end of life care. In addition, Donna Martin, RN, Beaumont Hospice Nurse, following discussions with family members, approached Little and Lively with her concerns about 2 South clinical nurses being able to provide the family of the dying patient with education about untoward symptoms at end of life.

 

Further investigation by Martin showed that a family was not familiar with signs of pain and how to know when their loved one was in pain or anxious. Having the family educated on this helps them feel involved with the care and provides the nurse with information so they can help keep the patient comfortable. However, family members were not familiar with the different types of breathing patterns that can be expected during end of life, such as cheyne-stokes respirations, agonal breathing and terminal congestions.

 

Family members knowing this information would decrease their fear and anxiety because they would know what to expect. They were uneasy seeing their loved one not eating or drinking, as they felt the patient was starving to death. They did not understand that their loved one’s organs were failing and that their body would not tolerate the food and fluid. Rather than reinforcing education that had already been given, Martin found herself providing family members with the initial education on what to watch for to assist the nurses with controlling symptoms for the end of life care. This showed that 2 South clinical nurses were not provided training on the management of symptoms at the end of life, which in turn made it difficult for them to educate the family on the end of life process.

 

At the October/November 2015 meeting of the Oncology Center of Excellence (COE), a multidisciplinary team, Little and Lively spoke about the need for hospice education for the 2 South clinical nurses. This education would need to include symptom management for anxiety/agitation, delirium, constipation, diarrhea, dyspnea, secretions, nausea/vomiting and pain that can occur at end of life. Sharon Solomon, MPA, BSN, RN, Director of Beaumont Hospice, stated that all their Hospice nurses went through education sessions provided by the Nurse Educator from Beaumont Home Services, Richard McKenzie, BSN, RN, and completed annual hospice education modules. These modules reviewed the management of untoward symptoms and the importance of effectively teaching patients and family members what to expect in end of life care. (Evidence SE6-1, Oncology Center of Excellence Meeting Minutes, October 15, 2015)

 

During daily leader rounding, Little noted through discussions with family members that some families did not understand the end of life process and what to expect. Little would assess the patient and ask family members if they understood why their loved one was having breathing changes, why their chest was rattling or other signs of the dying process. Little investigated further and, based on family members’ responses to questions she asked them, determined that some staff members were not effectively teaching patients and families about the end of life process. Little would then provide the family with education on what to expect with the changes in breathing and other symptoms.

 

One factor affecting the family education being provided was 2 South’s 18% clinical nurse turnover rate in the previous 12-18 months, which would indicate some inexperience with teaching patients and family members about the end of life process.

 

Educational Activity Focused on Improving Nurses’ Expertise in Teaching
On December 12, 2015, Little and Beaumont Hospice arranged for four 30-minute presentations to be provided to the 2 South staff.  The education program, “Management of untoward symptoms at the end of life,” was provided at various dates and times to make it accessible to clinical nurses on both the midnight shift and day shift. The program was provided from 0600-0630 and 1400-1430 on the following dates:  

  • Wednesday, January 27, 2016 (one presentation)
  • Monday, February 1, 2016 (one presentation)
  • Wednesday, February 3, 2016 (two presentations)

 

McKenzie came to 2 South on these dates to provide the clinical nurses with education on the management of untoward symptoms at the end of life. (Evidence SE6-2, Presentation – “Management of Untoward Symptoms at the End of Life” and Attendance List)

 

During McKenzie’s education sessions, he explained the importance of recognizing the patient’s changes throughout the end of life process. The topics discussed were management of anxiety/agitation, delirium, constipation, diarrhea, dyspnea, secretions, nausea/vomiting and pain, along with effective teaching strategies. He stressed that the patient and family should be taught that these are normal responses and should be expected. McKenzie also reviewed the importance of properly explaining what nurses are doing for their family member, and of notifying the clinical nurse if their loved one is having any signs of distress. The clinical nurses were asked to “commit to sit” to provide effective teaching to the patient and family and to reinforce the education as needed. “Committing to sit” involves the clinical nurse sitting at the bedside eye to eye with the patients and family and providing the teaching. A total of 32 clinical nurses (91%) and two Assistant Nurse Managers attended the 30-minute educational sessions.

 

Additional Education Resources to Improve Nurses’ Knowledge
Four Hospice education modules were added to Beaumont Grosse Pointe nursing mandatories, which the nurses on 2 South complete annually. These modules are located on Halogen, a web-based program that Beaumont Health uses to deliver online education to its staff members. Halogen was designed by Beaumont University, which is the corporate educational arm of Beaumont Health. The new modules were also added to the new hire orientation for clinical nurses on 2 South. The four modules focus on the following topics: “Introduction to Beaumont Hospice,” “Managing Pain,” “Management of End of Life Symptoms” and “Principles of Death and Dying.” (Evidence SE6-3, BGP In-Patient Nursing Corporate Mandatories)

 

Nurses are required to review the four education modules annually to increase their knowledge and their ability to educate patient and families. The following is a synopsis of the four education modules:

  • Introduction to Beaumont Hospice identifies the role of Hospice, the model of care, vision and goals. It outlines the benefits of Hospice and the different levels of care for patients on Hospice such as routine, continuous home care, acute inpatient care and respite.
  • Managing Pain teaches the nurse the signs and symptoms of pain the patient may experience and how to effectively manage the patient’s pain. Relaxation techniques, medication management, recognizing nonverbal behaviors associated with pain and how to include family members in assisting with the management of pain.
  • Management of End of Life Symptoms outlines the core principles for end of life care. The module reviews common symptoms such as nutrition/hydration, breathing changes, anxiety, delirium, constipation, dyspnea/respiratory distress, secretions, nausea/vomiting, pain and how to control these symptoms. A section on education of the family that these are the symptoms of the end of life and should be expected during this process. 
  • Principles of Death and Dying defines what is death and dying. It provides the clinical nurse with what to expect during the death and dying process beginning with weeks to days before death. The syndrome of imminent death and how to support the family during this time to ensure that they understand the death and dying process. The final topic is care of the body after death.  

 

Effectiveness of Education on Patient/Family Education
Once the classes and modules were completed, it was expected that clinical nurses would teach the patient and family about the dying process and what the nurses are doing for their loved ones. Audits were conducted on the education being provided to the patient and family members in the patient’s chart. (Evidence SE6-4, EMR-Patient Education Documentation) During daily leader rounding, Little would assess families’ knowledge of symptoms and the dying process and listen for any concerns or misunderstandings. Families often complimented the clinical nurses for ensuring their understanding. (Evidence SE6-5, Family Letter)

 

Colleen Knurek, LMSW, Beaumont Hospice, reported to Little a compliment from a patient and family who said the clinical nurses were wonderful with providing education about what to expect during the end of life and disease processes and with providing emotional support. Knurek said the family was able to verbalize the patient’s changes in breathing patterns and knew when they needed to call the clinical nurse to help keep their loved one comfortable. The family said the clinical nurses taught them what to expect with the change in breathing, so it did not alarm them as much. The clinical nurse ensured the patient was provided respect, comfort and dignity during end of life care, and used the most appropriate measures to alleviate pain and other physical symptoms. This was accomplished by the nurse explaining to the family members and patient the signs and symptoms of end of life and what to expect. Providing patients and families with this information helped them understand what to expect and how to deal with the situation.

 

The nurse provided the family with techniques such as being present, sitting with their loved one and talking to them, and helped them understand that even though their loved one cannot engage in conversation this does not mean that they cannot hear them. The family was also taught about providing soft music for their family member, moist compresses if their family member is too warm and emotional support to the family member during the end of life process. The family expressed their understanding of how to play a role in their loved one’s care to ensure comfort, and their decreased anxiety through the knowledge provided by the clinical nurse.
