Example One: Patient Experience – Communicating with the Patient and Family Regarding Delays in Scheduled Patient Surgery

 

Background/Problem
Customer service scores hold great importance to Surgical Services clinical nurses, with the Press Ganey™ patient satisfaction scores posted in staff break rooms and in the Surgical Services showcase. Nurses frequently communicate about these scores in staff meetings, and written communications include customer service updates to emphasize the importance of every communication with customers, including patients and their family and friends. 

 

Beaumont Grosse Pointe’s overall surgical volume has increased by 25% since 2012, and patient satisfaction is certainly a key factor contributing to this growth. Surgical Services continue to strive for improvement, as there is always an opportunity for greater customer satisfaction. 

 

The clinical nurses in the surgical area engage in open communication with nursing leadership, enabling them to express any concerns they have regarding the care provided in the operating rooms. The goal isto ensure that the patient experience is as positive as it can be. The most efficient way to express concerns to nursing leadership is through the multiple daily huddles that are held to review the activity of the day and to address any issues clinical nurses are facing. The following huddles are held in the surgical area:

  • 0700 for the Operating Room (OR) clinical nurses
  • 1000 for the Interdisciplinary Huddle, which includes clinical nurses from Anesthesia, Pre-Op, Post Anesthesia Care Unit (PACU), Inpatient Surgery and Anesthesia Pre-screening (APS)
  • 1300 Equipment Huddle, attended by clinical nurse Service Line Coordinators, Material Management and Central Processing Department

 

Everyone has a voice, with all input is both valued and appreciated by Nursing Leadership.

 

BGP Surgical Services comprises the following departments:

  • Anesthesia Pre-screening (APS)
  • Anesthesia
  • Pre-Op
  • IP Surgery
  • PACU
  • Endoscopy
  • Central Processing Department
  • Pain Clinic

 

Linda Witt, MA, BSN, RN, ONC, Director of Surgical Services, continually brainstorms with nurse leaders and clinical nurses to explore ways to improve the customer satisfaction scores in Surgical Services. Witt also meets monthly with the BGP Customer Service Coordinators, Mary Kay Blaine and Maureen Bennett, to discuss the scores.

 

In late 2013, one of the more common themes identified through analyzing Press Ganey scores and reading patient comments was the opportunity to improve upon managing the often unavoidable delays that patients and families experience in the procedural environment. During one of the customer service meetings in September 2013, Surgical Services clinical nurses Kathleen Kastner, RN and Christina Spencer, RN spoke with Witt and Administrative Managers Deborah Jankowski, BSN, RN and Roz Talampas, BSN, RN about improving communication with patients and families. The clinical nurses said that many variables skew the daily schedule, and delays are often unavoidable. While the APS nurses do a good job of setting the expectation that delays often occur when they speak with patients on the telephone before the day of surgery, patients were still giving negative responses for the Press Ganey questions regarding “Communication with Patients and Families.”  

 

Kastner and Spencer discussed what more could be done to increase patients and families’ satisfaction with communication, and the idea of providing a communication update every 30 minutes was suggested. Kastner and Spencer discussed this idea with Witt and Ruth Roberts, MSN, RN, CPAN, Education Specialist, during a huddle in September 2013,and everyone agreed this would be a good practice to implement and maintain. Witt suggested having volunteers help with this initiative. 

 

In fourth quarter 2013 (October–December 2013), Surgical Services’ Press Ganey patient satisfaction score for “Information about delays” was 60%. This score measures the percentage of patients/families responding “always.”

 

Goal Statement
To increase Surgical Services’ Press Ganey score for the question, “Information about delays.” 

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
January 2014
During a 10 a.m. interdisciplinary daily huddle, Witt presented Kastner and Spencer’s idea of rounding on patients and families to keep them informed about any delays in their procedure and informing them that they will be communicated with every 30 minutes. Witt explained the importance of communicating with patients and families regarding delays in the surgical schedule.

 

The idea of 30-minute rounding was discussed in the daily huddles for the next two weeks. After much discussion, Witt decided that the Pre-Op nurses would be responsible for keeping the patients and families apprised of any and all delays. 

 

Witt recruited some of the more experienced Pre-Op nurses to begin the process of informing patients and families of any delays in their procedure every 30 minutes. The following nurses agreed to take part in the initial training; Barszczowski, Carson, Cesarz, Duby, Dyarcsyk, Halter, Moses, Revenberg and Slocum. 

 

When discussing the process, the Pre-Op clinical nurses suggested that the APS clinical nurses should explain that surgical delays occur periodically, but that communication will be ongoing to keep the patient and family informed.

 

February 2014
Witt enlisted Roberts’ help to educate the PACU and APS clinical nurseson the new process for keeping the patient and family informed of delays in their surgical procedure. The following topics were discussed:

  • Review Press Ganey scores regarding delays
  • Discuss ways to improve care
  • Explain ways to communicate delays to patients and families
  • Set expectation of delays when APS calls the patients
  • Explain the delay to the patient and family
  • The importance of communicating with family every 30 minutes.

 

March 2014
The new process of keeping patients and families informed every 30 minutes of any and all delays in their procedure was implemented smoothly. Witt monitored compliance to ensure that patients and families were kept up to date every 30 minutes.The nurses would monitor the scheduled procedure time to ensure that updates were provided.    

 

During the implementation phase of this change, the clinical nurses began using one of the volunteers who monitor the surgical waiting room. Bettye Wright, RN, a retired nurse who was very supportive of patient and family communication, would assist the Pre-Op nurses with communicating with patients and families to keep them informed.

Example Two: Change in Nursing Practice: Eliminating Look-Alike Sound-Alike Medications in the Endoscopy Pyxis

 

Background/Problem
One of the procedure rooms in Endoscopy that is infrequently used for bronchoscopies had to be set up for this procedure, which is performed only six to seven times a month. Since this is an infrequently performed procedure and all clinical nurses need to maintain their skills, they have chosen to rotate through the area. With this process, it is very likely that each clinical nurse performs only one bronchoscopy case every two to three months.

 

On March 10, 2016, a patient was scheduled for a bronchoscopy in the Endoscopy room that is infrequently used for this.When the patient arrived for the procedure, the order was to give Versed 4mg IV push. When M.C., BSN, RN, Endoscopy clinical nurse, went to retrieve the Versed from the Pyxis® machine, she typed the letters “ver,” selected the medication, removed it and administered it to the patient. 

 

As M. C. was wasting the remaining medication, she noticed that she had selected and administered 4 mg of Verapamil instead of Versed. She immediately notified Stephanie Metcalf, BSN, RN, Charge Nurse for the shift, and Sandy Montgomery, BSN, RN, Administrative Manager. Montgomery notified Linda Witt, MA, BSN, RN, ONC, Director of Surgical Services, and Robert Marchese, MD, Chief of Medicine. The patient was taken to the endoscopy recovery area and examined by Cardiology. The patient remained stable throughout the procedure and recovery period and was transferred to the Critical Care Stepdown Unit.

 

A review of the Pyxis determined that entering “ver” auto selects two medications, Verapamil and Versed, in alphabetical order. The clinical nurse therefore needed to scroll down to click on the Versed. Being in a rush, the nurse in this case chose the first selection after typing “ver” and did not notice that it was the wrong medication until after it was given. The combination of needing to set up the room and this not being a routine procedure for the nurse may have caused the atmosphere to feel rushed.

 

M.C. spoke with Montgomery at length regarding the incident and suggested that the unit remove all medications that look alike and sound alike from the Pyxis system to prevent medication errors. M.C. also suggested reviewing all medications in the Pyxis and removing any that are not regularly administered. Montgomery believed these were great suggestions, and she informed M.C. that this error was going to be reviewed at a root cause analysis (RCA) meeting, to which she would be invited.

 

On March 14, 2016, Montgomery held a special staff meeting of Endoscopy clinical nurses to discuss the error. The following items were covered:

    • A description of the error
    • A discussion of ways to prevent the error
    • The importance of not rushing
    • The importance of not allowing interruptions or distractions
    • A review of the five rights of medication administration

 

The RCA was held on March 22, 2016 and included Operating Room nursing leadership, clinical nurses, the Director of Quality and Safety, staff members from Quality and Safety, and representatives from Medical Administration, Hospital Administration and Pharmacy. 

 

This incident resulted in a medication error rate of 1.53 per 1,000 doses administered.

 

Goal Statement
To decrease the rate of medication errors in Endoscopy to 0 by addressing look-alike, sound-alike medications. 

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
The RCA team determined through the RCA that the root cause of this error was medical management, specifically, two look-alike, sound-alike medications stored in alphabetical order in the Pyxis machine. The team encouraged Montgomery to work with the clinical nurses to recommend and implement strategies to prevent this type of error from occurring again.

 

The following strategies were implemented in the Endoscopy unit:

 

April 2016
On April 1, 2016, following the outcome of the RCA, Montgomery used suggestions from the Endoscopy clinical nurses to remove the following rarely used medications from the Pyxis:

  • Verapamil
  • Ephedrine
  • Digoxin

 

Montgomery spoke with Beaumont University and had the following four patient medication safety education modules added for all Endoscopy nurses to complete within the next 90 days. These modules, which reinforce the safe administration of medications, were assigned as a refresher for all clinical nurses.

  • 2016 Patient Medication Safety Lesson One 16076
  • 2016 Patient Medication Safety Lesson Two 16077
  • 2016 Patient Medication Safety Lesson Three 16078
  • 2016 Patient Medication Safety Lesson Four 16079

 

Finally, Montgomery changed the practice of giving IV push medications in the Endoscopy Suite. Whenever a clinical nurse in Endoscopy is giving an IV medication in Endoscopy, it now requires verification by two clinical nurses. The goal of this change in practice was to eliminate any further medication errors in the Endoscopy Suite.
