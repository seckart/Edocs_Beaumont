TL2

 

Strategic Planning TL2

 

Nurse leaders and clinical nurses advocate for resources to support nursing unit and organizational goals.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of a nurse leader's advocacy that resulted in the allocation of resources to support an organizational goal.
    AND

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of a clinical nurse's (or clinical nurses') advocacy that resulted in the allocation of resources to support a nursing unit goal.

 

 

 

Example One: Advocating for Resources to Support an Organizational Goal – Increasing the Nurse Specialty Certification Rate
 
Organizational Goal
In 2015, Beaumont Health set an overarching goal for all eight hospitals in the health system to develop plans to obtain Magnet® designation within the next five to seven years. Currently, two hospitals in the system have Magnet designation through the American Nurses Credentialing Center (ANCC): Beaumont Royal Oak and Beaumont Troy. Rick Swaine, MBA, MSF, CPA, Hospital President, and Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, set an organizational goal for Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) to achieve Magnet designation by the end of 2018. (Evidence TL2-1, Beaumont Hospital Grosse Pointe Operational Initative-2016)

 

Nurse Leader
In September 2015, Randy Whitney, MSA, BSN, RN, NE-BC was hired as the Director of Professional Practice and Magnet Program Director (MPD) for BGP.

 

Whitney conducted a review of the various meeting minutes, reports and plans to gain insight into BGP and identify any deficiencies in the pursuit of the organizational goal of Magnet designation by 2018. Based on Whitney’s previous experience as an MPD, he knew that specialty certification is important for Magnet designation and that a two-year plan for increasing nursing certification must be identified and measured. He noted that on October 15, 2014, the Nursing Clinical Integration Council discussed a goal to increase the certification rate by 5% at the hospital and unit levels.  

 

The focus of the Nursing Clinical Integration Council is to set the direction and strategic goals of nursing at BGP. Membership includes the Nursing Director and CNO of all areas of nursing at BGP that report to the CNO. Stewart’s CNO update at the October 2014 meeting included BGP’s progress toward obtaining Magnet designation. Stewart knew from discussion with the CNOs at Royal Oak and Troy that specialty nursing certification is a component of Magnet designation, which requires that a goal be set to increase the number of certified nurses. (Evidence TL2-2, October 15, 2014 Nursing Clinical Integration Council Minutes)

 

Whitney reviewed the number of certified nurses and compared it to the number from October 2014, and determined that the hospital and the Family Birth Center had both exceeded the goal of 5% for 2014. Based on his review of the numbers, Whitney determined that the certification rates would have to again increase by 5% by the end of 2015 for BGP to meet the organizational goal of Magnet designation. Whitney determined that the 5% goal set by the Nursing Clinical Integration Council in October 2014 was attainable.

 

Nurse Leader Advocacy

  • Getting buy in from key stakeholders

    • Whitney investigated ways to increase certification at BGP and determined that the ANCC Success Pays Program would assist in this. The ANCC Success Pays Program allows nurses to take a certification exam up to two times without an upfront charge, and the hospital is billed for only those nurses who pass the exam.

     

    In January 2016, Whitney approached Stewart and Pandora Walker, Director of Human Resources (HR), with a proposal to participate in the Success Pays Program. He stressed the importance of increasing BGP’s certification rate and that this program would assist in achieving the organizational goal of Magnet designation. Whitney developed a proposal for participating in the Success Pays certification program, presented it to Stewart and Walker and gained their approval. Walker, being new to the organization, wanted to verify that BGP would be able to enter into this agreement since it would be an added expense for the HR Department. It was proposed that the cost of the program be directed to the HR budget, as HR was paying the expenses for nurses who obtain specialty certification. (Evidence TL2-3, Proposal for Success Pays Certification Program)

     

  • Making a Business Case

    • By the end of January 2016, HR had not made a decision regarding the Success Pays Program. Whitney approached Stewart and requested that the proposal be presented to the Administrator’s weekly meeting to obtain funding from BGP instead of HR. Stewart supported the idea, and Whitney was added to the February 2, 2016 agenda to give an update on the Magnet Journey. (Evidence TL2-4, Agenda and Minutes of Administrator’s Weekly Meeting, February 2, 2016) The meeting was attended by Swaine; Stewart; Christine Stesney-Ridenour, MBA, Vice President and Chief Operating Officer; Kristen Cavender, BSIE, MBA, CSSBB, Administrative Director; Robert Kilpatrick, BSRS, RTR, Administrative Director; and Maria Miller, Controller BGP.

     

    Whitney presented the Success Pays proposal to the administrators, stating that is was necessary to meet the organizational goal of obtaining Magnet designation. He reviewed the following points of the proposal to ensure that they understood the importance of the program:

    • Objective – Increase certification rates.
    • Background – Why it is important to have certified nurses and the benefits of certification.
    • Summary of Success Pays Program – It was stressed that nurses would incur no out of pocket costs. One of the benefits of the Success Pays Program is that employees do not have to pay up front, as BGP is billed once the nurse passes the certification exam.
    • Current state at BGP – What nurses currently do to obtain reimbursement for certification.
    • Future state at BGP – How the program will be implemented at BGP.
    • Pros and cons of the Success Pays Program.
    • Cost of the program – Whitney explained that the program cost would be approximately $5,000 to $10,000. This would be based on the number of nurses who sign up for the program, as BGP would only be charged for the nurses who pass the certification exam.

 

Allocation of Resources
Based on Whitney’s presentation, the administrators agreed to fund the Success Pays Program for one year and determine the future direction at a later date. 

 

On February 16, 2016, Stewart signed a purchase agreement with the ANCC Success Pays Program to begin enrolling nurses on March 1, 2016. The program was finalized on February 18, 2016, and in early March Stewart distributed a memo to all BGP nursing colleagues outlining the Success Pays Program and how to access it. (Evidence TL2-5, Success Pays Purchase Agreement, Invoices, Memo to Nursing Colleagues and Memo on How to Access)

 

Since March 1, 2016, 10 nurses have registered for the Success Pays Program to sit for a certification exam. Seven BGP nurses have successfully achieved specialty certification, with the goal of a 5% increase in hospital certifications achieved by the end of 2016 (see SE3EO).

Whitney successfully advocated for resources to increase certification rates at BGP in support of the organizational goal of achieving Magnet designation. Without his advocacy and the support of Hospital Administration, the Success Pays Program would not be available to the BGP clinical nurses and nursing leadership.

 

Example Two: Advocating for Resources to Support a Nursing Unit Goal – Permanent Charge Nurse on the Critical Care Stepdown Unit

 

Nursing Unit Goal
Patients and their families are the heart of Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP), and nurses at all levels are dedicated ambassadors in providing “compassionate, extraordinary care every day.” Clinical nurses are empowered to be advocates in identifying resources needed to provide this extraordinary care. Critical Care Stepdown Unit (CCSU) clinical nurses identified and advocated for a resource need of improved daily clinical support and staffing to support the unit’s goal of providing compassionate, extraordinary care. This translated into the development of a permanent charge nurse role to support the resource need requested by clinical nurses. It was announced at the April 2014 staff meeting that the team leader position for nights was open, and the clinical nurses began to discuss the idea of a permanent charge nurse. (Evidence TL2-6, Critical Care Stepdown Unit Staff Meeting Minutes) The idea of the permanent charge nurse was discussed further in the Unit Practice Council on May 28, 2014.

 

Background
In March 2014, clinical nurses Jennifer Sams, RN; Edna Kouzoukas, RN; Theresa Lopez, RN; Janet Szewczyk, RN; and Michelle Urben, RN took the initiative to voice concerns to Susan Wolka, BSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager (ANM) CCSU, which led to the development of the permanent, daily charge nurse role that has since been implemented house-wide. With increasing patient acuity levels and shorter hospital stays, the role of the charge nurses has become integral to optimal patient care.

 

Clinical Nurses’ Advocacy

  • Communicating the need for Additional Resources to the ANM and Director through Unit Practice Council and Staff Meetings

    • In March 2014, CCSU clinical nurses began expressing their concerns regarding the need for manager support in relation to the staffing matrix. Several clinical nurses, including Sams, Kouzoukas, Lopez, Szewczyk and Urben, brought this to Wolka’s attention through verbal communication.

     

    During this time in 2014, CCSU used a “T-Code,” or charge nurse, on days when the ANM was not scheduled to work. This was generally one day per workweek and on weekends and holidays.

     

    On May 28, 2014, the CCSU Unit Practice Council (UPC) invited Wolka and Marie Reinman, RN, Director of Critical Care and Heart/Vascular, to the meeting to discuss the T-Code/charge nurse. Specifically, the UPC asked whether the T-Code/charge nurse should have a patient assignment when the ANM is at work, and for clarification of T-Code responsibilities. (Evidence TL2-7, Unit Practice Council Meeting Minutes, May 28, 2014) At that time, it was determined that the “as needed” charge nurse would take a light patient assignment to help facilitate the requirements of the floor.

    Around the same time, clinical nurses had been expressing in staff meetings, safety huddles and open door communication with Wolka that a daily charge nurse rather than the “as needed” charge nurse would be beneficial to the unit. The clinical nurses also said that a charge nurse with an assignment, even on days when an ANM is present, was not as advantageous to their needs, as it could possibly become overwhelming for the clinical nurses. Wolka believed that her role as ANM had been evolving into more of an administrative role, in which her clinical support to nurses was not always available.

     

    CCSU clinical nurses said that they believed a daily charge nurse without a patient assignment would provide a critical clinical resource for when they needed support in delivering expert, competent care. Other benefits of the daily charge nurse perceived and expressed by CCSU clinical nurses included improved patient care experience, increased clinical nurse job satisfaction, mediation and improved service recovery, and a resource for mentoring new nurses to decrease the nurse turnover rate.

     

  • Clinical Nurses Advocating for Additional Resources with the CNO

    • In June 2014, Wolka and Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BE, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, held a Nursing Leadership Forum (NLF). NLFs provide an opportunity for clinical nurses to spend time with ANMs, Nursing Leaders and the CNO to discuss topics that require attention and what is working well. The NLF meeting dates and times are posted for every employee on the unit to see, and all are welcome to attend. Stewart encourages clinical nurse advocacy and the expression of thoughts and concerns by staff members, while using the opportunity to share specific unit/hospital information. As breakdowns in communication are the most common root of problems, it is imperative to engage in open and honest communication throughout the year.

     

    At the June 2014 NLF meeting, CCSU clinical nurses said that a permanent daily charge nurse, instead of the “as needed” charge nurse, would support their professional practice and allow them to enhance the delivery of patient and family centered care.

     

  • Using Culture of Safety Scores to Advocate for Additional Resources

    • In June 2014, CCSU nurses participated in the Culture of Safety (COS) survey that is administered yearly by Beaumont Health. The results of the COS survey, which were shared with nurses at a meeting on August 4, 2016, echoed the concerns that clinical nurses had previously expressed to Wolka, Reinman and Stewart. Through the COS survey, the CCSU nurses requested more support with staffing, help in retaining new employees and to engage “seasoned-experienced” staff, ensuring the delivery of high-quality care to patients. The COS meeting emphasized that a charge nurse needed to be assigned on the day shift, even when the ANM is working. ANMs expressed that there were too many interruptions of their daily leadership tasks, which they believed resulted in them not being able to adequately engage in the clinical aspects of their position. These included staff coaching, the prompt handling of difficult patient situations, always being available to support the staff when a Rapid Response or Code CPR is called, collaborating with physicians and other disciplines on daily rounding, adequately assisting in the regulation of the flow on the unit and adequately leading the staff. (Evidence TL2-8, Summary of Inpatient Nursing Leadership Culture of Safety Meeting-CCSU August 4, 2014, Attendance Sheet and Culture of Safety Action Plan)

 

Allocation of Resources – Three Month Trial of Charge Nurse Role
Based on the clinical nurses’ advocacy, Wolka held a verbal discussion with Stewart in August 2014 and obtained permission to trial a daily T-Code/charge nurse. Stewart subsequently told Wolka that she would allocate resources to adjust the staffing matrix to reflect the T-Code/charge nurse for a trial of three months.

 

On September 4, 2014, the staffing matrix for CCSU was updated to reflect the inclusion of a charge RN. The three-month trial of a daily charge nurse was communicated to the staff at the CCSU staff meeting in September 2014. Guidelines for the dayshift charge nurse were developed at this time, outlining the general responsibilities assigned to this role. The three-month trial of the daily charge nurse (September–November 2014) proved successful. Feedback on the permanent charge nurse trial given during CCSU leadership discussions on October 3, 2014, showed that the staff was seeing improvements. (Evidence TL2-9, Pre-Post Staffing Matrix CCSU, September 2014 CCSU Staff Meeting Minutes and Guidelines for CCSU Charge Nurse)

 

Another COS survey was completed in December 2014, with the CCSU staff communicating that a daily charge nurse without a patient assignment was a “big need” that had been filled. With the overwhelmingly positive feedback from the trial and the advocacy of the clinical nurses, Stewart allocated resources for the indefinite continuation of the trial. Stewart also allocated additional resources to allow other units to trial the use of a permanent charge nurse. 

 

Participation in Budget Process
The trial was completed on CCSU in February 2015. The trial had continued to show the overwhelmingly positive impact of the permanent charge nurse on the clinical nurses’ goal to have a charge nurse without patients that they had advocated for. Stewart set into motion the fiscal appropriations to support the hiring of four permanent charge nurses on CCSU and on other units as staffing dictated. It was believed that dedicated, permanent charge nurses would be ideal in supporting the continued goals of BGP. The CCSU charge nurse position was posted on April 6, 2015.

 

Interviews with the four CCSU charge nurse candidates were held on April 20, 2015. Andrea Carstens, BSN, RN; Michelle Urben, RN; Theresa Lopez, RN; and Shelly Jennings, RN were all internal candidates.

 

The interviewing panel included Wolka and clinical nurses Lianne Pounders, RN and Christine Cooper, RN. The candidate interviews included questions regarding their personal leadership characteristics. After the interviews, the interview panel of peers determined that all four would be offered positions as permanent charge nurses, officially starting in May 2015. (Evidence TL2-10 Posting, Interviewing Tools and Assignment Sheet for Charge Nurse)

 

Success of Clinical Nurses’ Advocacy for Permanent Charge Nurses
Since the implementation of the permanent charge nurse positions, the results and feedback about the role have been continually positive. The COS reports for CCSU continue to be positive, showing that the charge nurse role has affected the overall perception of teamwork, promotion of patient safety, continuous improvement, openness of communication and with teamwork with other units

 

Staff comments about having a charge nurse without patients have been positive. For example, Kristine Palffy, RN stated that she appreciates the charge nurse role in helping her provide exceptional care and that she always knows that the charge nurse “has her back” and advocates for the needs of CCSU. Other staff comments reaffirm that the role of the permanent charge nurse has been an added value not only to the clinical nurses, but also to nursing leaders and the entire organization. Most importantly, it supports the Beaumont Health goal of providing “compassionate, extraordinary care every day,” the heart of why we all do what we do for our patients.
