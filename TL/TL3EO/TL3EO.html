The CNO influences organization-wide change beyond the scope of nursing.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of a CNO-influenced positive change that had organization-wide impact, beyond the scope of nursing services. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.

 

 

Increasing the HCAHPS Discharge Domain Score

 

Background
At Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP), a customer service meeting is held every Tuesday to review the HCAHPS scores for the previous week and other issues related to customer service. The meeting is for all leaders and clinical staff members from all areas of the hospital who are able to attend. The February 17, 2016 customer service meeting included a discussion about the low scores for the HCAHPS Discharge domain.  The participants determined that the data showed there was a problem with the discharge process and work flow.

 

The discharge paperwork, called the After Visit Summary (AVS), was lengthy and complicated for both patients and the nursing staff, as it typically consisted of six to10 printed pages for each discharge. This paperwork was overwhelming for patients and family members, and it was difficult to ascertain what was most important for the patient care needs after discharge.

 

In addition, there was no process in place to provide patients with information on their primary diagnosis that they could refer to at home. Many tools were available to the nursing staff, but there was no single tool identified as the one to print for all patients at discharge. The nurse would often talk with the patient and/or family members about the diagnosis, but there was no clear expectation about what was most important for the patient after discharge.

 

Another concern was the number of calls that patients or family members made in the evenings to the charge nurses and/or Assistant Nurse Managers about the discharge instructions and medications. The AVS was not clear about which medications the patient needed to take that day; some nurses “free-handed” comments about this, while others did not. There were no clear, basic instructions provided about medications.

 

As the discharge process was examined more closely during the customer service meeting, the participants identified that there was no consistent process on the day of discharge. Discharge instructions were most often provided quickly with little time spent at the bedside explaining the discharge and answering questions. The nurses rarely took time with the patient and family to ensure that they had the appropriate discharge education.

 

The hospital’s January 2016 HCAHPS score for the Discharge domain was at the 6th percentile, meaning 94% of the hospitals in the Press Ganey database were doing better than BGP. The Discharge domain consists of two questions:

  • During this stay, did doctors, nurses or other hospital staff talk with you about whether you would have the help you needed when you left the hospital?
  • During this stay, did you get information in writing about what symptoms or health problems to look out for after you left the hospital?

 

It was clear that a plan was needed to improve, simplify and bring structure to the discharge process and the education for the patient and family. 

 

Goal Statement
To increase the HCAHPS Discharge domain score for all discharged patients.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
On February 26, 2016, Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, convened an organization-wide group to improve the discharge education for patients. The group comprised leaders and bedside caregivers (from nursing, pharmacy, care management and respiratory therapy, along with volunteers) to discuss the role each of them played in patient discharge.  The role of the bedside caregiver is complex, and the team wanted to find a way to simplify the patient discharge process for the healthcare team and to increase the patients and family’s knowledge regarding home care. Although a clinical nurse is the last person to complete the discharge, many disciplines impact the patient’s preparation for discharge. This is why it was decided that this program was beyond the scope of nursing, and that an organization-wide change was needed to increase the patients and family’s knowledge.

 

During this meeting, the organization-wide group decided that discharge should be as “simple as 1-2-3,” so the program was initially called Discharge 1-2-3; it has since been simplified to DC123. Representatives from each specialty were asked to identify and bring to the next meeting three items that they should complete and review with the patient and family member prior to discharge. The representatives from each area were to speak with their staff and bring their three items to the meeting on March 11, 2016. The plan was then to begin the new process on March 30, 2016.

 

It was determined through discussions with various staffs that the disciplines of nursing, pharmacy, care management, respiratory therapy and volunteers were responsible for the majority of the required discharge teaching. To ensure that the discharge process was multidisciplinary, the disciplines proposed that they be responsible for the following areas:

 

Nursing: 

  • Discharge instruction
  • Medication information
  • After Visit Summary

 

Respiratory therapy:

  • Medication education
  • Oxygen requirement
  • Pulmonary plan at discharge

 

Care management:

  • Where is the patient going?
  • How are they getting there?
  • Plan at discharge

 

Pharmacy:

  • What the medication is for
  • How to use it
  • Side effects

 

Volunteers:

  • Arrive to room within 15 minutes of being called to escort patient out
  • Verify patient has discharge folder

 

The group held meetings on March 4 and 11, 2016 to review the suggestions about what each specialty would be responsible to complete. After the discussions at these meetings, the group developed a checklist based on the input of all the disciplines.

 

On March 28, 2016, the final checklist was approved and education was provided. Stewart, in conjunction with the members of multidisciplinary group, educated the caregivers on the new discharge process to ensure a smooth transition.

 

Education for the clinical assistants was completed on March 29, 2016. The checklist was to be placed on the patient discharge folder by the clinical assistant, and the checklist was to remain on the discharge folder until the patient left.

 

The Discharge 123 program began on March 30, 2016. To ease the process, the team decided that the folder was to be placed on the bedside table so each discipline had access to the checklist and folder. The goal was for each discipline to address and check off their items on the checklist by discharge. 

 

Pharmacy provides a medication card with the first dose of all new medications. The clinical nurse reviews the card with the patient and family member before administering the first dose of medication, and the card is then to be placed in the discharge folder.At discharge, if the patient is not going home on this particular medication, the clinical nurse discards the card.

 

If an item from the checklist has not been completed, the discharge RN will call for someone from that area to see the patient by the time of discharge (contact numbers are on the checklist). The patient discharge is not delayed, as someone will respond quickly. At discharge, the checklist is placed in the basket at the unit desk by a staff members or volunteer.

Outcomes

Following the interventions by Stewart’s team, the Discharge domain score for all discharged patients increased from the 6th percentile in January 2016 to an all-time high of the 65th percentile in July 2016.
