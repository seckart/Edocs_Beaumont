TL7

 

Advocacy and Influence TL7

 

Nurse leaders, with clinical nurse input, use trended data to acquire necessary resources to support the care delivery system(s).

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, where a nurse leader, with clinical nurse input, used trended data to acquire necessary resources to support the care delivery system(s).

 

 

3 Southeast Nurse Leaders Using Trended Turnover Data to Support the Decision to Hire a Team Leader

 

In 2014, 3 Southeast (3SE) Orthopedic Unit Assistant Nurse Managers (ANM) Alexis Rogers, RN and Mandy O’Toole, RN, reported directly to Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer. Rogers and O’Toole held authority for 3SE, a 44-bed Medical and Surgical Unit that specializes in the care of adult orthopedic patients. 

 

In 2014, the orthopedic volume was up 10% from 2013; however, the length of stay (LOS) decreased from 4.03 to 3.55 days. The average daily census (ADC) in 2014 was 19 patients per day. Stewart suggested that, although the volume was increasing, the LOS was decreasing so the ADC was also decreasing. It was decided that 3SE’s ADC did not support two ANM positions, so in March 2015 the off shift ANM position was eliminated. Rogers was selected for the 3SE ANM role because of her expertise and managerial experience, but O’Toole’s departure left a void in direct leadership support for the off shift staff. 

 

Clinical Nurse Input
In late March 2015, after O’Toole’s departure,3SE clinical nurses Patricia Danna, RN; Lori Leman, BSN, RN; and Sara Munsterman, BSN, RN requested a meeting with Rogers to express their concern that O’Toole’s departure had left a lack of leadership and clinical expertise on the off shift. They told Rogers that new clinical nurse hires for off shift positions are typically graduate or novice RNs, and that charge nurse responsibilities on the off shift fell to clinical nurses without the ANM. The off shift clinical nurse carried the charge phone, managed patient flow within the unit, created the staffing assignment for the on-coming shift, assigned surgical beds and acted as a resource to the staff, all while managing a patient assignment. Leman said that an excess of novice nurses on the midnight shift increased the burden on the experienced nurses. (Evidence TL7-1, Notes from March 30, 2015 Meeting)

 

To gain more insight into the issues, in April 2015 Rogers conducted informal exit interviews with clinical nurses Charlette Benson, RN and Jameson Thompson, RN, who were leaving 3SE. The nurses identified the following themes in these exit interviews:

  • Carrying the 3SE charge phone with a full patient assignment is stressful.
  • Carrying the 3SE charge phone with a full patient assignment interrupts patient care.
  • The shift was lacking a mentor.
  • The shit was lacking an experienced clinical nurse as a “go-to” person.

 

Trended Data
In late March 2015, Danna, Leman and Munsterman met with Rogers to discuss their concerns and determine the unit’s needs. Over the next few months, clinical nurse turnover on the unit continued. In July 2015, Rogers reviewed 3SE’s clinical nurse turnover and transfer trended data for April, May and June 2015. Beaumont Health defines“turnover” as someone who has left the corporation, and “transfer” as someone who has left their current unit. (Evidence TL7-2, 3SE Clinical Nurse Turnover Trended Data)

To further understand the issues and the nursing staff’s concerns, Rogers reviewed LOS trended data for 3SE, which demonstrated that the average LOS was decreasing. (Evidence TL7-3, 3SE Length of Stay Trended Data) Rogers knew that while the LOS had decreased, the throughput had increased. She recognized that with patients moving through the system more quickly, less experienced nurses can easily become overwhelmed with the required work.

After seeking input from the clinical nurses and reviewing the data, Rogers recognized that something needed to be done to create a positive, safe and supportive environment for off shift clinical nurses. During one of Rogers’ one-on-one meetings with Stewart, Rogers discussed the clinical nurses’ concerns about the lack of leadership on the off shift. Stewart suggested to Rogers that she meet with the clinical nurses and develop a plan to address their concerns and decrease the clinical nurse turnover rate on 3SE.

 

Rogers recalled that in late 2013 the Critical Care Stepdown Unit (CCSU) had experienced a high clinical nurse turnover and had a large influx of new graduate nurses on the off shift. To support the midnight shift clinical nurses, CCSU successfully implemented the Team Leader role. Rogers thought this might be exactly what the 3SE off shift needed. She therefore met with CCSU Assistant Nurse Managers Susan Wolka, BSN, RN and Eric Quenneville, BSN, RN to inquire about the success of the off shift Team Leader role on CCSU. Wolka and Quenneville gave Rogers a copy of the Team Leader job description and suggestions on how to proceed.

 

3SE had 20.85 budgeted full-time equivalents (FTEs) for clinical nurses. Of these, 10.42 FTEs were clinical nurses for the off shift. Rogers reached out to Dana Ludwig, Employment Representative Human Resource Grosse Pointe, for a list of the RNs hired to 3SE in 2015. The list showed that 53% of the RNs on the 3SE off shift had little to no nursing experience. 

 

Rogers began to develop her plan to address the turnover on 3SE. To further validate her proposal, in November 2015 she met with clinical nurses hired for the off shift in 2014 and 2015. Rogers asked about the 3SE onboarding experience and opportunities for improvement. Feedback from the nurses included the following:

  • The experienced staff members were too busy to answer questions. 
  • While orienting a new nurse, the preceptor often had to manage a patient care assignment and also carry the charge phone at times.
  • The midnight shift nurses felt unsupported.

 

Request for Additional Resources: Lead Position and Budgeted Dollars
In November 2015, Rogers used the trended data, the information obtained from Wolka and Quenneville, and the information obtained from the discussions with the clinical nurses to develop a proposal to present to Stewart. 

 

Rogers highlighted the increased clinical nurse turnover, the large number of inexperienced nurses on the off shift and comments from the clinical nurse survey.   Rogers proposed to Stewart that a Team Leader position be created for 3SE for the off shift.

 

The main duties of the team leader would be:

  • To collaborate with the ANM on clinical and non-clinical issues
  • To be responsible for the direct oversight and supervision of the off shift staff
  • To provide resources, guidance and training to the new clinical nurses
  • To promote and support a positive working relationship and effective teambuilding

 

Resources Acquired
Stewart was supportive of Rogers’ request. To acquire the resources needed to create this position, Rogers converted one clinical nurse FTE to a Team Leader position with additional budgeted dollars allocated for this position. (Evidence TL7-4, Form 525 #6697 Adding Team Leader Position, job posting on unit and new hire letter from Humarn Resources) Rogers used the CCSU Team Leader job description and customized it to meet the needs of the 3SE unit. The Team Leader position was posted internally on December 2, 2015 in the 3SE staff conference room.

 

In January 2016, clinical nurse Julie Lynch, BSN, RN was hired into the role of Team Leader for 3 Southeast. Lynch was familiar with 3SE’s culture and practices because of her previous tenure on the unit, making her a great fit.

 

Supporting the Care Delivery System
Rogers analyzed trended data and acquired the necessary resources to support the 3SE patient care delivery system. By stabilizing the staffing on the off shift and having more experienced nurses on the unit, patient outcomes and experience began to improve. One example is falls with injuries: In third quarter 2015, the rate of falls with injury was 0.57 per 1,000 patient days. This decreased to 0.0 in second quarter 2016, 0.49 in third quarter 2016 and 0.0 in fourth quarter 2016.

 

Lynch successfully improved the morale on 3SE and onboarded 10 newly hired clinical nurses. Ensuring that the clinical nurse positions are filled has improved the care delivery system. The presence of a Team Leader on the off shift has also unified the clinical nurses, and there was zero turnover from July 2016 to November 2016. (Evidence TL7-5, July 2016 to November 2016 Grosse Pointe Nursing Unit Turnover)
