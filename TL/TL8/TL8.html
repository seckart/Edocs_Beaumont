TL8

 

Visibility, Accessibility, and Communication TL8

 

The CNO uses various methods to communicate, be visible, and be accessible to nurses throughout the organization. Choose two of the three below:

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of communication between the clinical nurse(s) and the CNO that led to a change in the nurse practice environment.
    OR

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of communication between the clinical nurse(s) and the CNO that led to a change in the patient experience.
    OR
  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of communication between the clinical nurse(s) and the CNO that influenced a change in nursing practice

 

 

Example One: Change in the Nurse Practice Environment – Increasing Staffing Based on Acuity

 

The 2 South (2S) Unit at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) is a general medical/surgical unit with a number of specialties embedded within it. The 2S scope of practice states:

“2 South provides a high quality of care for a patient population consisting of neurological, pulmonary, hospice, oncology/hematology, and general medical diagnoses. The focus of care involves our stroke, pneumonia, COPD, cancer, and sickle cell patients.”

The unit also has an eight-bed video monitoring unit that is used for patients with mental status changes, dementia or general confusion and who are deemed re-directable from a remote location at the unit desk. In addition to this challenging patient type, 2S receives the majority of the patients who are transferred from the cardiac stepdown unit. To improve patient flow, it is imrperative that patients be removed from telemetry and transferred off the telemetry unit as soon as they are removed from telemetry. 2S cares for a number of complex patient types, and also experiences significant churn with patients coming and going at a rapid pace. As BGP’s volume has increased over the last three years, the volume of patients and speed at which they move from the telemetry unit to 2S has increased substantially.

 

The staffing matrix created for 2S was based on benchmarks for a general medical/surgical unit. Although 2S fits the definition of a medical/surgical unit, staffing has been a challenge. The staff on 2S had voiced concerns about the workload in general unit meetings and on the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) culture of safety survey. The matrix had been re-evaluated a number of times, but each time it was reviewed the unit seemed to fit the benchmark for a medical/surgical unit.

 

Similar to other healthcare organizations, BGP has worked hard to improve its patient experience scores. One of the tactics it uses is to hold a weekly Service Meeting at which Hospital Administration, including the Chief Nursing Officer, Hospital and Nursing Leaders, clinical nurses and other staff members, review the Hospital Consumer Assessment of Health Providers and System (HCAHPS) scores to identify actions to improve the patient experience scores. 

 

Communication with Clinical Nurses
On December 15, 2015, one of the last Service Meetings of 2015, Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer,challenged the clinical nurses and hospital staff to identify the reasons that BGP was not able to meet its customer service targets. Aaron Achatz, RN, clinical nurse from 2S, raised his hand and spoke openly to the group about the challenges the 2S clinical nurses face on a daily basis. He described a typical patient load and articulated what he, as a 2S clinical nurse, faces on a regular basis. Achatz described the complex patient needs and the challenges this presents to the clinical nurse due to the variety of specialties involved, saying that “it’s hard to be a specialist in everything.” He also spoke about the emotional needs of hospice patients and their families, which he said were not always being met due to the demands placed on the clinical nurses by the other patients they are caring for. Stewart listened and assured Achatz that the 2S staffing matrix would be reviewed and altered as necessary. Achatz was asked to be involved in the change process.

After this customer service meeting, Stewart and 2S Assistant Nurse Managers Pamela Lively, BSN, RN, NE-BC and Kimberly Little, BSN, RN met with Achatz and the 2S clinical nurses to encourage them to communicate with Stewart about what the 2S staffing matrix should look like to improve the nurse practice environment. The staffing matrix was appropriate based on the numbers and assumptions about the medical/
surgical patient type, but according to the 2S clinical nurses it was not appropriate. Achatz and the 2S clinical nurses communicated to Stewart their concerns about patient and family acuity on 2S, and Achatz followed up after the meeting with an email to Stewart clarifying and describing the challenges. (Evidence TL8-1, Service Meeting Agenda, Work Plan and Attendance, December 15, 2015 and Memo from Achatz to Stewart dated January 12, 2016)

 

Stewart asked Lively and Little to work with Achatz to develop a proposal to increase staffing on 2 South, which would decrease the nurse-to-patient ratio and improve the nurse practice environment. Lively worked with Achatz to pull a group together to review and adjust the staffing matrix based on what the clinical nurses believed they needed to provide care. In attendance at the meeting were members of the Unit Practice Council, including clinical nurses Timothy Pierce, BSN, RN; Annette Gipperich, RN; Emily Gross, BSN, RN; and Michael Hoekstra, BSN, RN. 

 

Based on the meetings with Achatz and the 2S clinical nurses, Stewart met with Lively and Little and approved the proposed changes to the 2S Unit Matrix to increase the RN-to-patient ratio to that recommended by Achatz and his colleagues. Although this matrix was outside the benchmark for a general medical/surgical unit, Stewart supported the change based on the needs of the 2S patients and the need to improve the clinical nurse practice environment. On January 12, 2016, Little sent the proposed revised matrix to Stewart for final approval. The proposed changes made to the staffing matrix on 2 South provides for a maximum of four patients to each nurse. (Evidence TL8-2, Little Email with Proposed 2S Staffing Matrix Changes)

 

Stewart asked Little whether Achatz and the 2S clinical nurses had viewed and approved the new staffing matrix, and Little said she would verify this before the matrix was officially published. Little subsequently sent Stewart an email confirming that Achatz agreed with the new 2S matrix. (Evidence TL8-3, Little Email regarding Clinical Nurse Approval of 2S Staffing Matrix)

 

On January 28, 2016, the new 2S staffing matrix was submitted to the Nursing Business Office (NBO) for implementation into practice. (Evidence TL8-4, New 2S Unit Staffing Matrix, January 27, 2016)

 

Although the staffing matrix was adjusted, the reality was that it would take time for the nurse leaders to hire and orient enough clinical nurses to meet this new matrix. 2S’s culture of safety survey results in June 2016 continued to reflect staffing concerns, and the unit did not hit the target score of 66% for the overall perception of safety. Multiple comments related to staffing were noted on the survey. Although Stewart and nursing leadership were committed to staffing the unit to the new matrix, BGP’s overall patient volume was up and leaders could not consistently staff the unit to the new numbers. 

 

Little and Lively continued to work on recruiting and orienting additional staff members. Stewart, Lively and Little met weekly with Dana Ludwig, Nursing Recruiter, and the matrix was fully implemented in July 2016 after all the clinical nurses were hired and trained. 

 

The clinical nurses’ practice environment was improved as a result of the communication between Achatz and Stewart. With the increase in clinical nurse staffing, the nurses were able to decrease the nurse-to-patient ratio on 2 South. This enabled the nurses to provide more individualized care for the patients, and it provided a more reasonable workload for the clinical nurses. 

 

The December 2016 culture of safety survey reflected an improvement in the overall perception of safety on 2S, from 63% in June 2016 to 68% (above the target) in December 2016, and the comments reflect the improvement in staffing. (Evidence TL8-5, Culture of Safety Scores)

Example Two: Change in Patient Experience – Quiet Time on the Critical Care Stepdown Unit

 

History: Communicating with Nurses
In May 2014, Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC was appointed Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer of Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP). In June 2014, the inpatient Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) culture of safety surveys were launched for the clinical nurses and ancillary staff on the units. The AHRQ survey assesses the following:

  • Teamwork within the unit
  • Supervisor/manager expectations & actions promoting safety
  • Organizational Learning – Continuous improvement
  • Management support for patient safety
  • Overall perceptions of patient safety
  • Feedback and communication about error
  • Communication openness
  • Frequency of events reported
  • Teamwork across units
  • Staffing
  • Handoffs & transitions
  • Non-punitive response to error

 

The results of the culture of safety survey that was administered in July 2014 showed many problems in the nurse practice environment, demonstrating that something needed to be done. It was clear to Stewart that she needed to visit the units and speak directly with clinical nurses to better understand the struggles in the practice environment and, most importantly, help solve them.

 

Stewart began holding regular Culture of Safety meetings with clinical nurses on a weekly or biweekly rotation. These were eventually renamed Nursing Leadership Forums, because in addition to being used to discuss the culture of safety they provide a communication venue for Stewart to hear directly from the bedside caregivers about the issues they face daily. Stewart does not bring an agenda to these meetings, as they are meant to be a truly open forum for clinical nurse-CNO communication.

 

To ensure the voices of all staff members are heard, Stewart meets on both the day and night shift on the same unit on the same day. Following each meeting, Stewart gives the department secretary notes on the meeting. These are put into a grid that is posted on the unit so clinical nurses who bring forward a concern know how it is followed up. This also allows those who were not present to see what was discussed and what is being done about it.

 

Assessing the Patient Experience
As the nurse practice environment improved over time, Stewart heard less about the work environment for the clinical nurses and more about what was and was not working for patients. Clinical nurses provided great ideas on what was needed to improve the patient experience.  

 

During the May 27, 2015 Nursing Leadership Forum (NLF), Stewart discussed the unit and hospital HCAHPS scores. Clinical nurses often see unit scores, but do not understand them well. Stewart believed these forums provided a great opportunity to help clinical nurses better understand HCAHPS scores and to ask them for suggestions to improve patients’ experience and the HCAHPS scores. 

 

During NLFs on the Critical Care Stepdown Unit (CCSU), Stewart spoke with clinical nurses about the “Quietness” domain, and many questions were raised about the noise level on the unit. The unit is a very busy telemetry unit with a lot of patient activity. Many of the patients admitted from the Emergency Center are admitted to the telemetry unit for a time and then transferred to a lower level of care on a medical/surgical unit. All this activity makes for a somewhat chaotic practice environment for the staff and a noisy environment for the patients.

 

Stewart was discussing the patient experience scores at the May 27, 2015 NLF when Devin Mahoney, BSN, RN, CCSU clinical nurse, suggested the idea of instituting a quiet time for the CCSU patients during mid-day so they can rest. (Evidence TL8-6, May 27, 2015 CCSU Leadership Discussion Points and Sign-in Sheet) Mahoney described quiet time to his co-workers as a designated time of the day when the lights in the hallways are dimmed and the activity on the unit subsides. Families and loved ones can still be present, but the staff would try to work quietly.

 

The CCSU staff members attending the NLF supported trying the quiet time. Mahoney described it as a time during which the patients could rest and be free from unnecessary distractions, which would allow them to feel more relaxed and have a better experience on the CCSU. The rest of the clinical nurses and nursing care assistants were supportive of the idea but requested more information about the process.

 

Based on the conversation with Mahoney, Stewart brought the idea of quiet time to the Model of Care meeting (MOC) on June 1, 2015 with Nurse Leaders from all inpatient units in attendance. Stewart discussed the idea with them, and they supported piloting the idea on CCSU. Leaders of other units were also interested, but they were encouraged to first see how it went on CCSU. (Evidence TL8-7, June 1, 2015 Model of Care Agenda and Meeting Minutes)

 

Implementing Quiet Time
On June 16, 2015, Stewart brought the quiet time concept proposed by Mahoney to the Inpatient Service meeting with Mary Kay Blaine, Director Service Excellence department. (Evidence TL8-8, Email from June 16, 2015 regarding Quiet Time) Leaders were assigned to put a plan together and bring it back to Stewart and her group.

Stewart worked with Jan McGlone, System Wayfinding/Signage Coordinator, to make professional quiet time signage for the units. These were delivered to BGP on June 25, 2016. (Evidence TL8-9, Email Signage Regarding QUIET Signs are Ready, and Email Quiet Time Verbiage for Tent Cards and Communications) Mary Zoumot, Corporate Communication Specialist from Beaumont Corporate Communications, was assigned to make tent cards to ensure the patients and families were aware of what quiet time entails.

The quiet time pilot began on CCSU in July 2015, with patients, families and clinical nurses immediately making positive comments. The simple step of turning the lights down not only allowed patients and families time to rest, it also gave staff time to decompress during their very busy shifts.

 

Evaluation of Quiet Time
As the success of quiet time was clear from the start, all BGP inpatient units wanted to implement it to enhance the patient experience. The units determined their own quiet times based on their unique cultures and needs. Based on the success of quiet time on CCSU, all BGP inpatient units instituted a designated quiet time beginning on July 27, 2015, with the times that the lights were dimmed based on each unit’s patient and staff needs.

 

This change in the patient experience was implemented as a result of a clinical nurse communicating with the CNO at an NLF and the CNO using her influence to bring the concept to reality.

 

Quiet Time Improves the Patient Experience
The implementation of the quiet time has been successful in improving the patient experience at BGP. Its success is measured using the Press Ganey™ HCAHPS score for the question, “During this hospital stay how often was the area around your room quiet at night”?

 

The results have been outstanding. In first quarter 2015, before implementation, BGP ranked in the 28th percentile for top box scores compared to the entire Press Ganey database. After the implementation, BGP’s ranking increased to the 37th percentile in third quarter 2015, 44th in fourth quarter 2015 and BGP’s highest ranking of the 48th percentile in first quarter 2016. BGP has improved the patient experience by providing a safe, quiet environment. (Evidence TL8-10, Press Ganey HCAHPS Survey Results for Q1-2015, Q3-2015, Q4-2015 and Q1-2016)
