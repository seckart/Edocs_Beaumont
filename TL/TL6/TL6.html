TL6

 

Advocacy and Influence TL6

 

The CNO advocates for organizational support of ongoing leadership development for all nurses, with a focus on mentoring and succession planning.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of each of the following activities:
    • Mentoring or succession planning activities for clinical nurses
      AND
    • Mentoring or succession planning activities for nurse managers
      AND
    • Mentoring or succession planning activities for nurse leaders (exclusive of nurse managers)
      AND
    • Mentoring or succession planning activities for the chief nursing officer

 

 

Example One: Mentoring for Clinical Nurses
Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) clinical nurses, as well as nurse leaders led by Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, have identified a number of areas in which clinical nurses benefit from mentoring, education and support. These areas were identified through a variety of forums and through concerns brought independently to Stewart or other leaders by clinical nurses.

 

In early 2015, the inpatient units at BGP experienced an increase in census that required additional assistance with their day to day operation. Stewart sought salary dollars to place charge nurses on the larger inpatient units to have someone readily available to help with the day to day operations of the units and provide direct support and mentoring to the clinical nurses. These were not intended to be manager roles, but would rather be clinical nurses who were not given a patient assignment so they could assist with the “churn” of the unit and be available as mentors for clinical situations such as a sudden change in a patient condition. As these roles operationalized, there was concern that the charge nurses practiced differently between units and even on the same unit.

 

To address this variation, nursing leadership met in February 2015 and identified consistent expectations for the charge nurses on all units. The managers solicited input from the charge nurses and clinical nurses on the units to develop guidelines for the charge nurses. In addition to support and mentoring, they identified responsibilities such as monitoring for core measures, rounding on patients and troubleshooting patient care issues. The lists of responsibilities were compiled into “guidelines for charge nurses.” (Evidence TL6-1, Guidelines for 2 South Charge Nurses)

 

Even with the guidelines, questions about the charge nurses’ role remained. Stewart held a number of conversations with the charge nurses and realized there was something missing in this role. The charge nurses are frontline clinical nurses who handle a variety of patient care issues. They have an Administrative Manager as a resource, but they did not necessarily have other resources, such as a peer to reach out too, because they did not know the charge nurses on the other units.

 

Stewart spoke with a number of charge nurses in spring 2015 and it became clear that they could benefit from mentoring and support not only from the unit manager, but from one another. There was interest in having regular charge nurse meetings so they could talk through issues and concerns. Stewart saw these meetings as an opportunity to mentor the charge nurses, and she set up the first charge nurse meeting for June 19, 2015.

 

Mentoring Activity: Charge Nurse Role
At the first charge nurse meeting, facilitated by Stewart, charge nurses from various units and shifts came together to meet one another and discuss issues. The first meeting focused on introductions, including their unit, shift, background and immediate issues they may have. It was clear that this group needed a regular forum in which to meet, network and discuss issues that affect the operations of the unit. Stewart began mentoring the charge nurses, and they discussed topics such as:

  • The charge nurse role
  • Boundaries of the charge nurse role
  • What to do about employee issues
  • Communication methods

 

Stewart scheduled monthly meetings on the day and time determined by the group. (Evidence TL6-2, June 19, 2015 Charge Nurse Team Meeting Minutes)

 

During the December 2, 2015 meeting, Stewart discussed with them the importance of the group being led by a charge nurse, with secretarial support and mentoring from Stewart. Stewart discussed how she would mentor the leaders by:

  • Assisting with setting an agenda
  • Giving advice on how to run a meeting
  • Explaining how to invite guests to the meeting

 

The group was asked for volunteers to lead the team, and clinical nurses Michelle Urben, RN and Theresa Lopez, RN, both from Critical Care Step Down, volunteered to co-lead. (Evidence TL6-3, November 4, 2015 and December 2, 2015 Charge Nurse Team Minutes and Committee Roster)

 

In January 2016, Urben and Lopez became the co-leaders of the charge nurse team meeting. Prior to the January meeting, Stewart met with Urben and Lopez to mentor and assist them with preparing the agenda for the first and second meeting. The charge nurses have since addressed issues and challenges as a team. The clinical nurse co-leaders continue to successfully lead this team and make positive changes in the work life of the clinical nurses on their units.

 

Mentoring Activities: Clinical Nurse Attends Professional Practice Model Retreat
To mentor and support Urben in her new role as co-lead of the charge nurse group, in June 2016 Stewart invited Urben to attend a two-day retreat hosted by Beaumont Health (BH) to identify the Professional Practice Model (PPM) for nursing in the new system. Stewart believed this would be a great mentoring opportunity, as Urben would be able to observe how to effectively make changes. Stewart met with Urben prior to the meeting to discuss what Urben should be observing and what she should bring back to BGP in her role as a co-leader of the charge nurse group. (Evidence TL6-4, Attendance Sheet from Professional Practice Model Retreat) More than 300 nurses from the eight Beaumont Health hospitals gathered to identify a PPM that represents the blended cultures of BH hospitals. On the first day of the retreat, Christy Dempsey, MSN, MBA, RN, CNOR, CENP, Chief Nursing Officer of Press Ganey Associates, presented “An Epidemic in Empathy,” which focused on efforts to reduce patient suffering and develop compassionate, connected care across the continuum.

 

Through Stewart’s mentorship in establishing the charge nurse team meeting and appointing and supporting two clinical nurses to co-lead the committee, the charge nurses became a very successful and productive group.  The group continues to meet monthly and has become an integral part of nursing leadership. (Evidence TL6-5, Charge Nurses Meeting Minutes, February 2017)

 

Example Two: Mentoring for Nurse Managers
The opportunities available to help Nurse Managers develop and grow include:

  • Educational assistance
  • 1:1 coaching and interviewing for new positions
  • Leadership training

 

Mentoring Activity: Obtaining Educational Assistance
To improve the Nurse Mangers’ leadership skills, and in recognition of the need for all Nurse Managers to obtain a BSN, in May 2015 Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, met with Rick Swaine, MBA, MSF, CPA, Hospital President, and Lisa Ouellette, Director of Human Resources, about the number of Assistant Nurse Managers (ANM) without a BSN. These leaders were valuable members of the Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) nursing leadership team. Stewart therefore, rather than removing them from the role, lobbied for funding to supplement the tuition reimbursement program for the ANMs to pursue a BSN degree as long as they completed it within a shortened time frame, by December 2016.

 

In June 2015, Stewart met with each ANM to discuss this proposal of enhanced educational assistance. For the six nurse leaders who needed this, the agreement was that the hospital would provide supplemental assistance of up to $12,000 toward a BSN, in addition to the regular tuition assistance, as long as they completed the degree by December 31, 2016. Alexis Rogers, RN, OCN, Assistant Nurse Manger, 3 South Orthopedic, express interest in the program. (Evidence TL6-6, Enhanced Educational Assistance Benefit Agreement)

 

Rogers expressed her desire to grow and expand her role in the organization, and Stewart suggested different activities she could engage in to improve her marketability.  Rogers had been an ANM on 3 South for over 10 years and was unable to transfer because she did not have a BSN degree. Stewart offered to provide mentoring to assist her in obtaining a promotion once she obtained her BSN. Rogers accepted the educational assistance and obtained her BSN in May 2016.

 

Mentoring Activity: 1:1 Coaching Interviewing for New Positions
Rogers approached Stewart about the possibility of interviewing for the position of Administrative Manager of the Internal Resource Team. Rogers explained to Stewart that, because she had been on 3 South for so long, she was interested in expanding her role and knowledge base. Stewart supported this idea since she knew Rogers’ ultimate goal was to assume a director position and that Rogers had not interviewed for a position in at least 10 years. Stewart assisted Rogers by discussing questions that might be asked and ways to learn how to interview effectively. (Evidence TL6-7, Email June 27, 2016 Stewart Coaching Rogers)

 

Mentoring Activity: Leadership Training
The Beaumont Health Nurse Executive Council, which Stewart is a member of, discussed the needs of the frontline nursing leadership and decided to host a series of Advisory Board Leadership Education sessions as a first step in leadership development.

 

Based on the results of a gap analysis, it was decided that the sessions would be offered exclusively to leaders at the five southern hospitals of Beaumont Health. Stewart asked if BGP could send several Nurse Managers to the leadership training, and she believed this would be a great mentoring activity for Rogers based on their previous discussion. It was agreed that BGP could send several leaders if space were available. ANMs were being reclassified as Administrative Managers (AM), and it was important to Stewart and her leadership team that, once this was complete, the Nurse Managers and their staffs felt they were prepared for this new role. Stewart secured spots in the leadership training for BGP, and Rogers was able to attend.

 

Rogers attended the leadership training, which consisted of three four-hour classes held over five months. The topics included:

  • Leading Through Vision-Effective Decision Making and Meeting Long-Term Objectives
  • Instilling Accountability-Elevating Staff Performance
  • Managing Disruptive Behaviors – Creating a Healthy Workplace Culture

(Evidence TL6-8, Workbook Leadership Training and Rogers’ Certification of Continuing Education)

 

Mentoring Activity: Leadership Skill Inventory
To further prepare Rogers and mentor her in her role, Stewart hosted a luncheon on November 8, 2016 and had all nurse leaders, including Rogers, complete a Leadership Skills Inventory.


The Administrative Managers used the Nurse Manager Skills Inventory Tool ©2006, Nurse Manager Leadership Partnership (NMLP), as a self-assessment tool to evaluate themselves in the following competencies:

  • Financial Management
  • Human Resource Management
  • Performance Improvement
  • Foundational Thinking Skills
  • Technology
  • Strategic Management
  • Appropriate Clinical Practice Knowledge
  • Human Resource Leadership Skills
  • Relationship Management and Influencing Behaviors
  • Diversity
  • Shared Decision-Making
  • Personal and Professional Accountability
  • Career Planning
  • Personal Journey Disciplines
  • Reflective Practice Reference Behaviors/Tenants

 

Based on these results and discussions at this session, it was decided, with Stewart’s support, that nursing leadership would meet once a month beginning in 2017 for “lunch and learns” to provide education and mentoring opportunities. The subjects for each session would be identified by the Nurse Managers and Stewart based on needs and interest. On January 11, 2017, Rogers attended the first lunch along with other members of the nursing leadership team. At this meeting, the group reviewed and discussed the results of the Nurse Manager Skills Inventory survey. (Evidence TL6-9, Sign-in Sheet and Example of Nurse Manager Skills Inventory Tool and CE Certificates)

 

Mentoring Activity: Beaumont University Leadership Courses
Beaumont Health offers an array of leadership courses for anyone who is promoted or hired into a nurse leader role in the health system. Many leadership courses are available through BH’s continuing education department, Beaumont University, and they are offered at no cost to employees. Stewart suggested to Rogers that she should review the courses available and attend those that she thought were appropriate. The following are the Leadership Development courses offered to leaders in 2016:

  • 7 Habits of Highly Effective People
  • Becoming a More Effective Leader:  Understanding Your Management Style
  • Crucial Conversations
  • Emotional Intelligence
  • Employee Relations Skills
  • Influencer
  • Leadership Essentials
  • Managing Toxic and Disruptive Behaviors
  • The Attitude Angel
  • Overcoming The Five Dysfunctions of a Team for Leaders
  • Generations in the Workplace

 

On January 19, 2017, Stewart sponsored all BGP Nurse Managers, including Rogers, to attend a leadership conference, “Taking the Lead 2017: Leading Systems, Others and Self,” held in Ann Arbor, Michigan. Stewart recognized that this conference would be an opportunity to provide additional mentoring to Rogers and the Nurse Manager group. The agenda covered a variety of subjects that are pertinent to Nurse Managers, including “Adapting Systems of Care for Diverse Populations,” “The Importance of Leaders at the Front-line” and “The Emerging Role of the Clinical Nurse Leader.”
(Evidence TL6-10, Brochure Taking the Lead 2017: Leading Systems, Others and Self Conference and CE Certificate)

 

Through Stewart’s mentoring and support, Rogers was promoted to Administrative Manager of 3 South Orthopedic Unit.

 

Example Three: Mentoring for Nurse Leaders (Directors/Administrators)
Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, provides mentoring to all Nurse Directors through one-on-one counseling and discussions and by providing educational opportunities. Beaumont Grosse Pointe’s (BGP) Nursing Directors were included in the education, programs and leadership survey used to guide the development of the Nurse Managers. These leaders actively participated in the Advisory Board sessions to build the knowledge and skills needed to enhance their performance. Nurse Directors networked and collaborated throughout the sessions, and they obtained tools and techniques to maximize the effectiveness of their role.

 

Steve Apigo, BSN, RN, Director of Emergency Center, provides one example of Stewart’s mentoring and support for Directors. With Stewart’s guidance and support, Apigo participated in the Advisory Board sessions, the leadership conference and the Nurse Manager Skills Inventory session to facilitate mentoring. (Evidence TL6-11, CE Certificate from the Leadership Conference)

 

Mentoring Activity: Running an Effective Meeting
BGP’s nursing retreat, which Stewart hosts for a full day every December, provides another opportunity for leader and peer mentoring. In 2016, the retreat included a number of clinical nurses who function in the charge nurse role.

 

Based on goals that Stewart and Apigo had identified goals, she used the retreat to mentor him in effectively facilitating a large meeting attended by nurses from multiple clinical roles with varying needs. At the retreat, Apigo observed Stewart’s actions in her leadership role and how she interacted with nurses from all levels. (Evidence TL6-12, Agenda and Attendance Roster from Leadership Retreat)

 

Mentoring Activity: Director and CNO One-on-One Meeting and Mentoring
Stewart meets monthly with each nurse leader in one-on-one sessions. This structured time is an opportunity for the Nursing Director to receive coaching and mentorship from the CNO. When a new Director starts in the role, one of the first items on their competency checklist is a meeting with Stewart, the CNO, to discuss the Nursing Mission and goals and the Nursing Strategic Plan. (Evidence TL6-13, Additional Competencies for Nursing Leaders)

 

For those who are new to leadership, the one-on-one meetings are often held weekly or biweekly, instead of monthly, for the first 90-120 days. Stewart believes this protected one-on-one time is very important to the development of a skilled nurse leader. For example, Apigo was promoted from Assistant Nurse Manager (ANM) to Director after one year in that role. While Apigo is a very good leader, the role of Director brings new challenges that have required Stewart’s mentoring and support. Apigo is open to her guidance and seeks it regularly in and between these meetings.

 

One example of Stewart’s mentoring of Apigo comes from late March 2017, when an ANM from the Emergency Center sent an email to Stewart asking for premium pay for the employees of the department. Premium pay is a leadership strategy used to incentivize clinical nurses to work extra hours when the staffing level falls below 70% of required staffing. 

 

Stewart met with Apigo on March 30, 2017 to discuss the request for premium pay from an ANM in his department, but he was unaware of it. Stewart discussed with him the importance of consistent communication and chain of command, and she coached him on how to address the issue with the ANM and how to ensure that the information provided was accurate. She also suggested that Apigo and the ANMs meet with Randy Whitney, MSA, BSN, RN, NE-BE, Director of Professional Practice, to discuss staffing and budgeting since he is the leader who is most knowledgeable about these issues.  (Evidence TL6-14, Memo to Stewart from ANM and Memo to Whitney from Apigo) 

 

Mentoring Activity: Educational Opportunities
Nurse leaders also have many opportunities for growth, development and mentorship using the resources made available to them from outside as well as inside the health system. Each year, Stewart encourages nurse leaders to attend and actively participate in leadership conferences. 

 

With Stewart’s guidance and mentoring, Apigo has been able to accomplish the following in his short time in the Director role:

  • Improved patient flow out of the Emergency Center
  • Implemented the Unit Practice Council in the Emergency Center
  • Developing a plan for a Geriatric Emergency Center

(Evidence TL6-15 Apigo Evaluation)

 

Example Four: Mentoring for Chief Nursing Officer
Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RNC, NE-BC, Vice President and Chief Nursing Officer, has been a nurse for over 32 years, working at the Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) location since becoming a registered nurse. The hospital has come under new executive leadership four times during her career, and she has been offered new and different opportunities with each change. One of the most significant changes in her career came when the hospital was acquired by the Beaumont Health System in 2008, followed by the merger of three hospital systems to become Beaumont Health.

 

In 2008, Beaumont Health System acquired the Grosse Pointe site. Stewart was Nurse Manager of the LDRP unit, and in the years following the acquisition she was provided nursing leadership education and growth opportunities that she had not been afforded before. In 2011, Stewart became a Director of Nursing and was ultimately asked to be the interim CNO when the Vice President/CNO took a leave of absence in May 2013. The CNO did not return, and the Vice President/CNO position was posted in February 2014. Stewart applied, interviewed and was appointed CNO for Beaumont Grosse Pointe in April 2014.

 

In 2015, Beaumont Health System merged with the Oakwood Health System and Botsford Hospital to become Beaumont Health (BH). This change brought Stewart new and exciting growth and mentoring opportunities. 

 

Mentoring Activity: System Lead for Staffing and Scheduling
During the initial transition to Beaumont Health, Stewart was part of a nursing leadership team, comprising the CNOs from all eight hospitals, that worked on a variety of transition projects. Maureen Bowman, MA, BSN, RN, NEA-BC, interim Beaumont System CNO, and Barbara Medvec, MSN, MSA, RN, NEA-BC, interim Oakwood System CNO, recommended that Stewart become the CNO sponsor/facilitator for the BH Staffing and Scheduling team. This team comprised clinical nurse and nurse leader representatives from the eight hospitals.

 

Bowman and Medvec mentored Stewart during the first few BH meetings. The task of the Staffing and Scheduling team was to evaluate the current state of staffing and scheduling programs at all eight sites. The focus was to identify similarities and differences in staffing and scheduling processes and programs as well as contingent staff models. (Evidence TL6-16, Minutes for Staffing and Scheduling Taskforce)

 

Mentoring Activity: CNO Academy
When Susan Grant, DNP, RN NEA-BC, FAAN, started her role as the Executive Vice President and CNO for BH in December 2015, Stewart’s growth and mentoring opportunities quickly increased as Grant was very attentive to the mentoring needs of her new leadership team. For example, she provided an opportunity for any of the system CNOs to attend the CNO Academy sponsored by AMN Healthcare in March 2016 in Chicago, Illinois. Stewart and three other CNOs from BH attended the conference. This was Stewart’s first opportunity to attend a conference of this caliber and an education offering on developing the role of the CNO. Another benefit of this gathering was that it enabled Stewart to spend time and develop relationships with the three CNOs from the other health systems. This provided additional mentoring opportunities, as Stewart could now reach out to these CNOs when questions arose.  Topics covered during the CNO Academy included “CNO, as the Architect: Designing the Big Picture,” “The Power of You, Self-Knowledge” and other nursing executive topics. 

 

Stewart was also assigned a personal mentor from AMN Healthcare, Joyce Batcheller, DNP, RN, NEA-BC, FAAN, whom Stewart had regular contact with during the next 12 months for mentoring in her new role as CNO. (Evidence TL6-17, Agenda, CE Certification for the CNO Academy and Email from Batcheller)

 

Mentoring Activity: Integration of Beaumont Health
As the work of this major merger of systems continued, Grant offered the CNOs many opportunities to be involved in a number of projects. Stewart’s interest in Human Resources proved to be valuable as the work of pulling together the benefits and compensation programs of three very different organizations began.

 

In spring 2016, Stewart and her colleague Diane Hartley, MSN, RN Chief Nursing Officer, Beaumont Wayne, were appointed the CNO representatives on the benefit work group. Stewart and Hartley attended many intense sessions of the benefit harmonization team and provided the voice of nursing in these meetings. In addition to representing nursing, they needed to bring the discussions back to the BH Nurse Executive Council so their CNO colleagues knew what was being discussed. Grant had facilitated mentoring activities for Stewart by placing her on the benefit harmonization team, and she also arranged mentoring for Stewart from Paul Conway, MBA, Senior Vice President and Chief Human Resource Officer. Conway helped Stewart gain knowledge of and exposure to issues related to harmonizing the benefits of the three founding organizations for the 40,000 employees of Beaumont Health.

 

The benefit harmonization committee met regularly to review and discuss subjects such as medical, pharmacy, vision, disability and time off programs. Although these three organizations were in the same market, they had very diverse programs. The team worked with a consultant, Willis, Towers, Watson, on this project, and Stewart was exposed to many new ways of looking at data, the significance of having the right data and examining it from many viewpoints.  As the group reviewed the current state and the harmonized state, it was clear that the blending of benefits was going to be a significant change for everyone. Stewart was also included in the Executive Leadership Team meetings as these benefit decisions were discussed. (Evidence TL6-18, PS Collaboration Session May 4, 2016 and Handout)

 

Another opportunity that Grant encouraged was Stewart’s involvement in the Corporate Enterprise Risk Management Committee, giving Stewart exposure to an area in which she had no prior experience. The Enterprise Risk Management Committee was formed to identify any potential risks to the corporation such as internal or external risks to the electronic medical record. Grant suggested to Stewart during one of their conversations that this committee would give her an opportunity to expand her knowledge base of issues outside nursing.    

 

Mentoring Activity: CNO One-on-One with System CNO
Stewart also engages in monthly one-on-one meetings with Grant in which she updates Grant on the status of nursing at BGP and seeks Grant’s opinions and guidance. As Grant is the first system CNO whom Stewart has worked with, this time has been invaluable. The challenges at BGP are often not unique, and Grant readily offers her experiences, advice and support to Stewart as a mentor. Based on their one-on-one meetings, Grant believed Stewart would be an appropriate speaker for the national conference of the Institute for Patient- and Family-Centered Care (IPFCC). One of the speakers could not attend because of an emergency, so Grant suggested to Beverley Johnson, President and CEO for IPFCC, that Stewart would be a great replacement. (Evidence TL6-19, Calendar of Stewart and Grant’s Appointment, and Memo Regarding Presentation Opportunity)

 

Another mentoring opportunity that Grant offered Stewart was involvement in a second-level review of an incident that occurred at another BH hospital. The CNO and Hospital President of that facility could not agree on the outcome of a significant situation involving a nurse and a patient. There had been many meetings between the CNO, Hospital President, Human Resources and the legal department on the case, and as they were at a standstill the case was escalated to Grant. Instead of providing her opinion, Grant asked that the group get together and include her and another neutral CNO to discuss this matter. Grant asked Stewart to be the CNO representative. 

 

On August 31, 2016, the team met and discussed the case. Present at the meeting were the CNO from BH Hospital-A, the President from BH Hospital-A, Grant and Stewart, who was the neutral CNO. The goal was to have two parties (Grant and Stewart) who were not involved in the incident review the data and render an opinion. This was the first second-level review by the System CNO and another CNO done at BH.

 

Following the BH merger, Stewart approached Grant about the future of the Staffing and Scheduling committee, of which Stewart had been the CNO sponsor. The committee was on hold due to competing priorities. Grant asked Stewart to not only continue as the CNO sponsor for the team, but to be the single source for the Staffing and Scheduling vendor, McKesson. Per Grant’s request, McKesson includes Stewart in all communications with any of the BH hospitals. (Evidence TL6-20, Calendar and Email regarding Case; September 19, 16 Email from Grant regarding McKesson)

 

Stewart continues to develop in her role of CNO of BGP, supported by the mentoring opportunities provided by Grant and Beaumont Health.
