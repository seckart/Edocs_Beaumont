Innovation NK4EO

 

Innovation in nursing is supported and encouraged.

  • Provide two examples, with supporting evidence, of an improvement that resulted from an innovation in nursing. Supporting evidence must be submitted in the form of a graph with a data table that clearly displays the data.

 

 

Example One: An Innovative Way to Decrease Falls on 3 West – All Hands On Deck

 

Background/Problem
At Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP), a Quality Care Patient Care (QCPC) meeting is held on the fourth Monday of every month with the inpatient nursing leaders. Patricia Ackley, BSN, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, 3 West, and Molly Brieden, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, 3 West, were in attendance when the quarterly data was presented and discussed. Total patient falls per 1,000 patient days was discussed because falls can cause serious harm to patients, increase patients’ length of stay, increase cost and affect patient satisfaction. BGP takes falls seriously because of their negative consequences.

 

At the March 2015 QCPC meeting, the NDNQI fall data for fourth quarter 2014 was reviewed, and it was determined that 3 West had a high fall rate. 3 West is a medical/surgical unit that focuses on GI/GU disorders. The data revealed that the unit’s fall rate for fourth quarter 2014 was 4.12 (total falls per 1,000 patient days). It was apparent that 3 West nurses would need to develop an action plan to decrease the number of falls on the unit.

 

The 3 West patient population tends to be older, which brings numerous comorbidities that can contribute to the effects of Sundowner’s Syndrome, which increases the likelihood of falls. Sundowner's Syndrome is the name given to an ailment that causes symptoms of confusion after “sundown.” These symptoms often appear in people who suffer from Alzheimer's disease or other forms of dementia. 3 West takes many postoperative GI/GU patients, so postoperative confusion can be another factor in patient falls. These two factors alone can put this patient population at high risk for falls.

 

When a patient is at high risk for falls, the clinical nurse requests a patient safety sitter from the Nursing Business Office (NBO). A safety sitter is an employee who will sit at the patient’s bedside and assist them with any daily needs that might put them at risk for falls, especially toileting.

 

To request a sitter, the clinical nurse assesses the patient and completes a sitter justification form to fax to the NBO. The form requires the following information:

  • Demographics
  • Indicators for Restraints: Safety, Behavioral, Suicide, Physician Order
  • Authorization from Leader

 

The NBO plans for and attempts to secure staffing for patient safety sitters to fill the unit’s needs to ensure patient safety which can, in turn, result in less falls. The process for requesting a patient safety sitter must be gone through every shift. If the NBO is not able to secure a sitter during the middle of the shift, it notifies the charge nurse and the staff checks on the patient frequently.

 

If the sitter was unavailable for the entire shift, it would be determined at the beginning of the shift whether a nursing care assistant (NCA) could be pulled out of staffing to sit with the patient. However, pulling an NCA out of staffing shorted the unit and potentially put other patients at risk of falling. The charge nurse was therefore put in the position of deciding which action to take, pulling the NCA to be a safety sitter or continuing with the NCA in the staffing mix. Given this situation, 3 West recognized the need for change.

 

Goal Statement
To decrease the total patient fall rate on 3 West, measured as total falls per 1,000 patient days.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
In April 2015, Brieden and Ackley discussed the fall data with the 3 West staff during daily huddles and individually with the clinical nurses and NCAs. 

 

An innovative idea was brought forth during a morning safety huddle by Maxine Griffith, BSN, RN, clinical nurse, 3 West. The morning safety huddle is a short meeting led by Ackley and Rowena Angeles, RN, Care Manager, in which the staff is debriefed on any safety issues on the unit.

 

The goal of Griffith’s innovative idea was to reduce patient falls while keeping the NCA in the staffing mix to prevent other patients from falling. Griffith suggested that, instead of the current practice of pulling a staff member off their patient assignment to have them sit with the patient, the patient safety sitter responsibilities would be divided among all the clinical nurses and NCAs working on the unit that shift. Each staff member would then take 30 minute turns sitting with the patient to help prevent falls.  Griffith had read about a similar approach to covering safety sitters when an NCA was not available. Her proposal innovated on this concept by ensuring that all nursing staff members take a turn siting with patients, thereby raising their awareness of the importance of ensuring patient safety. This plan promoted safety for all patients on the unit by ensuring adequate nursing staff to provide care.    

 

Research demonstrates that if all staff members are aware of a safety issue such as patients at high risk for falls, the less likely it is the patient will fall. The innovative project was termed the “All Hands On Deck” (AHOD) approach to reducing the number of falls on the unit.

 

In July 2015, after the All Hands on Deck process had been developed, Brieden and Ackley decided to pilot it for 30 days on 3 West to determine its impact on the unit’s patient fall rate according to the NDNQI data. Brieden developed a form for assigning the staff members’ scheduled time to sit with the patient and for identifying who their coverage is for the time they are sitting.

 

The form has a large clock on the top half to show when staff members are scheduled to sit and an area on the bottom half to designate staff coverage. It was decided that each staff member would be assigned a 30-minute slot in which they are expected to watch the patient to keep them from falling.

 

While the staff member sitting, another is assigned to assume the care of the sitter’s patient set. It was the responsibility of the outgoing shift’s charge nurse to fill out the AHOD clock and make out the coverage schedule for the on-coming shift. The schedule was to be made out at the beginning of the shift so the clinical nurses and NCAs could manage their time accordingly.

 

The expectation was that the staff sitter would not leave the patient’s bedside until the next person was there to relieve them of their duty; there would then not be a moment without a staff member at the bedside. To remind staff members of the time they were scheduled to sit, the clinical assistant was given a copy of the AHOD and the form was placed in the nursing stations, the break room and the patient room.

The program was fully implemented on 3 West by the end of third quarter 2015.

Outcomes

Because of the success of the pilot, the rest of the BGP nursing staff on all units were educated on AHOD during staff meetings and one-on-one conversations with the unit managers. The clinical assistants were given the form and educated on the process at the beginning of their shift on the unit to begin the rollout.

 

 

Example Two: Innovative Nursing Care in the Treatment of Sickle Cell Patients

 

Background/Problem
Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) is close to the urban area of Detroit, the population of which is predominantly African American. Many of the hospitals in the city of Detroit either closed or were converted to clinics in early 2013, which has led to an increase in the number of sickle cell patients being seen in the BGP Emergency Center.

 

At this same time, a lack of funding resulted in the closing of a number of pain clinics that had been crucial in managing the needs and medications of the sickle cell population. Because of this sudden lack of support within the City of Detroit, many patients traveled to surrounding hospitals for sickle cell symptom management.

 

In fourth quarter 2013, the BGP Emergency Center began receiving an influx of these young, very sick sickle cell patients. Although the symptoms of sickle cell disease are more easily managed using a consistent proactive approach, many patients found themselves with a lack of resources to do so. The closing of the clinics meant that many physicians were no longer available for this vulnerable patient population, so many patients who had been managed at the clinics were now finding themselves in full blown sickle cell crises necessitating emergent care. 

 

The physicians and nurses in the Emergency Center found themselves in unfamiliar territory, as most sickle cell patients had previously been seen in an outpatient setting. The patients coming to the Emergency Center were typically in sickle cell crisis, with the need for hydration and opioids frequently requiring inpatient admission. The patients were routinely admitted to the hospitalist on call, with no consistency in physician coverage. The patient would be assigned to the physician who was on call, and not necessarily to the physician who took care of them during their last admission. In addition, when sickle cell patients were admitted they were placed randomly in whichever unit had a ready bed, resulting in a lack of continuity of nursing care When the patient was ready for discharge, either from the Emergency Center or from the inpatient setting, they were being discharged with no primary care provider (PCP).  BGP would give the patients a referral to a PCP, but many did not follow up with a PCP because due to a lack of resources such as insurance and transportation. 

 

Because of the inconsistency in care providers and the lack of outpatient resources, this population was not managed across the continuum as effectively and efficiently as was desired. This resulted in an increase in the 30-day readmission rate for this population.  During fourth quarter 2013, BGP had one of the highest readmission rates for sickle cell patients for hospitals in the University Health System Consortium (UHC) (now Vizient) database. BGP’s fourth quarter 2013 30-day readmission rate for sickle cell patients was 27.7%.

 

Goal Statement
To decrease the readmission rate for sickle cell patients, as measured by 30-day readmissions.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
In January 2014, a Sickle Cell Subcommittee was formed. This was a nurse-driven effort to take the lead in determining how to best handle this patient population. The members of the subcommittee were nurse leaders, infusion nurses, oncology navigators, palliative care nurses, educators, care managers and administration members. 

 

2 South Assistant Nurse Managers Pam Lively, BSN, RN and Kim Little, BSN, RN agreed to champion this cause because these patients had many needs, including the voice of the nurse to advocate for them. Lively performed a literature review and learned that there was no nursing evidence-based practice for decreasing the 30-day readmission rate for sickle cell patients.

 

In February 2014, Lively and Little reached out to Tom Sullivan, RPh, Director of Pharmacy, and James Cherry, PharmD, MS, Assistant Director of Pharmacy, to gain insight into this issue. Sullivan informed them that the Pharmacy Clinical Integration Council (CIC) and the Pain CIC had been working concurrently to develop and implement an innovative Sickle Cell Order Set. The purpose of the Sickle Cell Order Set was to provide consistent care across the Beaumont Health System to ensure that all patients with sickle cell disease were receiving adequate relief for their pain.

 

The tool was available but had not been introduced or promoted to the medical staff. It was apparent that it was of little value unless it was consistently used.

 

Innovative Approach to the Care and Management of Sickle Cell Patients
During a March 2014 meeting of the Sickle Cell Subcommittee, Lively and Little requested that all patients with the primary diagnosis of sickle cell crisis be admitted to 2 South. This was an innovative and unusual proposal. Lively, in discussions with other area hospitals and hospitals within Beaumont Health System, identified that these patients were often not cohorted on one unit due to the management issues involved with this population The complexity of this chronic disease process and the need for increasing doses of opioids for pain management often presents challenges for nurses to provide the compassionate nursing care that had drawn them to the profession. However, Lively recognized that cohorting these patients on one unit would allow nurses to develop their expertise in the care of this patient population and would also allow the full implementation of the new Sickle Order Set.  

 

To support the care of this patient population, the Sickle Cell Order Set was introduced to the nurses on 2 South through staff meetings and one-on-one discussions with Lively and Little in March 2014. The clinical nurses were coached to contact the admitting physicians to educate them on and encourage the use of this order set. The nurses were also encouraged to provide copies of the order set to the patients as a tool to educate them and involve them in the management of their disease. Lively believed this would also strengthen the nurse-patient relationship.

 

In late March 2014, Lively, Little and the Sickle Cell Subcommittee determined that a physician champion would be helpful to better facilitate the management of this population. Alaa Mansour, MD was contacted and agreed to be the physician champion. All sickle cell patients who presented to the Emergency Center without a primary care physician would be admitted to Mansour. 

 

In April 2014, Sandra Rizzo, MSN, RN, CEN, Clinical Nurse Specialist, Emergency Center and member of the Sickle Cell Subcommittee, with the help of Glen Clark, MD, Director of Emergency Center, began a campaign to improve the care and management of sickle cell patients in the Emergency Center. Clark assumed responsibility for educating Emergency Center physicians on the order set for inpatient admissions. Rizzo was proactive in researching sickle cell disease and the nursing care of these patients. She presented her findings through a slide show to the entire Emergency Center staff, using a post test for clinical nurses to evaluate the effectiveness of the presentation. This information was then shared with the staff on 2 South.

 

In April 2014, Charlotta Marion, RN, Assistant Nurse Manager, Emergency Center, in collaboration with the Informatics Department, developed a report to track patients with a primary diagnosis of sickle cell crisis presenting to the EC and being admitted from the EC to the inpatient unit. This report was sent daily to Marion, Lively and Little, who were able to determine that the patients, if admitted, had been placed appropriately on 2 South, that they had been assigned to Mansour and that the order set was in use. Lively or Little subsequently followed up to guarantee this consistent approach.

 

By the end of second quarter, 2014, the innovative approach to the care and management of sickle cell patients was fully implemented. The comprehensive program included:

  • All patients without a PCP being assigned to Mansour
  • All patients being admitted to one unit, 2 South
  • The Sickle Cell Order Set being initiated on all patients
  • Consistent nursing staff

Outcomes
Following the concentrated efforts of the Sickle Cell Subcommittee and the innovative approach to the care and management of this patient population, the 30-day readmission rate for sickle cell patients decreased from 27.7% in fourth quarter 2013 to 9.1% in first quarter 2015.
