Research NK2

 

Nurses disseminate the organization's nursing research findings to internal and external audiences.

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of how clinical nurses disseminated to internal audiences knowledge obtained through the organization's nursing research.
    AND

  • Provide one example, with supporting evidence, of how clinical nurses disseminated to external audiences knowledge obtained through the organization’s nursing research.

 

 

 

Introduction:"Evaluating Registered Nurses' Knowledge Regarding Evidence-Based Practice in Medical Surgical Nurses in a Community Hospital"

 

Evidence-based practice (EBP) is a problem-solving approach to clinical decision making that incorporates a search for the best and latest evidence, clinical expertise and assessment, and patient preference values into the context of caring.

 

In October 2016, after obtaining the Beaumont Health System Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval, a 29-question survey was administered to medical/surgical nurses on 2 South, 3 South and 3 West.

 

The study, titled “Evaluating Registered Nurses’ Knowledge regarding Evidence-Based Practice in Medical Surgical Nurses in a Community Hospital,” was conducted by Randy Whitney, MSA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Director of Professional Practice as the Primary Investigator (PI), and Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Chief Nursing Officer, as the Co-Primary Investigator. (Evidence NK2-1, IRB Approval Letter for Research # 2016-343)

 

The purpose of the study was to evaluate the EBP knowledge of nurses on three medical/surgical units at Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP). The researchers were trying to determine whether an active unit practice council (UPC) increased nurses’ knowledge of EBP. The UPCs are the shared governance structure at Beaumont Grosse Pointe.

 

The hypothesis was based on the researchers’ knowledge of the three units and the activities of the UPCs. It was anticipated that 3 West would score the highest, followed by 2 South, and that 3 South would score the lowest.

 

The study measured five factors related to nurses’ knowledge of EBP:

  • Organization Culture
  • Unit Culture
  • Knowledge and Skill of Nurse
  • Time
  • Attitude

 

As anticipated, the unit that scored the highest was 3 West, which has the most active UPC.  The unit with the next highest score was 3 South, which has the least active UPC. The surprising results were from 2 South, which had the lowest score; this result was not anticipated by the PI or Co-PI. (Evidence NK2-2, Results of the Research Study)

 

Knowledge Obtained
The study’s hypothesis was partially met. In the research proposal, it was hypothesized that an active UPC increased nurses’ knowledge of EBP. This proved accurate on 3 West, which scored the highest in all five categories and has the most active UPC. This UPC conducts a Journal Club for nurses to review journal articles for the most current practice in a medical/surgical unit. 

 

The researchers also determined that the age and experience of the clinical nurse plays an important role in the knowledge of EBP. 2 South, with the second most active UPC, scored the lowest on the survey. A review of the data showed that a higher percentage of 2 South nurses were age 41 or older: 51.9% on 2 south, compared to 37.1% on 3 West and 23.1% on 3 South. The experience level of clinical nurses might also play a role: 33.3% of 2 South clinical nurses had 20 or more years of experience, compared to 7.4% on 3 West and 7.7% on 3 South.

The researchers believed the age and experience of the nurses may have contributed to the lower scores on 2 South, as EBP was most likely not studied when these clinical nurses completed basic nursing school.

 

The researchers also identified in the results that clinical nurses cited time as a major issue in their knowledge of EBP, as the “Time” factor received the lowest score in all three units. The two Time questions in the survey were:

  • Nurses have enough time on the job to implement EBP findings
  • I could find one hour per week on the job for EBP activities, if I made it a priority

The researchers also identified that the unit culture is more supportive of EBP than the organizational culture. These results demonstrated that the hospital must do more to encourage and support EBP. This might be related to the issue of time: If clinical nurses do not have enough time to complete EBP activities, this might be interpreted as the organization not supporting EBP.

Clinical Nurses’ Dissemination of the Knowledge Obtained
The results were discussed with Alexis Rogers, BSN, RN, ONC, Administrative Manager, 3 South, and Pam Lively, BSN, RN, NE-BDC, Administrative Manager, 2 South, both of whom believed that these units’ clinical nurses should own the results and develop a plan to correct any deficiencies.

 

The researchers presented the study findings to the UPC on 2 South and to Team Leader Julie Lynch, BSN, RN, clinical nurse on 3 South. The researchers believed the units should own the results, disseminate the information to their peers on the unit and present the results and plans for improvement to other professionals.

 

The results were presented internally by 2 South clinical nurses Emily Gross, BSN, RN; Michael Hoekstra, BSN, RN; and Timothy Pierce, BSN, RN to the Professional Nurse Council on May 10, 2017, and externally by Julie Lynch, BSN, RN, 3 South Charge Nurse, at a conference titled, “Taking the Lead 2017: Leading Systems, Others and Self.”

 

UPC clinical nurses were supported by the researchers (Whitney and Stewart) in reviewing the data. Deb Briske, MSN, RN, CEN, 2 South Clinical Nurse Specialist, supported the groups with the presentations, and BGP supported the group financially with the costs related to these presentations.

Randy Whitney, MSA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Director of Professional Practice and PI of the research study, reviewed the demographics of the study, including sex, age, education level, years as a registered nurse, years at Beaumont, employment status, shift worked, position and current education activities. Deb Briske, MSN, RN, CEN, Clinical Nurse Specialist for 2 South, assisted the 2 South UPC members in developing their presentation through which they disseminated the information obtained from the research study. (Evidence NK2-3, Agenda of UPC)

 

Based on the data, the UPC decided to focus on the Unit Culture and improve the clinical nurses’ knowledge regarding EBP. They agreed with the conclusion of the nurse researchers that, because of the age and experience of the 2 South nurses, EBP had not been taught when these nurses completed a nursing education program. 

 

Internal Dissemination of the Knowledge Obtained
The 2 South UPC members presented the results of the research study completed on 2 South at the hospital Professional Nurse Council (PNC) meeting on May 10, 2017, during BGP’s celebration of Nurses Week. The PNC, which is a council of BGP’s shared governance model, consists of clinical nurses from all areas where nursing is practiced and three nurse leaders who serve as advisors.

 

Gross, Hoekstra and Pierce presented the key findings of the research study on 2 South. 2 South UPC clinical nurses shared with the PNC future plans for 2 South, including developing a bulletin board for EBP articles to be posted, possibly starting a journal club, providing ongoing education on EBP and repeating the study in six to 12 months to determine whether the education was beneficial. (Evidence NK2-4, Agenda and Minutes from May 10, 2017 PNC Meeting; Brochure and Handout from 2 South)

 

The results of the research that was completed on 2 South and the plan for how to improve clinical nurses’ knowledge regarding EBP was well received by the PNC and other nursing staff members. More than 25 people were in attendance as 2 South disseminated the research findings to this internal audience. (Evidence NK2-5, Sign-in Sheet for PNC Meeting)

 

Example Two: Clinical Nurse Dissemination to an External Audience – “Taking the Lead 2017: Leading Systems, Others and Self”

In late November 2016, the preliminary results of the survey were presented to Alexis Rogers, BSN, RN, 3 South Administrative Manager, and Julie Lynch, BSN, RN, 3 South Team Leader, by Randy Whitney, MSA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Director of Professional Practice and PI of the research study.

 

Lynch had been extremely interested to see the results of the survey, as she believed 3 South was lacking in knowledge of EBP and was interested in assisting her peers in improving their knowledge.  

 

One of the purposes of the study was to determine the nurses’ knowledge regarding EBP and to use the data to formulate an action plan to increase the knowledge and awareness of EBP in the 3 South Orthopaedic Unit.

 

Knowledge Obtained
Lynch reviewed with Rogers the results of the survey, which indicated that the clinical nurses on 3 South believed that time and knowledge and skills were factors in their knowledge of EBP. The results indicated that there was no time for and little knowledge of EBP activities on 3 South.

 

Based on the results, Lynch, with Rogers’ assistance, implemented five activities that would improve 3 South clinical nurses’ knowledge of EBP.

  • Development of a Resource Room – This was a room designated on the unit as a lounge where clinical nurses could unwind and review educational materials.
  • Development of a Journal Club –  This would be designed to review journal articles that support the concepts of EBP.
  • Development of a Book Shelf – 3 South clinical nurses were encouraged to bring in gently used current journals and textbooks for other staff members to read.
  • Implementation of Education Huddles – These were 2-3 minute huddles to discuss any educational topic.
  • Encouragement for nurses on the unit to complete a BSN degree.

 

Lynch shared these strategies with Anne Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC, Chief Nursing Officer and Co-PI of the research study, who said these were excellent ideas and suggested to Lynch that she meet with Whitney to explore ways to disseminate this information outside of BGP.

 

External Dissemination of the Knowledge Obtained
Stewart reviewed a call for poster abstracts for the conference “Taking the Lead 2017: Leading Systems, Others and Self”, and she shared the information with Lynch. The conference was held on January 16, 2017 at the Kensington Court Hotel in Ann Arbor, Michigan. The sponsors of the event were the Institute of Professional Nursing, Michigan League for Nursing, Beaumont Health and the University of Michigan Health System. (Evidence NK2-6, Call for Abstracts and Conference Brochure)

 

Lynch submitted an abstract as the lead presenter, with the PI and Co-PI of the research study as additional authors. On December 12, 2016, Lynch, as well as Whitney and Stewart, were notified that the poster was accepted for presentation at the “Taking the Lead 2017: Leading Systems, Others and Self” conference. (Evidence NK2-7, Abstract and Acceptance Letter)

 

Lynch presented the findings at the conference through a poster. More than 300 nurses from various hospitals in the metropolitan Detroit area attended the conference, along with people from the local universities. (Evidence NK2-8, Handouts and CE Certificate)

 

The poster was well received at the conference and generated discussion regarding the conclusion that was presented. The conclusion was:

“The nurses in this study demonstrated a similar level of knowledge of Evidence-Based Practice on two of the three units, with different demographic data. 3 South has the highest percentage of inexperienced nurses and the lowest percent of BSN nurses but remained in the median of the 5 factors assessed”.
(Evidence NK2-9, Copy of Poster Presentation)
