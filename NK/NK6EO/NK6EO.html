Example One: Emergency Center Work Flow Improvement for Clinical Efficiency – Decreasing the Percentage of Patients Who Leave Without Being Seen

 

Background/Problem
Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) is a community teaching hospital with a 34-bed Emergency Center (EC) that sees approximately 40,000 patients annually. Six of the 34 beds were designated as Fast Track area beds, used during peak hours and staffed by an RN and mid-level provider (MLP) who saw patients with minor conditions. The many exclusion criteria for the Fast Track area, in addition to the presenting complaint, included the patient’s age, medical history and ambulatory status.

 

It was clear to nurse leaders Steve Apigo, BSN, RN, Director of Emergency Center, and Charlotta Marion, BSN, RN, Associate Nurse Manager, that the six beds in the Fast Track area were being underutilized much of the time, and on most days were empty. With a steady increase in patient volumes, over 20% from 2012 to 2015, the EC experienced an increase in the number and percent of patients who left without being seen (LWOBS). Patients who leave the EC before seeing a medical provider typically do so because of long waits. According to national benchmarks, the LWOBS rate should be below 2%. Nurse leaders knew that decreasing the LWOBS rate would require decreasing the amount of time it takes for a patient to see a medical provider and begin treatment.

 

Apigo and Marion solicited suggestions from the Emergency Center team of nurses and physicians about how to adjust work flow to decrease wait times, with the goal of decreasing the LWOBS rate and thereby improving clinical efficiency. 

 

In February 2015, the work flow for patients going to Fast Track and not requiring emergent care was as follows:

  • The patient arrived and was greeted by an emergency technician or nurse.
  • The patient was asked the reason for the visit.
  • Vital signs were taken.
  • Registration was completed.

 

At this point, if a treatment room was available:

  • The patient was brought to the treatment room where they were assigned a primary nurse.
  • The patient waited to be examined by the physician or MLP, and orders were entered in the electronic medical record (EMR).
  • The nurse or technician acknowledged the orders in the EMR and carried them out. How fast the orders were carried out depended on a number of factors, including patient volume and staff availability.

 

When all the treatment rooms were full, the patient was sent to the waiting room after the initial quick registration and triage to wait for the next available room. This wait time was also dependent on volumes and available staff and resources.  

 

Advance Treatment Guidelines (ATG) are corporate policies, guidelines and standing orders for nurses to begin some diagnostic testing and treatments based on presenting symptoms, prior to the provider seeing the patient. The ATGs were developed to improve throughput times in the EC and are used by nurses to move patients more efficiently through the department.

 

However, the ATGs were not being consistently used by EC clinical nurses for a variety of reasons, such as duplication of orders, physicians not supporting the ATGs and nurses’ lack of confidence in using them. Apigo and Marion believed that use of the ATGs was the key to successfully decreasing the LWOBS rate. The challenge was how to improve the work flow so nurses would feel empowered to use the ATGs consistently.

 

Apigo and Marion held discussions with the EC staff, including clinical nurses, charge nurses, physicians and educators. The consensus of the EC staff was that a more consistent use of ATGs would be the best way to decrease wait times and thereby improve clinical efficiency, as measured by a decrease in the LWOBS rate. The challenge was to develop a work flow process to ensure the ease and consistent use of ATGs by clinical nurses.

 

In March 2015, the Emergency Center’s LWOBS rate was 3.54%, above the national benchmark of 2%. 

 

Goal Statement
To decrease the rate of patients who left without being seen (LWOBS) in the Emergency Center.

 

Description of the Intervention/Initiative/Activities
April 2015–June 2015
Apigo, Marion and Brad O’Shell, BSN, RN, Associate Nurse Manager, completed an assessment of department work flow processes to identify areas for improvement. Various benchmarks and data were reviewed, including:

  • Door to physician
  • Door to diagnostics
  • Door to admit
  • Left without being seen

 

Marion, O’Shell and Sandra Rizzo, MSN, RN CEN, Clinical Nurse Specialist, completed the following to gather information about the work flow design of the Emergency Center:

  • A review of the literature to gather information for process improvement
  • A review of the Beaumont Health Emergency Centers, including their physical layout, the patient flow process and how patients are admitted to the department.

 

July 2015: Work Flow Committee
A Work Flow Committee was developed to find ways to improve the work flow in the area. The committee included nursing leadership, charge nurses and volunteers from the clinical nursing staff. It was also determined that physicians should be part of the work flow design.

 

After assessing the current work flow, identifying areas of inefficiencies and discussing options with clinical nurses, charge nurses, physicians and educators, the Work Flow Committee decided to convert the Fast Track area of the EC to a hybrid space. This area would be used for patient care, treatment and diagnostics, using ATGs, before the patient is seen by the physician. The Emergency Center Work Flow Committee, led by Marion, developed a plan with an algorithm of the new work flow.

 

August 2015–September 2015: Design
The Work Flow Committee’s goal was to redesign the area to improve work flow. Based on input from the committee, the following work flow changes were made to improve efficiency: 

  • Necessary supplies and equipment were moved to the area, with clinical nurse input into how the room was to be arranged and stocked for optimal work flow.
  • The area was to be staffed by three to four RNs and EC technicians.
  • All patients with an Emergency Severity Index (ESI) score of 3, 4 or 5 would begin the Emergency Department visit in the front end process.
  • The area was reconfigured so that patients who are ambulatory, wheelchair bound or on a stretcher would receive a quick triage, nursing assessment and appropriate diagnostic testing (ATGs), based on presenting symptoms.
  • The patient would then be transported to the main Emergency Department, where care would be handed off to a primary nurse.

 

October 2015: Education
All staff members were educated on the new work flow design through change of shift huddles, staff meetings, one-to-one meetings and emails from Marion, with the goal of beginning the program in November 2015.

 

November 2015–January 2016: Implementation  
The new process began in November 2015. The implementation phase was given to the charge nurses, who were available 24 hours a day, seven days a week to assist with supplemental education and problem solving. The following charge nurses were responsible for ensuring the process ran smoothly:

  • Brooke Meier, RN
  • Beverly Gandolfi, RN
  • Joan Shimko, BSN, RN, CEN
  • Thomas Difilippo, BSN, RN
  • Sundare Ganti, RN
  • Anna Welch, BSN, RN
  • Kristen Skoney, RN

 

Although there was some resistance from the clinical nurses, the EC leadership team decided that no changes would be made during the first three months, giving time to accurately assess the strengths and weaknesses of the change.

February 2016–March 2016
The process was evaluated after the three-month implementation period, November 2015 through January 2016, with Marion gathering data such as LWOBS rates and nursing staff input. The LWOBS rate was decreased, with fewer patients leaving the EC prior to evaluation. Feedback from clinical nurses included several suggestions for changes to the work flow, with the following suggestions implemented by Marion and O’Shell to improve the work flow of the EC:

  • Perform vital signs upon entry rather than in the front end process.
  • Provide additional equipment such as computers on wheels and vital sign machines.
  • An additional RN was needed at the greeter desk acting as a Triage Care Coordinator.
  • The entire Fast Track area should be used for the new work flow, rather than just a portion of it, which was in the original plan.
  • Patients with a lower acuity (ESI 4 or 5), conditions not requiring them to disrobe and conditions for which lab work is not expected to be necessary, would be placed in designated hallway spaces in the main Emergency Department during times of overcrowding.

 

By March 2016, all work flow improvements were fully implemented.

Outcomes

Following the interventions to improve clinical efficiency through the EC Work Flow Committee’s design, the rate of patients who left without being seen decreased from 3.54% in March 2015 to 1.10% in June 2016. This improvement brought the rate to well below the national average of 2%.
