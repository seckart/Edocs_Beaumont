INTRODUCTION  

 

“Evaluating Registered Nurses’ Knowledge regarding Evidence-based Practice in Medical Surgical Nurses in a Community Hospital”

 

Primary Investigator: Randy G. Whitney, MSA, BSN, RN, NE-BC
Co-Primary Investigator: Anne M. Stewart, MBA, BSN, RN, NE-BC
Study Participants: Clinical Nurses – 2 South, 3 South and 3 West at
Beaumont Grosse Pointe

 

Research Question and Hypothesis
Research Question: Do medical surgical nurses who have a strong Unit Practice Council have greater knowledge regarding evidence-based practice than those nurses who have a weaker Unit Practice Council?

Hypothesis: Units with stronger Unit Practice Councils will have greater knowledge of evidence-based practice.

 

Study Rationale
Beaumont Grosse Pointe (BGP) has three medical surgical units, all of which have Unit Practice Councils (UPC) at various levels of development. The UPCs are the shared governance model that is used at BGP. The UPCs meet once a month to plan activities that support the unit. They review issues such as patient care issues, education needs and general unit operations.  These are staff-led councils that meet independent of nursing leadership. 

 

The 2 South UPC is an active council that meets monthly and has input into the operation of the unit. The 3 South UPC is less active and has been re-invigorated with a new chairperson leading it since June 2016. The 3 West UPC is very active and functions on its own with little assistance from nursing leadership. In addition to the normal

functions of a UPC, this council has a Journal Club. 

 

The purpose of this study was to determine whether 3 West had a greater knowledge of evidence-based practice (EBP) due to its more active UPC. The premises of the investigators were that 3 West would have the highest knowledge of EBP, followed by 2 South, and that 3 South would have the lowest knowledge.

 

Literature Review
A review of the literature provided some interesting facts regarding EBP. As healthcare systems worldwide move toward instituting evidence-based practices, its implementation can be challenging. Conducting a baseline assessment to determine nurses’ knowledge of and readiness for EBP presents opportunities to plan strategies before implementation (Thiel & Ghosh, 2008).

 

A study that was conducted in the United States and completed in 2005, “Nurses Readiness for EBP from the Perspective of Informational Literacy,” showed that “RNs in the United States are not ready for EBP” (Pravikoff, Tanner, & Pierce, 2005).

 

Evidence-based practice in nursing is an approach to clinical decision making based on the combination of best evidence available, clinical expertise and patient values and preference to improve outcomes. The importance of EBP in nursing has been established with the demonstrated improvement in patient outcomes (Melnyk, & Finout-Overholt, 2011).

 

The ongoing evolution of nursing as a profession required the development of EBP outcomes and nurses’ ability to access and evaluate professional literature. EBP empowers nurses to form innovative learning partnerships with colleagues to nourish and strengthen critical thinking skills, and to integrate research knowledge in leading best practices. Literature suggests that much of current practice is frequently based on experience, tradition and intuition rather than on evidence. (Malik, G., McKenna, L., & Plummer, V., 2014)

 

In the study by Malik, G. (2014), 95% of the participants used organizational policies and protocols as first-line information and source of knowledge. This result was different from previous published studies, in which nurses had been found to rely mostly on colleagues and experience as a first source for information. 

 

The outcome of the Malik, G. (2014) study indicated that participants rely on personal experience and organizational policies and protocols as formal sources of knowledge.  They showed positive attitudes toward EBP; however, lack of knowledge, poor skills, limited time and lack of resources were perceived as major obstacles to implementing EBP. To embrace EBP, nurses need time away from bedside responsibilities, autonomy over practice, education on finding and accessing evidence and mentorship for successful implementation.

    Research Sample
    This was a descriptive, explorative survey in which medical surgical nurses from three medical surgical units were asked to complete a 29-question 2005 Nursing Evidence-Based Practice Survey and to provide some non-identifying demographic information. 

     

    All nurses on the three units were given the opportunity to participate in the voluntary study. The sample size consisted of 39 registered nurses from 2 South, 31 registered nurses from 3 South and 36 registered nurses from 3 West.

     

    Data Collection Methods
    The 29-question 2005 Nursing Evidence-Based Practice Survey was distributed along with a request for some non-identifying demographic information. The hard-copy surveys were distributed by hand by the staffing clerk in the Nursing Business Office. It was decided that since both Whitney and Stewart are in leadership positions, a neutral party, Dunn, would distribute the surveys to prevent any feeling of coercion.

     

    The packet included a cover letter, an information sheet, the demographic sheet and the survey. Each packet included an envelope addressed to Whitney so they could be returned anonymously. At this point this was a one-time study, although it may be repeated in three to five years. (Evidence NK1EO-4, Survey Packet)

     

    The results of the 29-question survey were categorized into five factors to determine the nurses’ knowledge of and readiness for evidence-based practice. The five factors were Organizational Culture, Unit Culture, Knowledge and Skill of the Nurse, Time and Attitude.

    • Organizational Culture was defined as: Does Beaumont Grosse Pointe support the use of evidence-based practice in nurses’ practices?
    • Unit Culture was defined as: Does the unit nursing leadership and staff support the use of evidence-based practice?
    • Knowledge and Skill of the Nurse was defined as: Do the nurses on the unit know what evidence-based practice is, do they know where to find the evidence-based information and do they use evidence-based information in their nursing practice? 
    • Time was defined as: Do the nurses have time to research and utilize evidence in their daily practice and do they have time to implement their findings? 
    • Attitude measures how the nurse feels about evidence-based practice. 

     

    The survey used a 5-point Likert scale: 1- Strongly Disagree, 2- Disagree, 3-Uncertain, 4-Agree and 5-Strongly Agree.

    Significance of the Results
    For the five factors that the study looked at, 3 West had the highest score of the three units and also had results that were higher than the average of the units. For Unit Culture, 3 West had a score of 4.10 compared to 2 South’s score of 2.43. It is interesting to note that 3 West is the unit that has the most active UPC.

    DISCUSSION

    Summary of Key Findings
    The study demonstrates that the nurses believe that evidence-based practice is an important part of the nursing care that is provided. However, time seems to be a factor in the use of evidence, as the scores for Time were the lowest of all five factors. The Unit Culture (3.80) is more supportive of evidence-based practice than the Organization Culture (3.43). The hypothesis was partially correct, in that 3 West, the unit with the most active UPC, scored the highest in all five factors. The 2 South unit, with the second most active UPC, scored the lowest in all five factors, and the 3 South unit, with the least active UPC, had the second highest scores in all five factors. 

     

    Analysis of the Findings
    In this study, the goal was to evaluate the knowledge regarding evidence-based practice of medical surgical registered nurses in a community hospital. The research question was, Does the unit with a strong UPC have greater knowledge regarding evidence-based practice? When analyzing the results the following must be noted:

    • The return rate was highest on 3 West (75%) and lowest on 3 South (41.94%). The demographics show that gender was basically the same on all three units, with 3 South having the fewest males. Reviewing age, 2 South had the highest percentage of staff members age 40 and over (51.9%), compared to 23.1% on 3 South and 37.1% on 3 West. Education levels varied between units; 3 West had the highest number of BSN and higher-prepared nurses (55.6%), compared to 2 South at 48.1% and 3 South at 30.8%.
    • 3 West, which has the most active UPC, scored the highest in all five factors. The most significant was the Unit Culture. 3 West scored 4.1, compared to 2 South at 3.43 and 3 South at 3.91. This indicated that the activities of the UPC and nursing leadership are promoting an environment that is supportive of evidence-based activities. 
    • 2 South had the lowest score for Unit Culture 3.43. This unit has an active UPC, but based on the score it appears that evidence-based practice is not as supported on the unit. One possible reason for this could be the experience level on 2 South. 2 South has 33.3% of registered nurses with 20-plus years of experience as a registered nurse, compared to 7.7% on 3 South and 7.4% on 3 West. Nurses with 20-plus years of experience may not have studied EBP as part of their educational preparation. The number of nurses in school is also the lowest on 2 South, 7.4%, compared to 23.1% on 3 South and 18.5% on 3 West. These may also contribute to the lower Unit Culture scores.
    • Scores for 3 South, which had the least active UPC, were between those of 2 South and 3 West; however, it should be noted that 3 South’s scores tend to be closer to 3 West than to 2 South.
    • 2 South has the lowest scores for Time, 2.79, compared to 3.15 on 3 West. The questions in the Time factor are: 1. Nurses have enough time on the job to implement evidence-based practice findings and 2. I could find one hour per week on the job for evidence-based practice activities if I made it a priority. The scores make it evident that 2 South feels that the lack of time is a major factor in the unit not being as knowledgeable and supportive of evidence-based practice.

     

    Implications of the Findings
    There are two major implications of the findings. One is that the stronger the UPC, the more active use of evidence-based practice is on that unit. This was determined by a subjective assessment of the researchers. The researchers knew that 3 West had the most active UPC and provided the most evidence-based practice of the three units studied. It was assumed that they would have the highest scores. It is interesting to note that the unit with the lowest scores has the second most active UPC, which was not what the researchers were hypothesizing.

    The researchers believed that 2 South would score the second highest based on the activity of the UPC.

     

    The other interesting finding was that the organization culture for evidence-based practice was lower than the unit culture. This must be studied further, as nurses must feel supported by the organization in their use of EBP.

     

    These results will be shared with the units involved to give them a better understanding of the use of evidence-based practice.

     

    Impact for the Organization
    Based on the results of the study, the following interventions will be instituted by nurse leaders for the inpatient nursing units of Beaumont Grosse Pointe:

    • Review evidence-based practice ideas and suggestions in a future nursing education session
    • Review the results with the 2 South UPC and have Nursing Education develop a plan on how to increase knowledge regarding evidence-based practice
    • Highlight 3 West’s successful UPC model and encourage other units’ UPCs to benchmark with 3 West to implement best practices
    • Encourage and support 3 South’s continued development of its UPC
